# Nepal Energy And Hydropower: Question-Wise Analysis

This document answers the research frame domain by domain. It is written as a working analysis bed, not as a final public-facing essay. Where the evidence is strong, the answers are direct. Where the evidence is partial, the answer states the limit instead of pretending to know more than the sources support.

## Short Answer

Nepal is not short of water or gross hydropower potential. It is short of storage, dry-season regulation, transmission depth, and institutional coordination. The country has already crossed from chronic shortage into seasonal surplus in wet months, but because more than 90% of hydropower capacity is run-of-river, it still imports power in the dry season and cannot fully monetize monsoon abundance year-round. The big strategic story is therefore not "does Nepal have hydropower?" but "can Nepal convert highly seasonal mountain water into dependable national power, industrial growth, and negotiated regional leverage?" [S2][S3][S4][S5][S6][S7]

## A. River System Mapping And Hydrology

### A1. What are Nepal's major river basins, tributaries, and source types?

Nepal's hydrological spine is built around four major Himalayan systems: Mahakali, Karnali, Gandaki/Narayani, and Koshi. The 2024 River Basin Plans also identify six medium basins, including West Rapti, Babai, Bagmati, Kamala, Kankai, and Mechi, plus the Southern River Blocks draining directly from the Siwalik/Chure range. [S2]

The major tributary structure is not evenly distributed:

- Koshi is built around the Arun, Tamor, Sunkoshi system and its large snow- and glacier-influenced tributaries.
- Gandaki is built around Kali Gandaki, Marsyangdi, Budhi Gandaki, Trishuli, Seti, Madi, and East Rapti.
- Karnali is built around Bheri, West Seti, Karnali main stem, Humla Karnali, Mugu Karnali, and Tila.
- Mahakali is a transboundary western basin with far smaller current development than the three eastern systems. [S1][S2][S3]

The most useful classification is not a crude glacier-fed versus rain-fed split. Nepal's major Himalayan systems are mixed basins: snow and glacier melt matter, but monsoon rainfall still dominates the annual hydrograph in most cases. Medium hill rivers are mostly rain-fed with spring and groundwater support. Siwalik rivers are flashy monsoon systems with weak dry-season dependability. [S1][S10][S11]

### A2. What do we know about average flow, seasonal flow, and dry-season stability?

The 2024 River Basin Plans give the clearest recent basin-scale discharge numbers at or near the Nepal-India border:

- Koshi: about 1,827 m3/s, equivalent to 57.6 billion cubic meters annually; around 67-78% of runoff arrives in the monsoon. [S2]
- Gandaki: about 1,952 m3/s, equivalent to 61.6 billion cubic meters annually; about 74% of runoff arrives in the monsoon. [S2]
- Karnali: about 1,256 m3/s, equivalent to 39.6 billion cubic meters annually; about 72% of runoff arrives in the monsoon. [S2]
- Mahakali: the basin plan emphasizes that about 73% of surface runoff arrives in the four monsoon months; the public text visible from the report is more precise on seasonality than on annual discharge. [S2]

The older National Water Plan estimated Nepal's total average annual runoff at roughly 225 BCM, but that should be treated as a historical planning baseline rather than a current measured truth because it was built on older records. [S1]

The central hydrological fact is seasonality. Multiple sources converge on roughly 70-80% of annual river flow arriving during the monsoon, with some basins experiencing order-of-magnitude reductions from monsoon peak to dry-season low flow. This is why annual average discharge, by itself, is not an energy answer. [S1][S2][S11]

### A3. What needs to be mapped to make this analyzable rather than decorative?

The minimum useful mapping stack is:

- major basins and main tributaries
- average annual discharge by basin
- seasonal runoff share by basin
- major operating hydropower plants
- storage and peaking projects
- transmission corridors and substations
- protected areas and geohazard exposure

Without all seven layers, the map is misleading. River volume alone overstates potential; project location alone hides timing; hydropower potential alone ignores delivery; basin maps without protected-area or sediment context understate development risk. [S2][S3][S4][S6]

### A4. Which rivers are hydrologically promising, underused, or misleading?

High-confidence interpretation:

- Karnali is underused relative to resource quality and strategic importance. It has very large potential, steep topography, and comparatively lighter development than Gandaki or the eastern corridors, but it is constrained by remoteness, financing, and grid evacuation. [S2][S3][S4]
- Gandaki is the most mature "buildable" basin because it combines strong head, historical project concentration, and comparatively better connectivity. [S2][S3]
- Koshi has the largest gross potential in the WECS 2019 assessment, but it is also a high-hazard, high-sediment basin and cannot be treated as easy capacity. [S2][S3][S12]
- Medium and Siwalik rivers can look attractive on annual-flow maps and then disappoint on dry-season firmness and dispatch value. [S1][S11]

Bottom line: Nepal is not hydrologically underendowed. The problem is that the best-looking rivers on paper are often the hardest rivers to regulate, finance, and evacuate power from.

## B. Hydropower Potential And Plant Distribution

### B1. What does the existing hydropower footprint look like?

By FY 2024/25, NEA reports total installed generation capacity of 3,591.262 MW, of which 3,389.912 MW is hydropower, 141.940 MW solar, 53.410 MW thermal, and 6 MW bagasse. Hydro therefore accounts for about 94.4% of installed generation capacity. [S4]

The 2024/25 annual report and World Bank memo together show a system dominated by independent power producers and by non-storage hydro:

- hydropower capacity reached roughly 2,990 MW by end-2024 in the World Bank economic memo [S6]
- more than 70% of the hydropower capacity added since 2018 came from IPPs [S6]
- IPPs accounted for 64% of total installed hydropower capacity by 2024 [S6]
- more than 90% of hydropower capacity is run-of-river or peaking run-of-river [S6]

The public Naxa/DoED-linked portal shows 572 projects in the broader license pipeline: 311 survey, 180 generation, and 81 operation licenses. That portal is useful for geography and pipeline shape, but it is not a complete ledger of actual operating national capacity. [S4][S15]

### B2. Are projects located where the resource is best, or where projects were easiest to build?

The evidence points to both, but with a strong bias toward buildability.

Most of Nepal's existing operating plants cluster on rivers where head is high, access was achievable, and financing could be assembled without the full complexity of reservoir development. That is why the fleet is so heavily weighted toward ROR and PROR. In other words, Nepal has often built where energy could be monetized fastest, not where basin regulation would have the highest long-run strategic value. [S3][S4][S6]

The best examples are Marsyangdi, Kali Gandaki, Trishuli, Bhotekoshi, and Tamakoshi corridors: steep valleys, established hydropower history, and stronger institutional familiarity. Karnali's lower development relative to resource quality is strong evidence that resource endowment alone does not determine buildout. [S2][S3][S4]

### B3. How much hydropower potential is real?

The safest answer is to separate three numbers:

- older slogan number: 83,500 MW gross potential [S3]
- newer WECS screening estimate: 72,544 MW gross potential [S3]
- newer WECS screening estimate: 32,680 MW techno-economic potential [S3]

These numbers are not substitutes for one another. Gross potential is theoretical. Techno-economic potential is already screened for development realism, but it is still not the same thing as financially closed, socially accepted, geologically confirmed, transmission-connected capacity. [S3]

The gross basin distribution in WECS 2019 is concentrated in the big three basins:

- Koshi: 27,805 MW
- Karnali: 20,385 MW
- Gandaki: 19,803 MW
- all remaining basins combined: about 4,551 MW [S3]

That means roughly 94% of Nepal's gross hydropower potential is concentrated in the three major Himalayan systems. The real strategic choice is therefore not "which small rivers should Nepal build everywhere?" but "how should Nepal sequence the big-basin opportunity under seasonal, environmental, grid, and financing constraints?" [S2][S3]

### B4. What project mix best fits Nepal?

The evidence supports a mixed answer:

- ROR and PROR will remain useful because they are faster, cheaper, and more bankable in mountain terrain.
- storage and multipurpose projects are indispensable if Nepal wants dry-season firmness, irrigation, flood control, and export credibility.
- pumped storage, BESS, and stronger dispatch systems become more attractive as wet-season surplus grows.
- the least rational future is adding only more ROR while leaving storage and grid lagging. [S4][S6][S7]

## C. Dams, Storage, And Water Regulation

### C1. What storage exists today?

Operational storage is minimal. Kulekhani is still the country's decisive storage outlier.

- Kulekhani I is Nepal's only reservoir-type hydropower station in the strict NEA description, with 60 MW capacity. [S4]
- Kulekhani II adds 32 MW and Kulekhani III adds 14 MW as a storage cascade, bringing the operational storage cascade to 106 MW. [S4]
- the combined generation of Kulekhani I, II, and III in FY 2024/25 was 279.8 GWh. [S4]

At national scale, that is extremely small relative to the river system Nepal sits on.

The World Bank water diagnostic says Nepal's storage volume requirement to satisfy annual seasonal water demand is 29.86 cubic kilometers and that it effectively has none of this required seasonal storage at national scale. It also cites JICA estimates that Nepal will need at least 1,993 MW of storage hydropower by 2032 in a business-as-usual demand case and 3,154 MW in a high-demand case. [S7]

### C2. How is Nepal generating hydro without many large dams?

By leaning on ROR and PROR designs.

- ROR uses river water as it comes, with little or no seasonal carryover.
- PROR stores only limited daily pondage to shift generation into peak hours.
- reservoir plants can store wet-season water into the dry season. [S3][S4]

Nepal historically favored ROR/PROR because they are cheaper, quicker to finance, involve less large-scale resettlement, and fit the private IPP-led model better than huge multipurpose reservoirs. But that solved the capacity-addition problem without solving the seasonal-value problem. [S4][S6][S7]

### C3. How much instability is really a storage problem?

A large share of it.

WECS states that when Nepal relies mainly on ROR and PROR, dry-season hydropower production falls to roughly one-third of installed capacity. The World Bank water diagnostic says the same thing in different language: because most of the fleet is run-of-river, winter brings severe domestic electricity shortages. [S3][S7]

That is why Nepal can simultaneously:

- add thousands of megawatts of hydropower capacity
- export electricity during the monsoon
- and still import electricity in the dry season [S4][S6]

This is best understood as a storage-and-timing failure more than a raw generation failure.

### C4. Which storage projects matter most?

The most strategically important current and candidate projects are:

- Dudhkoshi Storage Hydroelectric Project: 670 MW, including a 600 MW main powerhouse and 70 MW dam-toe powerhouse, with an estimated 1,581 million cubic meter reservoir and 3,377 GWh annual generation. [S4]
- Tanahu Hydropower Project: 140 MW storage project, around 67% complete in the FY 2024/25 NEA report. [S4]
- Uttar Ganga storage project: 828 MW in the project pipeline. [S4][S15]
- Budhi Gandaki: 1,200 MW proposed storage project repeatedly identified as system-shaping but stalled. [S2][S6]
- Pancheshwar on the Mahakali: 4,800-5,040 MW multipurpose project depending on source framing, but still unrealized. [S2][S9]

These are not just generation projects. They are also water-regulation projects. That is what makes them strategically different from more ROR additions.

## D. Seasonal Variability And Climate Stress

### D1. How extreme is seasonality?

Very.

Recent official basin plans put roughly 72-78% of annual runoff into the monsoon season for the major basins. Flood studies and basin case studies align with this. Even in mixed snow- and glacier-fed basins, monsoon rainfall dominates the timing of usable flow. [S2][S10][S11]

This means the most important hydrological number for energy planning is not annual discharge. It is the shape of the annual hydrograph.

### D2. What does that do to electricity generation?

It creates the core contradiction in Nepal's electricity system:

- generation peaks in the rainy season, especially June-November
- demand peaks in the drier winter half of the year, roughly December-May
- exports become possible in wet months
- imports remain necessary in dry months [S4][S6][S7]

The trade data makes the point clearly:

- in 2018 Nepal imported about 2,582 GWh and exported only about 3 GWh [S6]
- in 2023 Nepal imported about 1,895 GWh and exported about 1,946 GWh, becoming a net exporter for the first time [S6]
- NEA daily reports still show sharp wet/dry swings, with zero-import wet-season days and zero-export dry-season days. [S4]

### D3. Where is the best head-versus-flow tradeoff?

There is no universal answer, but the broad pattern is:

- upper reaches give steeper head and high energy density
- lower reaches give more flow and easier regulation
- mid-catchment corridors often offer the most buildable tradeoff

Upstream sites are more exposed to remoteness, glacial hazards, sediment pulses, landslides, and high civil-works cost. Downstream sites are better for multipurpose regulation and larger reservoirs but often face lower gradient, higher social footprint, and more complex transboundary politics. Nepal's future buildout therefore has to be basin-specific rather than slogan-based. [S2][S3][S12]

### D4. How is climate change changing the problem?

The climate effect is not a simple fall in water. It is a rise in variability, hazard, and sediment stress.

Key patterns from the literature:

- glacier area across the Hindu Kush Himalaya fell by roughly 12% between 1990 and 2020, with faster recent ice loss. [S13]
- Nepal-specific glacier inventories show substantial long-run glacier retreat. [S14]
- Koshi and Sunkoshi studies show more variable seasonal water outcomes across sub-basins, not a uniform basin-wide trend. [S11][S12]
- ICIMOD and other sources point to growing GLOF, landslide, erosion, and sediment risks. [S10][S13][S14]

For energy planning, climate change increases the premium on storage, sediment management, dam safety, and flexible system operation.

## E. Hydropower Technology And Engineering Architecture

### E1. What engineering architectures dominate Nepal today?

Nepal's dominant architecture is diversion-based mountain hydropower:

- ROR plants using diversion weirs, tunnels, penstocks, and steep head
- PROR plants using limited pondage for daily peaking
- one small storage cascade centered on Kulekhani

The NEA annual report shows the large-generation fleet still organized around four PROR plants, two storage-cascade plants, and one ROR plant in its main large-plant group. That is a concentrated operating archetype, not a diversified one. [S4]

### E2. What reduces actual performance?

The biggest performance reducers are:

- seasonality
- sediment and debris load
- floods and landslides
- aging equipment and slow rehabilitation
- weak grid evacuation

The NEA report and World Bank memo both note siltation, debris, and flood damage. The World Bank memo says September 2024 floods and landslides damaged 11 operational hydropower projects with more than 600 MW combined capacity. [S4][S6]

### E3. How does Nepal compare with better mountain hydro systems?

Nepal does not mainly lag on turbine theory. It lags on system architecture.

The countries and systems Nepal should learn from are not just those that build hydro, but those that combine:

- cascaded reservoir management
- pumped storage
- sediment handling and desanding
- strong transmission evacuation
- digital dispatch and balancing
- basin-level project sequencing

Nepal's gap is therefore more in flexibility, storage, and coordination than in the basic ability to construct hydro plants.

### E4. What would a modern hydropower strategy look like?

A modern strategy would combine:

- selective storage and multipurpose reservoirs
- pumped storage where system economics justify it
- BESS for short-duration balancing and wet-season arbitrage support
- better sediment management and catchment monitoring
- rehabilitation and automation of older plants
- basin-level sequencing instead of project-by-project opportunism [S4][S6][S7]

## F. Grid Readiness And Electricity System Reliability

### F1. What does the grid look like physically and institutionally?

NEA remains the central system operator and single buyer for most grid power.

By FY 2079/80, the system had:

- 5,742 circuit-km of 66 kV and above transmission lines
- 8,867 MVA of substation capacity [S5]

By 2024, system losses were still about 12.7%. [S6]

Institutionally, Nepal has privatized much of generation growth but not grid governance. That means the system can add plants faster than it can add flexibility.

### F2. How reliable is the grid?

Far better than during the load-shedding era, but still weaker than headline electrification figures suggest.

- scheduled load shedding was eliminated in 2018 [S6]
- 76% of firms still reported regular power outages in 2022 [S6]
- more than 13% of firms reported sales losses above 10% from power cuts in 2022 [S6]

This means Nepal has largely fixed the old scarcity optics while still carrying a substantial reliability penalty for business.

### F3. Is the current grid prepared for more hydro?

Not enough.

The current limitations are not only generation shortages. They are:

- weak east-west transfer depth
- insufficient evacuation from remote hydro corridors
- substation and transformer bottlenecks
- limited balancing capability
- limited cross-border transfer capacity, currently around 1,000 MW [S4][S6]

This is why new generation can still become stranded generation.

### F4. Is Nepal over-focused on generation?

Yes.

The system evidence strongly suggests that Nepal has treated megawatts as the heroic metric and left too much value trapped in transmission, storage, market design, and demand creation. A serious exporter grid would need more 220/400 kV evacuation, more cross-border links, stronger system operation, and more flexible demand inside Nepal. [S4][S6]

## G. Energy Economics, Trade, Contracts, And Demand Distribution

### G1. What is the current import-export pattern?

Nepal's position has changed fast:

- 2018: imported about 2,582 GWh, exported about 3 GWh [S6]
- 2023: imported about 1,895 GWh, exported about 1,946 GWh [S6]
- FY 2024/25: of 15,641 GWh available energy, 11% was still imported from India, with 89% supplied by domestic NEA/subsidiary and IPP generation. [S4]

The right interpretation is not "Nepal is now energy secure." It is "Nepal has entered a seasonal export phase but not yet a fully balanced year-round phase."

### G2. What market and contract constraints matter?

The key constraints are:

- a single-buyer NEA-centered market structure
- long-term take-or-pay PPAs
- foreign-exchange exposure in project finance
- dependence on Indian market access and Indian regulatory rules for cross-border trade
- limited transmission capacity for larger export volumes [S4][S6]

So the export question is no longer whether Nepal can sell power. It can. The real question is whether Nepal can sell at scale, on durable terms, and without remaining trapped by seasonal mismatch.

### G3. Is electricity more valuable as an export or as domestic industrial input?

The evidence supports a mixed answer with a clear long-run tilt toward domestic value creation.

Wet-season exports are economically useful because they monetize surplus power and strengthen NEA cash flow. But Nepal's per-capita electricity use is still only around 370-400 kWh, which is very low. Industry still relies heavily on imported fossil fuels and captive generation. That means the long-run developmental value of reliable domestic electricity is likely higher than the value of exporting every available surplus unit. [S5][S6]

### G4. Where is electricity actually going?

Nepal's broader final energy system is still not electricity-centered.

For 2022 final energy consumption, WECS reports:

- residential: 323,463 TJ, or 60.75%
- industry: 111,306 TJ, or 20.91%
- transport: 55,553 TJ, or 10.43%
- commercial: 26,835 TJ, or 5.04%
- agriculture: 5,040 TJ
- construction/mining: 10,222 TJ [S5]

Grid electricity was only 4.96% of final energy in the 2022 balance. Biomass still dominated. That means Nepal's electricity story is more advanced than its total energy transition story. [S5]

### G5. What economic transformation could reliable power unlock?

Reliable power could:

- reduce captive diesel generation
- raise manufacturing competitiveness
- electrify cooking and transport
- reduce fossil import dependence
- increase service-sector productivity
- support digitally delivered exports

Nepal's hydropower question therefore overlaps with industrial policy. The opportunity is not merely export revenue. It is structural transformation if electricity becomes abundant, reliable, and cheap enough to change how firms and households use energy. [S5][S6]

## H. Geopolitics Of Nepal's Water

### H1. How important are Nepal's rivers downstream?

The old but still widely cited National Water Plan estimate is that Nepal-origin rivers contribute about 40% of the Ganges' mean annual flow and about 70% of its dry-season flow. The exact percentages should be treated cautiously because the underlying hydrology is historical, but the strategic direction is clear: Nepal matters downstream, especially in the dry season. [S1]

Koshi is especially significant. ICIMOD describes it as supporting more than 40 million people and notes that it contributes disproportionately high sediment loads to the Ganges system. [S9][S10]

### H2. Does Nepal have real upstream leverage?

Yes, but it is narrower than political rhetoric implies.

Nepal has real leverage in:

- siting and timing of transboundary works
- flood control and sediment management cooperation
- regulation and seasonal flow through storage projects
- cross-border electricity trade

Nepal does not have unlimited unilateral leverage because:

- major treaties are project-specific
- India remains the main downstream market and negotiating counterweight
- Nepal lacks large-scale storage and basin-control infrastructure of its own [S2][S9]

### H3. How should Nepal be compared regionally?

Carefully.

Nepal is strategically important as an upstream Himalayan contributor to the Ganges system, but it is not the only such contributor. Tibet/China, Indian catchments, and other Himalayan systems all matter. The correct claim is that Nepal is regionally important and underleveraged, not that it singularly controls South Asian water security. [S2][S8][S9]

### H4. How important are these rivers to downstream agriculture and flood risk?

Very important in some corridors, especially through irrigation and flood regimes affecting the Gangetic plains.

Mahakali already supports irrigation via the Mahakali irrigation system. Koshi matters heavily for flood and sediment risk in Bihar and downstream plains. Gandaki and Karnali matter more through broader basin hydrology, water availability, and transboundary project potential than through a single dramatic narrative. [S2][S9][S10]

### H5. How does climate change change the geopolitics?

Climate change raises the value of storage, coordination, and flood management.

If variability, sediment, GLOF exposure, and extreme rainfall rise, then Nepal's role as an upstream regulator becomes more important. That does not automatically create bargaining power. But it does make storage, basin-level data, and coordinated infrastructure more geopolitically valuable than they were under more stable hydrological conditions. [S10][S13][S14]

## Cross-Cutting Synthesis

### Is Nepal's main problem lack of hydropower potential?

No. The best evidence says the main problem is systems conversion: turning abundant but seasonal water into firm, valuable power. [S2][S3][S6][S7]

### What is the bottleneck hierarchy?

Best current ranking:

1. storage and dry-season regulation
2. transmission and evacuation capacity
3. market design and cross-border access
4. domestic electricity demand deepening, especially industry
5. basin-level planning, data quality, and climate resilience

### Is Nepal wasting water, elevation, or timing?

Mostly timing. Secondarily, delivery. Nepal has plenty of head and plenty of water, but too little ability to hold monsoon water and move its value through the grid at the right time. [S2][S4][S6][S7]

### If Nepal solved only one bottleneck, what would unlock the most?

The highest-leverage move is not simply adding more hydro plants. It is building storage-plus-grid depth so that wet-season abundance becomes dry-season power and commercially usable energy. [S4][S6][S7]

### Is Nepal closer to energy-rich than people realize?

Yes in gross resource terms, no in firm-power terms.

Nepal is resource-rich, but not yet dispatch-rich.

## Current Thesis Direction

The strongest evidence-supported thesis at this stage is:

Nepal's energy constraint is not the absence of hydropower resource. It is the absence of enough storage, transmission, and system design to convert highly seasonal mountain water into dependable electricity, industrial value, and strategic leverage.

## What Still Needs Better Data

- DHM long time-series discharge data by tributary
- monthly import-export time series in one clean public dataset
- plant-level curtailment and outage data
- more rigorous basin-by-basin sediment and geology overlays
- stronger public data on end-use electricity demand by sector and time of day
