# Nepal Grid, Electricity Trade, Demand, and Energy Economics

## Bottom Line
Nepal is no longer a simple "power shortage" case. The country has moved into a more complex system where the main questions are seasonality, transmission, market access, and whether electricity can be turned into domestic industrial growth rather than only wet-season export revenue.

The core pattern is this: Nepal now has enough generation to cover much of its annual electricity need in wet months, but its run-of-river hydro system still creates a dry-season deficit, and the grid plus cross-border trade architecture determine whether that surplus can be monetized or wasted.

## Grid Structure
NEA remains the dominant system operator: it owns almost all transmission and distribution assets and is the single buyer for most grid power under long-term take-or-pay PPAs. The sector is therefore vertically concentrated even as generation has become more private-sector led.

The current grid picture is better than a decade ago, but it is not yet a fully flexible national market. WB and WECS sources point to:
- 66 kV and above transmission lines at 5,742 circuit-km in FY 2079/80.
- Substation capacity at 8,867 MVA.
- NEA system losses still around 12.7% in 2024.
- Cross-border transfer capacity still capped around 1,000 MW, with the 400 kV Muzaffarpur-Dhalkebar corridor a key bottleneck.

## Reliability and Seasonal Balance
Nepal has largely escaped the old load-shedding era. The World Bank notes that scheduled load shedding ended in 2018, after outages had lasted up to 14 hours per day between 2007 and 2017 and cost an estimated 7 percent of GDP annually.

That said, reliability is not the same as access. The network still struggles with outages, weak transformers, and constrained evacuation from remote hydro sites to load centers. The World Bank Country Economic Memorandum says 76 percent of firms still experienced regular power cuts in 2022, and more than 13 percent reported annual sales losses above 10 percent because of outages.

Seasonality is the structural issue. Because run-of-river hydropower dominates the fleet, generation peaks in the rainy season, roughly June-November, while demand peaks in the dry season, December-May. The result is a system that can look surplus-rich on an annual basis while still importing power in the dry season.

NEA daily operational reports make this visible:
- 2023-08-22: 8,651 MWh exported, 0 MWh imported.
- 2024-03-07: 14,152 MWh imported, 0 MWh exported.

That is the real Nepal pattern: wet-season surplus, dry-season deficit.

## Trade Pattern
Nepal’s trade position has changed quickly:
- In 2018, Nepal imported about 2,582 GWh, roughly 37 percent of total electricity consumption, while exporting only about 3 GWh.
- In 2023, it imported about 1,895 GWh, about 16 percent of consumption, while exporting 1,946 GWh, becoming a net electricity exporter for the first time.
- In FY 2022/23, total electricity imported from India reached 1,833 GWh, up 18.79 percent from the prior year.
- By 2024, electricity exports to India had risen to about 0.3 percent of GDP.
- WECS also reports about 452 MW of export on a day-ahead basis through the Indian Energy Exchange in FY 2079/80.

This matters because it means Nepal is not locked into permanent importer status. The country can export meaningful energy when monsoon flows are strong and the market is open. The problem is that export volumes, export prices, and export timing are all constrained by seasonality and grid access.

## Pricing and Contract Constraints
The trade framework is still politically and institutionally shaped, not purely market-shaped.

Important constraints:
- The 2014 Nepal-India Power Trade Agreement covers cross-border transmission, interconnection, and grid connectivity.
- The agreement framework explicitly involves energy accounting, settlement procedures, and barriers such as tariff, levies, fees, and taxes.
- A Nepal-India Power Exchange Committee note from 2011 says Nepal asked for a tariff review after the 10th PEC meeting because tariffs were rising 5.5 percent per year.
- The World Bank notes that NEA sells most power to a single buyer on take-or-pay PPAs, many with foreign currency exposure, which creates financial risk for the utility.
- A 2025 MoEWRI/JICA-linked plan says IEX green-market power can trade at roughly 10 percent above the regular market, but Nepal would need stronger transmission links and longer-term PPAs with Indian and Bangladeshi buyers to scale that opportunity.

So the trade issue is not "can Nepal export?" It already does. The issue is whether Nepal can export on terms that are liquid, bankable, and large enough to matter.

## Demand and Consumption
Electricity demand is rising fast, but the structure of demand still leaves room for large gains from electrification and industrialization.

The World Bank’s 2025 documents say:
- Domestic electricity generation rose from about 3,600 GWh in 2015 to more than 12,000 GWh in 2024.
- Per-capita electricity consumption reached about 400 kWh in 2024, still among the lowest in the world.
- Electricity demand has grown at about 10 percent annually over the past decade.

But Nepal’s broader energy system is still dominated by biomass and imported fossil fuels. In the WECS 2024 energy balance:
- Residential final consumption was 323,463 TJ, or 60.75 percent.
- Industry consumed 111,306 TJ, or 20.91 percent.
- Transport consumed 55,553 TJ, or 10.43 percent.
- Commercial consumed 26,835 TJ, or 5.04 percent.
- Agriculture consumed 5,040 TJ, and construction/mining 10,222 TJ.

Within electricity-specific demand, the story is still underdeveloped:
- In 2022, industry consumed about 12,800 TJ of electricity, only 9 percent of final industry energy use.
- Services consumed about 4,100 TJ of electricity, only 13 percent of final services energy use.
- Residential electricity use has grown, but electricity was still only about 5 percent of household energy mix in 2022.

This is the key economic insight: Nepal’s domestic economy is not yet electricity-intensive enough. That is a constraint, but also an opportunity.

## Domestic Industry vs Export
The strongest case for domestic use is not sentimental nationalism. It is economics.

The World Bank reports that unreliable power remains a major obstacle to firms, with 36 percent using captive generators in 2022. If Nepal can convert supply into reliable industrial power, the value created domestically may exceed the value of selling marginal surplus power across the border.

Exports still matter, but mostly as a way to monetize surplus wet-season generation and improve NEA cash flow. The higher-value long-run path is to use reliable electricity to:
- raise manufacturing output,
- reduce captive generation,
- support services and digital activity,
- electrify cooking and transport,
- and deepen domestic load so hydropower projects are financed against a larger, steadier market.

## Transmission Constraints and Stranded Generation
The binding constraint is increasingly delivery, not only generation.

Evidence points to:
- weak and constrained transmission and distribution networks,
- outdated and undercapacity transformers,
- limited capacity to evacuate power from remote hydropower plants,
- and insufficient high-voltage cross-border interconnection.

The World Bank CEM is explicit: Nepal’s limited cross-border transmission capacity, plus a lack of 400 kV or higher lines, constrains large-scale exports. The current capacity limit is about 1,000 MW.

The strategic implication is that some hydropower potential is effectively stranded twice:
- stranded geographically, because it is far from load centers,
- and stranded commercially, because export routes and market access are incomplete.

## Read on the System
If the country solves only one bottleneck, it should probably be transmission-plus-market design rather than just building more plants. Generation growth without grid depth only creates congestion, dry-season imports, and occasional wet-season oversupply.

The more useful national objective is not "more hydropower" in the abstract. It is:
- more dispatchable hydropower,
- more transmission capacity,
- better trade terms,
- deeper domestic industrial demand,
- and enough storage or balancing to turn monsoon abundance into year-round value.
