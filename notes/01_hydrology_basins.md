# Nepal Hydrology and River-Basin Fundamentals

This memo is the base layer for the larger Nepal energy/hydropower project. It focuses on river systems, discharge, source type, seasonality, and the hydrological constraints that matter for power planning.

## 1. Basin structure

Nepal has more than 6,000 rivers and a dense north-south drainage network. The four major Himalayan river systems are the Mahakali, Karnali, Narayani (Gandaki), and Saptakoshi. The medium rivers are mainly Kankai, Kamala, Bagmati, West Rapti, and Babai. The southern Siwalik rivers are numerous, flashy, and often dry in the non-monsoon season. The country’s total average annual runoff is estimated at about 225 BCM, but that estimate is based on station data available up to 1995, so it should be treated as a historical planning baseline rather than a current truth.

## 2. Source type and hydrological meaning

The cleanest practical classification is not “glacier-fed vs rain-fed” as a binary, but a gradient:

- The major Himalayan systems are mixed basins, with snow and glacier melt in the upper reaches and monsoon rainfall dominating much of the annual hydrograph.
- The medium hill rivers are primarily rain-fed, but springs and groundwater keep them alive through the dry season.
- The Siwalik rivers are overwhelmingly monsoon systems: useful for flash flooding, local irrigation, and debris transport, but weak for dependable year-round power generation.

Even in a glaciated basin, rainfall can dominate annual discharge. A recent Tamakoshi basin study found that rain runoff contributed about 62% of total streamflow, baseflow about 20%, snowmelt about 5%, and glacier melt about 13%. That is a useful warning against assuming that “high mountain” automatically means “glacier water dominates the river.”

## 3. Seasonal regime

Seasonality is the central hydrological fact in Nepal.

- A DHM-based flood paper states that roughly 70-80% of total river flow occurs during the monsoon, while snowmelt helps sustain pre-monsoon and post-monsoon streamflow.
- The same paper shows that the top 1% of extreme flows in the Karnali, Narayani, and Koshi outlets are heavily concentrated in the monsoon, with 90-99% of those events occurring from mid-June to early September.
- August is the key flood month in the major basins, and July-August account for more than 81% of the top five flow events at the major outlet stations.

For the Tamakoshi case study:

- 81% of precipitation occurred in the monsoon season.
- 74% of total discharge occurred in the monsoon season.
- Streamflow was highest in August and lowest in February.
- Water storage was positive from April to August and negative from September to March.

That seasonal profile matters more than annual average runoff for hydropower planning, because a river with a large annual volume can still be a bad power source if the water arrives in a narrow monsoon window and disappears in the dry season.

## 4. Hydrological underuse and physical constraints

The National Water Plan states that Nepal has about 225 BCM of annual water availability, but only about 15 BCM had been utilized for economic and social purposes at the time of the plan. The plan also says the larger and perennial Himalayan rivers were largely left untapped except for a few run-of-river schemes, and that future development would need storage to capture rainy-season water for dry-season use.

The key hydrological constraints for hydropower are:

- Flow timing, not just volume.
- Sediment and debris load, especially from steep mountain catchments.
- Remoteness and difficult access.
- Limited continuous discharge records at many tributaries.
- Basin-specific variability that makes national averages misleading.

This is why the common slogan “Nepal has huge hydropower potential” is incomplete. A basin can be water-rich and still poor for dependable electricity if the water is too seasonal, too sediment-laden, or too far from transmission.

## 5. Climate stress and visible trends

Recent literature already shows the hydrology is changing, and not uniformly.

- In the Koshi basin, a 2023 modeling study found the upper basin warming faster than the lower basin, pre-monsoon warming more strongly than other seasons, and annual water availability not necessarily declining, but becoming more variable across seasons and sub-basins. The study also projected more flood days in some scenarios and fewer low-flow days in others, depending on sub-basin.
- In the Sunkoshi basin, another 2023 study projected upper-basin rainfall and river flow increases but lower-basin declines, which is a strong reminder that “the basin” is not hydrologically uniform.
- A 2023 study of Nepal’s major and medium rivers found mean annual discharge in the post-impact period fell by 5.86% in major rivers and 7.94% in medium rivers, with overall hydrologic alteration ranging from 28.7% to 46.7%.
- ICIMOD notes that glacier thinning and retreat are creating and enlarging glacial lakes in Nepal, which increases GLOF risk.
- ICIMOD’s permafrost work warns that thawing frozen ground can increase landslides and sediment loads, fill hydropower reservoirs, and worsen downstream flooding.

The practical takeaway is that climate change is not just a temperature story. It is a timing story, a sediment story, and a hazard story.

## 6. Data gaps and access limits

This is where the current hydrology layer is still thin:

- The basin-level discharge values in the National Water Plan are old and based on data available up to 1995.
- DHM’s citizen charter shows that hydrological data requests require an application and data request form, and data verification is charged. That means long discharge series are not broadly open by default.
- A recent streamflow paper on major and medium rivers explicitly says the authors do not have permission to share the data.
- Some river basins are well studied, but many tributaries still depend on model reconstruction or station-specific studies rather than open gauge archives.

That means the next research layer should not try to build a false sense of precision. The correct move is to treat the major-basin numbers as the stable backbone and then add tributary-level station data only where the source quality is strong.

## 7. Working interpretation for the later hydropower analysis

The hydrology layer points to a simple but important conclusion: Nepal’s energy opportunity is constrained less by total water volume than by seasonality, storage, sediment, and data quality.

The high-value river systems are the ones that combine:

- Enough head.
- Reliable dry-season flow or storage potential.
- Manageable sediment.
- Transmission access.
- Basin-level planning rather than isolated project logic.

That is the frame to carry into the hydropower, grid, and geopolitics work.
