# Nepal Hydropower Projects, Potential, Dams, and Storage

## Snapshot

The operating system is still overwhelmingly hydro-dominant and still mostly run-of-river or peaking run-of-river. The latest NEA annual report says Nepal's total installed generation capacity reached **3,591.262 MW** in FY 2024/25, of which **3,389.912 MW** is hydro, **141.940 MW** solar, **53.410 MW** thermal, and **6.000 MW** bagasse. Hydro is therefore about **94.4%** of installed capacity.

The public hydropower-portal dataset exposes **572 projects**: **311 survey**, **180 generation**, and **81 operation** licenses. That is a pipeline inventory, not an operating-capacity ledger, but it is useful for seeing where the next wave of projects is concentrated.

## Existing Footprint

The NEA report is explicit that the large operating fleet is still narrow in type. Its large-generation O&M group covers **seven hydropower plants totaling 465 MW**; **four are PROR**, **two are storage-cascade**, and **one is ROR**. The same report says **Kulekhani I, II, and III are the only seasonal storage-based plants** in that fleet.

## Storage and Regulation

Kulekhani remains the critical storage outlier. NEA says it is Nepal's **only reservoir-type hydropower station**. In FY 2024/25, the reservoir's maximum and minimum water levels were **1531.5 MASL** and **1486.3 MASL**, and the combined generation of KL1, KL2, and KL3 was **279.8 GWh**.

The WECS study also states the key structural constraint plainly: when Nepal relies almost entirely on ROR/PROR, dry-season hydropower output falls to **about one-third of installed capacity**. The report ties that directly to the lack of reservoir plants.

## Potential, And What The Numbers Mean

WECS (2019) estimates Nepal's **gross hydropower potential** at **72,544 MW** and **techno-economic potential** at **32,680 MW**. It also cites older work that put gross potential at **83,500 MW** and economically viable potential at about **45,610 MW**. These numbers are not interchangeable.

The WECS study is a screening exercise, not a bankable project list. Its own conclusion says the identified ROR and reservoir projects are based mainly on topography, and that the sites still need detailed geological and feasibility work.

## Why Storage Has Lagged

The sources point to a simple explanation set: steep terrain, geologic risk, landslide and sediment load, high capital intensity, long gestation, and a historical bias toward quicker ROR/PROR builds. That is an inference from the project mix and the project-screening language in WECS, not a single quoted sentence.

The engineering and sediment evidence is concrete. NEA reports damage and debris/silt issues at Upper Trishuli 3A. WECS field observations for potential reservoir sites explicitly flag sediment yield risk, including a note that the Saptakoshi reservoir site has high sediment yield capacity. Kulekhani also had a major flood/debris event in FY 2024/25.

## Read This As The Core Takeaway

Nepal is not short of hydropower resource. It is short of storage, regulated dispatch, and bankable project conversion from theoretical basin potential into operating assets.
