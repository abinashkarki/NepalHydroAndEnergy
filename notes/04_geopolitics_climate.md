# Nepal Water Geopolitics, Downstream Basin Significance, and Climate Risk

## Bottom Line

Nepal is not strategically important only because it has mountains and rivers. It sits at the headwaters of three major Ganges tributary systems, and the evidence base from WECS and ICIMOD shows that its rivers matter downstream in both water volume and sediment. The catch is that Nepal's leverage is real but narrow: it is strongest in basin-level hydrology and flood control, not in unilateral control over downstream water politics.

The main pattern is simple. Nepal has large annual water availability, but little storage and limited basin-scale regulation. That makes the country hydrologically rich but operationally fragile. Climate change then compounds the problem by making the system more variable, more sediment-heavy, and more hazard-prone rather than simply "wetter" or "drier."

## 1. Nepal's Place In The Basin System

The WECS National Water Plan identifies four major Himalayan river systems in Nepal: Koshi, Narayani/Gandaki, Karnali, and Mahakali. It also says rivers flowing from Nepal contribute about 40% of the Ganges' mean annual flow and about 70% of its dry-season flow, using data available up to 1995 [S1]. That is the single most important downstream significance point in the entire topic.

The basin numbers matter:

- Koshi is the largest river basin in Nepal. The National Water Plan gives it a catchment of 60,400 km2, with 27,863 km2 in Nepal, and an average discharge of about 1,409 m3/s at Chatara [S1].
- Gandaki/Narayani has a catchment of 34,960 km2, with close to 90% in Nepal, and an average discharge of about 1,600 m3/s at Narayangadh [S1].
- Karnali has a catchment of 43,679 km2, with about 94% in Nepal, and an average discharge of about 1,397 m3/s at Chisapani [S1].
- The Gandaki basin is transboundary and extends from China through Nepal into India. ICIMOD gives it a drainage area of 46,300 km2, split roughly 72% Nepal, 18% India, and 10% China, and notes that it joins the Ganges at Hajipur near Patna [S2].

The implication is not that Nepal "controls" the Ganges. It does not. The implication is that Nepal occupies a hydrologically important upstream position in a basin system where dry-season water matters a great deal downstream.

## 2. Downstream Significance: Where The Evidence Is Strongest

The strongest downstream case is the Koshi basin. ICIMOD describes it as one of the most significant flow contributors to the Ganges River, shared by China, Nepal, and India, and supporting over 40 million people [S5]. Another ICIMOD source notes that the Koshi basin is only about 5% of the Ganges basin but contributes nearly 25% of the sediment load, with about 120 million tonnes of sediment transported from the basin into the Ganges each year [S6].

That sediment number matters as much as the water number. For downstream India, especially Bihar and the lower plains, the risk is not just shortage; it is also flood damage, channel instability, embankment stress, and long-run aggradation. In other words, Nepal's river systems are strategic because they deliver both water and hazard.

For Gandaki and Karnali, the significance is real but somewhat less often dramatized in the literature. The Gandaki working paper makes clear that the basin is transboundary, glacier-influenced, and economically and socially important across Nepal, China, and India [S2]. For Karnali, the hydro-climatic record is important because it shows that a large western basin can experience decreasing precipitation while discharge remains relatively constant over the observed period, which complicates simple "more rain equals more power" narratives [S3].

## 3. Treaty And Institutional Context

The bilateral architecture is project-based, not basin-sovereignty-based.

- The Kosi agreements were framed around a barrage, flood banks, canals, irrigation, hydropower, and erosion control on land in Nepal, with a coordination committee for implementation [S7].
- The Gandak agreement was similarly tied to the Gandak Irrigation and Power Project, not to a general basin-sharing framework [S8].
- The 1996 Mahakali Treaty is the most explicit on principle: Nepal's water requirements are to be given prime consideration, and local use up to 5% of the average annual flow at Pancheshwar is permitted, but any new project on the boundary river requires mutual agreement [S9].

My reading, based on these instruments, is that Nepal has formal rights and consultation leverage, but the institutional design narrows that leverage into specific projects and barrages. That is a meaningful constraint. It means Nepal can influence negotiation over works, timing, and benefits, but it cannot simply translate upstream geography into open-ended downstream control.

## 4. Flood, Sediment, And Water Security Risks

The Koshi is the clearest basin where flood and sediment risk become geopolitically visible. ICIMOD's Koshi basin work repeatedly frames the basin as prone to floods, landslides, GLOFs, and droughts, with cascading impacts across China, Nepal, and India [S5, S6]. A later ICIMOD flood-modelling note for the basin says the rugged topography and monsoon season make it especially prone to floods and landslides [S10].

The sediment story is not a side issue. It is a core engineering and governance problem. If sediment loads are high, reservoirs silt up faster, turbines wear faster, channel forms change, and flood risk can intensify downstream. That makes storage and hydropower engineering inseparable from erosion control, slope stability, and catchment management.

## 5. Climate Change: What It Changes Strategically

Climate change matters here less as a vague threat than as a force multiplier of variability.

ICIMOD's most recent HKH glacier reporting says glacier area across the Hindu Kush Himalaya fell by about 12% between 1990 and 2020, and that ice loss rates have doubled since 2000 [S11]. ICIMOD also says the region's cryosphere changes are "unprecedented and largely irreversible" under current warming trajectories, with floods, landslides, sedimentation, erosion, and GLOFs expected to rise [S12].

For Nepal specifically, the older ICIMOD glacier inventory material shows a clear long-run retreat signal, with Nepal's glacier area falling from 5,168.3 km2 in the 1980 baseline to 3,902.4 km2 in 2010, a decline of 24.5% [S13]. That is not a minor adjustment; it is a structural change in high-altitude storage.

The Karnali basin study is especially useful because it shows the kind of non-linear response that matters for energy planning. From 1981 to 2012, average precipitation trended downward by 4.91 mm/year, roughly 10% on average, while the basin's discharge remained constant over 1981-2010 and peak discharge lagged peak precipitation by about one month [S3]. That is exactly the sort of basin behavior that makes annual averages misleading.

The strategic inference is straightforward. Climate change will not just reduce water; it will change the timing, reliability, and sediment content of the water. For hydropower, that means the future problem is not only generation potential. It is also seasonal mismatch, dry-season reliability, dam safety, and dispatchability.

## 6. What Not To Overclaim

- Do not say Nepal "controls" downstream water security. It does not.
- Do not treat discharge numbers as timeless constants. The National Water Plan figures are useful order-of-magnitude references, but they are based on older data and station-specific records [S1].
- Do not use hydropower potential numbers as if they are all technically or economically buildable. WECS's 2019 assessment estimates 72,544 MW gross potential, but only 32,680 MW techno-economic potential [S4].
- Do not collapse climate risk into glacier retreat alone. Snow persistence, permafrost, landslides, sediment, and flood routing matter just as much [S12].

## 7. Synthesis For The Larger Project

The cleanest thesis direction from this memo is:

Nepal's water importance is real, but it is mostly a problem of system design rather than raw scarcity. The country sits upstream of politically and economically important basin systems, yet it lacks the storage, sediment control, and basin institutions needed to convert that geography into stable power, flood protection, and long-run strategic leverage.
