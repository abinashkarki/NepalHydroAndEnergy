# AEPC Renewable Framework and Progress Reports

Primary source for Nepal's off-grid and distributed solar story. AEPC ("Alternative Energy Promotion Centre") is the government institution responsible for **rural electrification through renewables**, standing alongside (not inside) NEA. It is the operator and financier of the solar-home-system (SHS) programme, community mini-grids, institutional rooftop systems, and the subsidy / concessional-finance channel that rural solar rides on.

## What the source covers

Periodic AEPC "Progress Report" / "Renewable Energy Framework" / annual yearbook documents cover:

- Cumulative SHS installations by district, province, and year
- Village-scale mini-grid portfolio (locations, capacity, operator)
- Institutional solar (schools, clinics, govt offices)
- Improved cookstove and biogas programmes (context, not solar)
- Subsidy structure and donor-financing channels
- Policy and regulatory framework for rural renewables

## Key data points (approximate, 2021–2024 window)

- **Cumulative SHS installed:** ~1.1 million systems (as of ~2021, with additions through 2024)
- **Cumulative SHS capacity:** ~80–100 MWp
- **Community mini-grids (operational):** ~30 sites, ~1.1 MWp aggregate
- **Institutional solar:** ~25,000 sites (rural clinics, schools, telecom, govt offices), ~15 MWp aggregate
- **Geography:** Karnali, Sudurpaschim, and remote hill districts of Koshi / Gandaki / Bagmati dominate installations (see [[data-rooftop-shs-deployment]])
- **Subsidy structure:** Tiered, with higher subsidies for remote/difficult districts; typical residential SHS subsidy ~30–50% of capex
- **Annual new installations:** declining as grid reach expands (grid displaces SHS on economic grounds above ~30 HH/km²), replacement/upgrade cycle becoming dominant

## Why AEPC matters strategically

AEPC's relationship with NEA is the most under-discussed institutional story in Nepal solar:

1. **AEPC reaches households NEA cannot.** Remote hill and Karnali districts are uneconomic for grid extension at current tariffs; AEPC's subsidized SHS is the only electrification pathway that fits those geographies.

2. **AEPC is the operational home of community-level solar.** No mini-grid, no micro-grid, no community solar cooperative in Nepal exists without AEPC technical backstop or finance.

3. **AEPC is the natural host for the agrivoltaic programme.** If [[agrivoltaics-and-land]] implies a lease-template law and a tariff adder, AEPC's rural footprint and cooperative linkages make it the natural programme administrator — not NEA.

4. **AEPC integrates into the political coalition for solar.** The rural-electrification constituency that has sustained AEPC for three decades is the same constituency that would vote for Terai agrivoltaics and Karnali off-grid scale-up.

## Documents and subsources

The "AEPC renewable framework" source is a composite of:

- **AEPC Yearbook / Annual Progress Report** (annual, typically one-year lag in publication)
- **National Renewable Energy Framework (NREF) 2017** and revisions
- **Renewable Energy Subsidy Policy 2016** and updates
- **AEPC-published feasibility studies** for specific mini-grid and institutional projects
- **Donor project completion reports** (ADB, WB, EIB, UNDP) that flow through AEPC programmes

## Data-quality notes

- AEPC yearbooks lag by ~1–2 years in publication.
- District-level data fidelity is high (AEPC subsidizes per-system; each install has a record) but aggregation into the public yearbook is lagged and sometimes incomplete.
- The 1.1 M cumulative SHS count includes decommissioned and replaced systems; the *operational* fleet is likely 700,000–900,000 at any time.
- Mini-grid reliability / continuous-operation data is uneven; some grids have been dormant between donor-project cycles.
