# Tanahu Hydropower Project

A 140 MW **storage-type** reservoir scheme on the Seti River — the
only large dam-and-reservoir project actually in active construction
in Nepal, and therefore the single most important test case for
whether the country can execute [[storage-deficit]]-closing assets at
all. ADB-financed, NEA-owned, ~67% complete as of FY 2024/25, and
shadowed by two Accountability Mechanism complaints over land and
buffer-zone compensation.

## Significance

Tanahu matters less for its nameplate 140 MW and more as **proof of
concept** — is Nepal capable of delivering a large reservoir project
at all, on a reasonable timeline, with multilateral financing? Until
Tanahu commissions, the only operating storage plant in the country is
[[kulekhani-cascade]] (106 MW). Tanahu will nearly triple the storage
fleet on day one.

The strategic implication cascades upward. If Tanahu completes,
[[dudhkoshi-storage]] (670 MW) and [[budhigandaki]] (1200 MW) become
easier to finance: the next multilateral conversation no longer
begins "Nepal has never actually built a large reservoir since 1982."
If Tanahu fails — especially on the social-compliance dimension the
Accountability Mechanism is reviewing — future large storage
proposals will carry that memory as a risk premium.

Compare Tanahu (140 MW, reservoir, ADB, domestic) to [[arun-3]] (900 MW,
peaking RoR, SJVN-India, export-oriented). Both are under construction
but solve different problems: Tanahu addresses dry-season [[firm-power]]
for Nepal; Arun-3 adds monsoon export capacity to India. Nepal's
structural energy problem is the former, not the latter.

## Limitations & Controversies

**Social-compliance complaints.** The 2024 and 2025 Accountability
Mechanism filings are the visible surface of a harder question: can
a large reservoir in the middle hills be executed with buffer-zone
demarcation, untitled-land registration, and free prior informed
consent done properly? Current complaints flag all three. ADB
accepted the 2025 complaint for eligibility review in April.

**Schedule discipline.** 67% physical progress after ~10 years of
construction is slower than comparable multilateral-financed projects
elsewhere. Each additional year compounds NEA's dry-season
[[firm-power]] exposure, since the entire medium-term planning
rationale for Tanahu depends on it arriving before the
[[seasonal-mismatch]] pressure peaks.

**Not a basin-scale answer.** Even at full output, Tanahu cannot
close the dry-season gap on its own. It is one node in a portfolio
that would need Dudh Koshi Storage, Budhi Gandaki or similar scale
to operate alongside. Framing Tanahu as "the storage solution" sets
up disappointment; framing it as "the first storage receipt" sets
up a reasonable expectation.
