# Karnali Basin

## Overview

The Karnali basin is Nepal's western major river system and the **largest by area** at ~43,153 km² within Nepal. Its tributaries include Bheri, West Seti, Humla Karnali, Mugu Karnali, and Tila. The Karnali feeds the Ghaghara, which contributes ~40% of the Ganges' baseflow at Varanasi.

This is Nepal's **most dramatically underused** basin — essentially zero large-scale hydropower despite having the best sites.

## Key Facts

| Fact | Value |
|------|-------|
| Area in Nepal | 43,153 km² |
| Average discharge (border) | 1,256 m³/s (~39.6 BCM/yr) |
| Monsoon share | 65–72% |
| Peak-to-dry ratio | 15–20:1 (best stability) |
| Gross hydropower potential | 20,385 MW (28.1% of national total) |

## Why It Matters Most

The Karnali has the **best dry-season flow stability** of any major Nepali system (lowest peak-to-dry ratio at 15–20:1 vs 25–50:1 for Koshi). WECS calls it the basin with the highest hydropower potential. It is the most obvious strategic priority for future development.

## Why It Remains Undeveloped

- **Remoteness** — western Nepal has historically had fewer roads, less political attention, and less financing reach
- **Transmission gap** — the grid west of Butwal relies on legacy 132 kV lines incapable of handling large generation
- **Political neglect** — eastern-centric development patterns systematically disadvantaged the west
- The proposed [[karnali-chisapani]] (10,800 MW) alone would transform Nepal's energy posture, but it remains conceptual

## Climate Projections

CMIP6 modeling projects the Karnali will experience **3–23% decrease in winter/spring discharge** by end of century, alongside **18–51% increase in monsoon/post-monsoon discharge**. Temporal concentration of water will become even more extreme. See [[peak-water]].

A useful basin-specific warning from ICIMOD: over **1981–2012**, average precipitation in the Karnali study basin trended downward by **4.91 mm/year**, yet discharge stayed broadly constant over the observed record. That is exactly the kind of rainfall-flow decoupling that makes annual averages misleading for energy planning.

## Downstream Significance

The Karnali/Ghaghara system serves ~2–3 million ha in UP and connects to Bangladesh's Ganges flow. See [[ganges-contribution]].
