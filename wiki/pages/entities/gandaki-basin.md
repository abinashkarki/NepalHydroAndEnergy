# Gandaki Basin

## Overview

The Gandaki (Narayani) basin is Nepal's central major river system, draining ~32,148 km² within Nepal. Its tributaries include Kali Gandaki, Marsyangdi, Budhi Gandaki, Trishuli, Seti, Madi, and East Rapti. The Kali Gandaki gorge between Annapurna and Dhaulagiri drops >5,500 vertical meters — by some measures the world's deepest.

This is Nepal's **most developed hydropower corridor**.

## Key Facts

| Fact | Value |
|------|-------|
| Area in Nepal | 32,148 km² |
| Average discharge (border) | 1,952 m³/s (~61.6 BCM/yr) |
| Monsoon share | 74–80% |
| Peak-to-dry ratio | 22–30:1 |
| Gross hydropower potential | 19,803 MW (27.3% of national total) |

## Hydropower Significance

The most mature "buildable" basin. Hosts Nepal's early landmark projects — Kali Gandaki A (144 MW), Middle Marsyangdi (70 MW), and the [[kulekhani-cascade]] (on a tributary). The corridor benefited from road access (Prithvi and Siddhartha highways) and historical institutional familiarity.

The proposed [[budhigandaki]] (1,200 MW) would be the basin's first major storage project and a system-shaping intervention for [[nepal-energy-profile]].

## Constraints

- **[[sediment-as-design-constraint]]** — the Kali Gandaki A plant is the definitive case study for turbine erosion. Suspended sediment spikes from 20 ppm (dry) to 50,000 ppm (monsoon). 65 million tonnes/year pass the dam site.
- More developed does not mean fully tapped. Significant untapped tributary potential remains.

## Downstream Significance

The Gandak system irrigates ~1.5–2 million ha in Bihar and eastern Uttar Pradesh. The Gandak Treaty (1959) gave Nepal only 15 MW from a major barrage — one of the most cited examples of treaty asymmetry. See [[hydro-geopolitics]].
