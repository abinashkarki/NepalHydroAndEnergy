## The Stranded Generation Case Study

Likhu-2 generated only **~146 GWh (60% of contracted energy)** in its first full
operating year (FY 2023/24). The COD was July 31, 2023 — two weeks into the fiscal
year — making FY 2023/24 a full operating year. At 60% of contracted energy,
Likhu-2 would appear in the bottom quartile of Nepal's private IPP generation
performance data, alongside projects genuinely failing due to [[q-design-discharge]]
optimism.

**This appearance is misleading.** CARE Ratings Nepal's analysis confirms that the
cause was not hydrology, not Q-design failure, and not a turbine defect. It was a
**grid evacuation bottleneck** at the NEA New Khimti substation.

The New Khimti substation — the single gateway connecting the Likhu basin to the
national grid via the Khimti–Dhalkebar 220 kV backbone — was equipped with a
single 100 MVA transformer when Likhu-2 commissioned. It had been sized to service
Upper Tamakoshi (456 MW). As the Likhu cascade plants (combined ~213 MW) came
online between 2021 and 2024, they saturated the node. When the substation cannot
step up and push power down the 220 kV line, a run-of-river plant has one option:
dump water. Likhu-2 effectively wasted 40% of viable contracted generation because
the state transmission infrastructure was not ready for the private generation it
had approved. See [[buildability]].

## The Recovery — Grid Fixed, Plant Proved

In late 2023, NEA installed a new **200 MVA auto-transformer** at New Khimti,
raising total substation capacity to 300 MVA. The result was immediate:

| Fiscal Year | Contracted (GWh) | Actual (GWh) | % of Contracted | Cause |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| FY 2023/24 | 242.37 | ~146 | **60%** | NEA substation bottleneck (stranded generation) |
| FY 2024/25 | 242.37 | ~221 | **91%** | Bottleneck resolved + favourable hydrology |

Source: CARE Ratings Nepal (March 2026 issuer rating disclosure for GHAL).

91% of contracted energy in a fully operational year places Likhu-2 among the
better-performing private IPPs in the sector — not an underperformer. The jump from
60% to 91% is not explained by improved hydrology (though that helped); it is
explained by whether the state had built the transmission infrastructure to receive
what the private plant was ready to generate.

This has a direct implication for how the [[q-design-discharge]] performance table
should read Likhu-2. The FY 2023/24 figure is not evidence of design-energy
overestimation. It is evidence of transmission planning failure.

## Financial Architecture

The Likhu-2 SPV (GHAL) was originally budgeted at NPR 9,313 million. A capacity
upgrade from 33.4 MW to 55 MW, combined with Interest During Construction, raised
the final cost to NPR 10,968–11,246 million (~NPR 204 million/MW). Financing:

- Debt: NPR 8,534 million (~78% of capital)
- Equity: ~22%
- Debt consortium led by **Machhapuchchhre Bank (MBL)**, with Mega Bank, Nepal
  SBI, Nepal Bangladesh Bank, Everest Bank, and Vibor Society Development Bank

The financial metrics in FY 2024/25 (post-recovery):

| Metric | Value |
|---|---|
| Overall gearing ratio | 3.60x |
| Total debt / Gross Cash Accruals | 19.51x (improved from 35.45x in FY 2023/24) |
| Interest coverage ratio | 1.66x |
| CARE Nepal rating (March 2026) | CARE-NP BBB- (Is) |

A 1.66x interest coverage ratio is thin but serviceable — typical for a highly
leveraged first-decade IPP when generation is near contracted levels. The main
ongoing risk is exposure to Nepal's floating interest rate environment: with
NPR 8.5 billion in variable-rate debt, base rate movements can quickly erode the
1.66x cushion.

## The Take-and-Pay Exposure

Approximately **25% of Likhu-2's contracted energy** is on "take-and-pay" terms —
NEA only purchases this tranche if the national grid requires it. During monsoon
season, when all domestic RoR projects peak simultaneously and export channels
to India are saturated, NEA actively curtails take-and-pay purchases. This
structural revenue leakage is a persistent risk on top of the leverage ratio.

Likhu-1 (the larger MV Dugar project upstream) has better terms: 80% take-or-pay,
20% take-and-pay.

## The Likhu Cascade

Likhu-2 is one of four projects in the Likhu cascade, all now fully operational:

| Project | MW | Developer | COD | Primary Equity |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Likhu-1 | 77.0 | Pan Himalaya Energy Ltd | Apr 2024 | MV Dugar Group + Mogha Energy (UAE) |
| **Likhu-2** | **55.0** | **Global Hydropower Associate Ltd** | **Jul 2023** | **MV Dugar Group + Mogha Energy (UAE)** |
| Likhu-4 | 52.4 | Green Ventures Ltd | Nov 2021 | Triveni Group + Bhilwara Energy (India) |
| Likhu Khola A | 29.04 | Numbur Himalaya Hydropower Ltd | Feb 2022 | MV Dugar Group + Mogha Energy (UAE) |

Note: Butwal Power Company (BPC) has no equity stake in any cascade plant.
BPC's subsidiary HCEL provided engineering consultancy only.

The MV Dugar Group secured Foreign Direct Investment from **Mogha Energy DMCC**,
an energy investment firm based in the UAE. This FDI model — a Nepali conglomerate
pairing with foreign capital for a cluster of simultaneous projects — is a
structural development in Nepal's IPP financing beyond the traditional domestic
bank consortium.

All four plants share the New Khimti evacuation corridor. Following the 2023
transformer upgrade, NEA secured export approvals from India's Central Electricity
Authority (CEA) to wheel power from Likhu-4 and Likhu-2 across the
Dhalkebar–Muzaffarpur 400 kV interconnector into the Indian day-ahead market —
transforming the same corridor that caused stranded generation in 2023 into an
export pathway by 2024/25.

## Likhu-4 — A Different Story in the Same Cascade

Likhu-4 (52.4 MW, Green Ventures Ltd) was originally designed as a **120 MW
export-oriented** project, intended to wheel power directly to India. A dispute over
cross-border transmission wheeling charges caused GVL to abandon the export model
entirely — redesigning and downscaling to 52.4 MW for the domestic grid. The
project commissioned November 2021 but suffered a structural collapse of a
desanding basin wall on the eve of production testing (May 2021), publicly blamed
on the Indian civil contractor SSNR Projects. Green Ventures currently carries a
CARE-NP BB rating, constrained by high capital costs and the legacy of its
commissioning failures. See [[buildability]].
