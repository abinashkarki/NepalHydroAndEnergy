# Karnali Chisapani Project

The **largest single hydropower project ever proposed for Nepal** at 10,800 MW — an order of magnitude beyond anything operating or under construction. Located on the [[karnali-basin]] main stem near the Chisapani gorge in the western Terai, this is the project that would transform Nepal into one of Asia's major power producers. It is also the most purely conceptual project in the wiki: no DPR, no timeline, and no single institution driving it forward.

### Engineering
A proposed ~270 m dam on the Karnali main stem — the largest single hydropower project ever proposed for Nepal. The design concept envisions a storage dam that would impound the Karnali's monsoon flow and release it through the dry season, providing both power generation and downstream regulation. But no detailed engineering design exists; the 10,800 MW figure is a planning estimate from basin potential studies, not a turbine-by-turbine layout.

### Storage
The [[wb-ganges-strategic-basin-assessment]] describes a valley-shape paradox that constrains Karnali Chisapani's effective storage: despite the enormous dam height, the steep Karnali valley walls mean that only 16.2 of the total 28.2 BCM can be actively regulated. The remaining 12 BCM is dead storage — water sitting below the lowest outlet level, trapped by topography. This is a geological constraint, not a design choice, and it means the project's flood-control and irrigation-regulation value is structurally capped regardless of dam height.

### Output
At 10,800 MW, Karnali Chisapani would be one of the world's largest single hydropower installations — comparable to Brazil's Belo Monte (11,233 MW) and exceeding China's Xiluodu (13,860 MW, though that is a cascade). But the project has no design energy estimate — a sign of just how conceptual it remains. For comparison: Belo Monte generates ~39,500 GWh/yr; a 10,800 MW storage plant on the Karnali could theoretically produce 35,000–50,000 GWh/yr depending on head and flow regime. The output benefits (flood control for downstream India, irrigation regulation for the Ghaghara system, and massive [[storage-deficit]] relief) are strategic promises, not bankable numbers.

### Financial
No cost estimate exists at the conceptual stage, but a back-of-envelope calculation is sobering. At a conservative USD 1.2 million/MW for a storage dam of this scale (Three Gorges came in at ~USD 1,000/kW in 1990s dollars; large Himalayan storage runs higher), the total construction cost could reach USD 13 billion — roughly one-third of Nepal's annual GDP. Even a fraction of that would require co-investment at a scale Nepal has never attempted.

## Historical Context

Karnali Chisapani has been a fixture of Nepal's hydropower imagination since at least the 1960s, when early basin surveys identified the Chisapani gorge as a potential mega-dam site. The project appears in every generation of Nepal's hydropower master planning — from Hari Man Shrestha's seminal 1966 PhD thesis through the 1990s basin studies to the World Bank's Ganges Strategic Basin Assessment. But it has never moved beyond the conceptual stage because the question has never been *whether* it can be built — the question is *who* builds it and *who* buys the power.

The pattern is instructive: every time Nepal's hydropower ambition resets (post-1990 democracy, post-Maoist conflict, post-2015 constitution), Karnali Chisapani reappears as the aspirational endpoint. The gap between recurring aspiration and zero progress is itself a data point for [[buildability]] — a reminder that megaprojects require more than good topography.

Karnali Chisapani belongs in the same conversation as Grand Inga: a technically viable mega-site that cannot advance without a governance and financing architecture that does not yet exist. Itaipu required a bilateral treaty between Brazil and Paraguay in 1973; construction started in 1975 and took 9 years. The comparison suggests that the engineering timeline is the easy part — the political timeline is the constraint.

## Downstream Bilateral Context

The Karnali River becomes the Ghaghara River in India, joining the Ganges system in Uttar Pradesh. The Ghaghara is the largest tributary of the Ganges by discharge, and its monsoon floods are a recurring disaster in eastern UP and Bihar. A 16 BCM effective storage reservoir at Chisapani could regulate approximately 15–20% of Ghaghara wet-season flow, converting destructive flood peaks into dry-season irrigation releases.

The bilateral calculus is asymmetric in ways that have blocked progress:
- **Nepal's benefit**: 10,800 MW of generation, massive export revenue, domestic dry-season firming, national prestige
- **India's benefit**: flood control (valued in billions of avoided damages), irrigation regulation for the Ghaghara command area, a large block of clean energy at the border
- **The valuation problem**: India can get flood-control benefits from domestic dams on the Ghaghara downstream without a treaty; Nepal cannot capture those benefits unilaterally without India as co-investor and offtaker. This is the same asymmetric valuation that has stalled [[pancheshwar]].

## Why It Remains Conceptual

1. **Scale of investment far exceeds Nepal's fiscal capacity.** Total cost could reach USD 10–15 billion — at minimum 4–6x the cost of Nepal's most expensive project to date (Budhigandaki at ~USD 2.77 billion).
2. **Requires India as co-investor and primary buyer.** No other market can absorb 10,800 MW. India's domestic hydropower ambitions (Arunachal Pradesh, Bhutan joint ventures) compete for the same political and financial bandwidth.
3. **Downstream bilateral politics.** Water sharing, flood-control valuation, and irrigation benefit allocation are unresolved at the conceptual level. The Pancheshwar precedent (30 years, zero output) does not inspire confidence.
4. **Environmental and social displacement at unprecedented scale.** A 270 m dam on the Karnali main stem would displace tens of thousands of people across multiple districts (Kailali, Bardiya, Surkhet, Achham). The reservoir would flood significant agricultural land in the Terai.
5. **No institutional champion.** Unlike Budhigandaki (Budhigandaki Company Limited), Dudhkoshi (NEA/AIIB), or Arun-3 (SJVN India), Karnali Chisapani has no developer, no lead financier, and no project office.
6. **Pre-feasibility has never been funded.** The project has never received the USD 10–20 million that a serious pre-feasibility study would require. It exists only in basin-scale assessments.

## What Pre-Feasibility Would Require

To move from concept to credible candidate, Karnali Chisapani would need:
- A **bilateral agreement-in-principle** with India establishing a joint project structure (treaty, not MOU — the Pancheshwar lesson)
- A **pre-feasibility study** funded by a multilateral institution (World Bank, ADB, or AIIB) covering geotechnical investigation, hydrological modelling, environmental and social baseline, and preliminary economic analysis
- A **displacement and resettlement framework** that accounts for the project's unprecedented footprint
- A **power purchase framework** with India that covers export from a plant of this scale — the existing cross-border PPAs (Arun-3, Upper Karnali) are for projects under 1,000 MW; 10,800 MW is a different order of commercial commitment
- A **transmission corridor plan** — the existing Dhalkebar-Muzaffarpur 400 kV cross-border line is rated for ~1,000 MW; Karnali Chisapani would need a dedicated high-capacity interconnector, possibly HVDC

## Significance

If ever realized, Karnali Chisapani would transform Nepal's energy position from regional exporter to Asian power producer. It would provide massive [[storage-deficit]] relief, flood control for downstream India, and irrigation regulation for the Ghaghara system.

But the project also illustrates the valley-shape paradox from the [[wb-ganges-strategic-basin-assessment]]: despite the enormous dam, steep valley walls constrain effective storage to roughly half the total volume. The dead storage is a permanent reminder that topography, not ambition, sets the ceiling.

More broadly, Karnali Chisapani is the limiting case for Nepal's hydropower strategy. If [[pancheshwar]] tests whether bilateral governance can deliver a 6,480 MW project, Karnali Chisapani tests whether the governance architecture at all can reach an order of magnitude beyond that. The answer, so far, is no.
