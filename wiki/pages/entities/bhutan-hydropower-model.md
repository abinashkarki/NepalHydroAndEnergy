# Bhutan Hydropower Model

Bhutan is the most frequent regional comparison for Nepal's hydropower trajectory. The comparison is instructive and somewhat brutal.

## Bhutan's Success

| Metric | Bhutan | Nepal |
|--------|--------|-------|
| Hydro contribution to GDP | ~14% | 3–5% |
| Hydro share of government revenue | ~26% | Marginal |
| Export price to India | $0.05–0.07/kWh | $0.03–0.05/kWh |
| Model | India-financed, India-built, India-integrated | Self-financed, fragmented, sovereignty-preserving |

Bhutan's flagship plants (Chukha, Tala, Mangdechhu) were built with Indian grants and soft loans. India is co-investor with direct economic interest in project success. This gives Bhutan an unconditionally secure export market.

## Why Nepal Chose Differently

Nepal's more vibrant democracy, critical civil society, and strategic desire to balance Indian influence against Chinese investment led it to resist the "Bhutan model." Nepal opted for a **slower, contested, fragmented development path** to preserve economic and political sovereignty.

This is a defensible policy choice, but it costs money: approximately **$80–120 million per year** in foregone export premium at current volumes.

## Lessons

- Bhutan proves Himalayan hydropower can generate national wealth
- But Bhutan's model requires granting India near-total control
- Bhutan's projects have also suffered massive delays and cost overruns under Indian management
- Nepal cannot replicate Bhutan without fundamentally changing its foreign policy posture
