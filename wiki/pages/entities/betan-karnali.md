# Betan Karnali HP

Betan Karnali is a large semi-reservoir hydropower project on the Karnali, historically carried in the registry as a 688 MW survey-stage project and more recently advanced in public reporting as a 439 MW generation-licence / investment-approval project. It is pinned in the [[data-layer-priority-watchlist]] and [[data-layer-top-10-capacity-projects]] because it sits directly in the Karnali buildout story: large western capacity, storage-like peaking value, institutional Nepali financing, and dependency on how [[upper-karnali]] is finally resolved upstream.

## Why It Matters

Betan Karnali is one of the clearest examples of why the wiki separates registry capacity from strategic value. The map's top-10 layer uses the older 688 MW registry record because that is the source field used to rank the display set. Current public project reporting, however, describes a **439 MW semi-reservoir** scheme with an estimated cost around NPR 93-93.4 billion.

That revised scale still matters. A 439 MW semi-reservoir plant with peaking value is more useful to Nepal's dry-season and evening-balancing problem than a simple wet-season-heavy capacity addition. It also matters institutionally: reporting on the 2025 licence and investment approval frames the ownership/finance structure around the Employees Provident Fund, NEA, Electricity Generation Company Ltd., EPF depositors, civil servants, local communities, and the public.

## Status And Timing

This page should be read as a current project record, not only as a survey-stage registry entry. Public reporting in July 2025 said Nepal's energy ministry granted a generation licence for the **439 MW** Betan Karnali project and that the company aimed to start construction immediately and complete within five years. The Rising Nepal then reported in November 2025 that the Investment Board Nepal approved investment for the **439 MW** Betan Karnali Semi-Reservoir Hydropower Project and formed a negotiation committee for the Project Development Agreement.

## Risks And Open Questions

The first open question is the **688 MW versus 439 MW** basis. The map ranking follows the older registry value; the project-development story now appears to have moved to the smaller semi-reservoir configuration. Any downstream analysis should state which number it is using.

The second question is finance and sequencing. Betan's institutional-investor model is attractive because it is domestic and long-tenor, but the project still has to close financing, sign/complete PDA arrangements, award construction packages, and coordinate with upstream [[upper-karnali]] assumptions. Until those steps are visible, the five-year completion target should be read as a developer/government objective rather than a bankable COD forecast.
