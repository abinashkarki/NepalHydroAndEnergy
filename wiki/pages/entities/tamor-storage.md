# Tamor Storage

Tamor Storage is a proposed 200 MW storage hydropower project on the Tamor River in eastern Nepal, currently at the survey/study stage.

## Storage Value

Storage hydropower is the critical missing piece in Nepal's generation portfolio. Run-of-river projects dominate the fleet — they produce abundant energy during the monsoon but collapse during the dry season when system demand peaks. See [[storage-deficit]] and [[seasonal-mismatch]].

Tamor Storage, at 200 MW with a reservoir, would provide **firm, dispatchable capacity** to the eastern grid — the kind of generation that can be scheduled to meet evening peak demand in the dry winter months. This is fundamentally different from the run-of-river model where output is at the mercy of seasonal hydrology.

## Development Hurdles

Storage projects in Nepal face three compounding challenges:
1. **Geology**: Dam sites in the young, seismically active Himalaya require extensive investigation
2. **Resettlement**: Reservoirs displace communities, creating political and social costs that run-of-river projects avoid
3. **Financing**: Storage projects have higher capital costs and longer construction timelines, requiring a PPA framework that compensates firm capacity — not just energy (kWh)

The ERC's 2026 directive on reservoir-based power plants attempts to address this by transitioning large projects to a cost-plus determination model, explicitly allowing developers to factor in loan interest, depreciation, and foreign exchange risk. See [[nea-triple-authority]].

## System Context

In a system increasingly saturated with monsoon-heavy run-of-river generation, Tamor Storage's value lies not in its annual energy contribution but in its seasonal firmness. A 200 MW reservoir that can deliver into the dry-season evening peak would have outsized system value compared to a 200 MW run-of-river project that adds to the monsoon surplus. See [[firm-power]] and [[solar-hydro-complementarity]] for the integrated portfolio argument.
