## The Trishuli Corridor — Nepal's Densest River

Trishuli Galchhi sits on the single most hydropower-dense river in Nepal. From the
original 24 MW [[trishuli|Trishuli]] plant (1967) to the 216 MW
[[upper-trishuli-1]] upstream, the Trishuli corridor now hosts roughly 540 MW of
cumulative capacity across generation-licensed, under-construction, and operating
projects — including [[upper-trishuli-3a]] (60 MW), [[upper-trishuli-3b]] (37 MW),
and multiple smaller plants. See [[trishuli-cascade]] for the full corridor profile.

The corridor's defining advantage is proximity: the Kathmandu load center is under
50 km away, meaning minimal transmission losses and no dependence on the
Khimti–Dhalkebar trunk that bottlenecks eastern-basin projects. Trishuli Galchhi's
location in Nuwakot district, directly abutting the Kathmandu Valley's northern
edge, makes it one of the most strategically sited projects in the pipeline.

## Low-Head Design — a Different Engineering Profile

At 58.5 m gross head with Kaplan turbines and 176 m³/s design discharge, Trishuli
Galchhi represents a fundamentally different engineering philosophy from the
high-head Pelton plants that dominate Nepal's private IPP pipeline.

**What low-head means for this project:**

- **High unit flow, low head.** Unlike a 400 m-head Pelton project that funnels a
  small jet of water through a narrow penstock, Trishuli Galchhi must move 176 m³/s
  of water through a large-diameter 8.15 km headrace tunnel. Civil works are
  proportionally larger — the intake, desilting basin, tunnel diameter, and
  powerhouse tailrace all scale with discharge volume, not head.

- **Run-of-river in the truest sense.** A 24.5 m barrage dam with 8 × 12 m radial
  gates operates as a river-level diversion — no significant storage, no reservoir
  pondage beyond diurnal regulation. The plant's output tracks river flow almost in
  real time, making it more exposed to seasonal hydrology than plants with small
  pondage or peaking reservoirs.

- **Lower cost per MW — in theory.** At ~NPR 14.40 billion (NPR 192M/MW), the
  capital cost per megawatt is at the lower end of the benchmark range. Low-head
  plants trade expensive steel penstocks and surge shafts for large concrete volumes
  and extensive tunnel systems. The 70:30 debt-equity ratio reflects a conventional
  consortium structure where lower per-MW cost keeps absolute equity commitments
  manageable.

- **Kaplan turbines** for the 2 × 44 MW units are the correct choice for the head
  range: double-regulated (adjustable runner blades and wicket gates) to maintain
  efficiency across fluctuating river flows. This is technically sound but adds
  mechanical complexity and maintenance cost compared to simple Pelton runners.

## Api Power's Portfolio Play

Trishuli Galchhi is not a standalone project but part of Api Power Company Ltd's
broader portfolio buildout. Through the Siddhakali Power Ltd SPV (~80% Api Power
equity), the project sits alongside other Api Power assets including Upper Naugarh
Gad (8 MW).

The CARE-NP B- issuer rating assigned to Api Power in 2022 tells the portfolio
story. A B- rating — four notches below investment grade — reflects significant
financial stress at the holding-company level:

- **Multiple concurrent under-construction exposures** across the portfolio strain
  developer equity commitments. A developer simultaneously funding several SPVs
  faces disproportionate liquidity pressure during construction delays (which
  Trishuli Galchhi has experienced).
- **Concentrated river risk.** Api Power's portfolio is geographically
  concentrated, exposing its aggregate revenue to the same hydrological and
  regulatory corridor dynamics.
- **The ICRA Grade 4 from 2016** pre-dated the financial stress and is now six
  years stale — it predates the portfolio's construction-phase accumulations and
  the 2019–2023 interest rate cycle that strained leveraged IPP developers across
  Nepal.

The Laxmi Bank-led consortium financing structure is conventional by Nepali IPP
standards, but the developer's credit profile matters more for construction-phase
lenders than for operational-phase debt service. If construction delays compress
rainy-season generation before the loan repayment clock starts, the project's
economics tighten immediately.

## Construction Delay Risk

The original PPA filing set RCOD at May 2020 — a date that has clearly passed
without commissioning. Six years from projected completion with no public progress
reports suggests at least a 4–5 year delay, consistent with patterns at dozens of
Nepali IPP projects that encountered post-2015 earthquake reconstruction costs,
COVID-era construction interruptions, COVID-era supply chain disruptions, and the
protracted equity-raising timeline typical of lower-credit developers.

No recent progress reports or construction milestone disclosures have been found
for Trishuli Galchhi. The absence of contractor information, verified completion
percentages, or an updated commissioning target in the public record is itself a
data point: projects nearing COD tend to produce disclosure, while projects stuck
in mid-construction tend to produce silence.

## Cascade Context — Sharing the Trishuli

Trishuli Galchhi shares the river with some of Nepal's most prominent projects:

- **Upstream:** [[upper-trishuli-1]] (216 MW) is the cascade's dominant project,
  with a different developer consortium (Korea South-East Power, Nepal Water &
  Energy Development Company) and a far more complex engineering profile.
- **Parallel and downstream:** [[upper-trishuli-3a]] (60 MW) and
  [[upper-trishuli-3b]] (37 MW) operate in the same reach, with interlinked
  hydrology.
- **Downstream:** The original [[trishuli]] plant (24 MW, 1967) is Nepal's
  second-oldest operating hydropower asset and remains in service under NEA
  operation — a living piece of Nepal's hydropower history at the bottom of the
  cascade.

Unlike the Likhu cascade ([[trishuli-cascade|Trishuli context also described here]]),
the Trishuli corridor is not dominated by a single developer or lending group. It
is a competitive corridor with multiple developers, multiple contractor groups, and
varying technical approaches — from high-head tunnels (Upper Trishuli-1) to
low-head barrages (Trishuli Galchhi) to medium-head plants (3A/3B). This diversity
is a strength in normal operations but a coordination challenge during extreme
hydrological events where upstream gates affect downstream head.
