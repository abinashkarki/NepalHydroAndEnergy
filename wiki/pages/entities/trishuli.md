## Specifications (NEA FY 2081/82)

Trishuli is **Nepal's oldest operating hydropower plant**, commissioned in
**1967** with Indian government funding. Originally 21 MW (7 × 3 MW units),
it was rehabilitated in 1995 to 24 MW with six 3.5 MW Francis units and one
3 MW standby unit. As a peaking run-of-river plant on the Trishuli River
in Nuwakot, it has anchored the Kathmandu Valley grid for nearly six decades.

In FY 2081/82, Trishuli completed significant rehabilitation work: a 10 MVA
(66/11 kV) and an 18 MVA (6.6/66 kV) transformer were installed, tested, and
commissioned. A 6.6 kV, 3000A switchgear and a 400V, 300A LT AC panel were
also commissioned. The auto guide vane closing system was modernized and
redesigned across all units for improved overspeed protection — a critical
safety upgrade for a plant running 1960s turbine hardware.

Powerhouse maintenance covered rotor shaft work on Unit 1, bearing repairs
on Units 2, 5, and 6, MIV repair on Unit 2, and oil cooler installation on
Unit 1.

## The Heritage Plant — 58 Years and Counting

Trishuli was commissioned when Nepal's grid barely existed. At 21 MW, it
was the first major power source serving Kathmandu — and for years, it was
the grid. Over 58 years, it has accumulated **6,031.9 GWh** of lifetime
generation, roughly a third of [[kali-gandaki-a]]'s lifetime output from
one-sixth the capacity, purely because it has been running for 35 more years.

The 1995 rehabilitation — from 21 to 24 MW — was Nepal's first major
hydropower upgrade. The current rehabilitation program (transformers,
switchgear, digital governors, overspeed protection) is the second round.
At 58 years, the question is not whether Trishuli needs another rehab but
whether the electromechanical core — turbine runners, generator windings,
penstock integrity — can sustain another upgrade cycle or whether the
plant is approaching end of economic life.

This is the **aging-fleet extreme case**. Every rehabilitation argument
applied to [[kali-gandaki-a]] (22 years old) and [[marsyangdi]] (36 years old)
applies to Trishuli at nearly triple the age. The plant that Nepal's grid
was built around is also the plant that demonstrates, more starkly than any
other, what Himalayan hydro looks like at 60 — and whether another round of
rehab is technically possible, financially justified, or a signal that NEA
needs a replacement strategy for its oldest assets.

## 60% of Design — The Fleeting Rehab Benefit

Trishuli's 60% design-performance ratio is the lowest in the NEA fleet.
At 97.7 GWh from a 163 GWh design, the rehab that raised capacity from 21
to 24 MW in 1995 did not prevent progressive output decline. The 17%
year-over-year drop (FY 2080/81 to 2081/82) suggests the decline is
continuing.

This is the structural problem: a 60% PLF at 24 MW compares unfavorably
with even the private IPP median. The current rehab — new transformers,
digital switchgear, modernized overspeed protection — addresses electrical
and control infrastructure but does not touch the hydraulic core:
turbine runners, guide vanes, penstock lining, intake structure. Those
are the components where 58 years of sediment-laden Himalayan river flow
accumulates surface wear and geometric erosion.

The parallel is [[chameliya-hydropower]]: a plant built at extreme cost
that underperforms due to engineering failures. Trishuli is the inverse —
a plant built at low cost for its era that is now underperforming due to
decades of normal wear. Both end up in the same place: a generation asset
that delivers substantially below what the nameplate promises. See
[[claim-mw-not-equal-value]].
