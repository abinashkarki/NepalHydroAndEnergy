## Specifications (NEA FY 2081/82)

Kulekhani I is **Nepal's only reservoir-type hydropower station** —
a dam-and-reservoir facility that stores monsoon runoff and releases it
for generation during the dry months when the RoR fleet collapses. Two
30 MW Francis units operate as a peaking plant, also supporting voltage
and system stability for the central grid.

The reservoir has a 65 m uncontrolled spillway and two radial gates
designed for operation at EL. 1530 masl. On 12 Ashoj 2081, heavy
rainfall caused the water level to rise beyond the spillway's capacity —
both radial gates were opened at 1,530.20 masl to stabilize the
reservoir, a reminder that even Nepal's only reservoir must handle
extreme inflow events that RoR headworks simply pass through.

## The Seasonal Shift in Practice

Kulekhani I takes the water that RoR plants must spill during monsoon
surplus and converts it into dry-season generation from Mangsir through
Baisakh (November–May). The NEA report states the plant "stores monsoon
water and operates mainly during the dry season to meet peak demand."
The reservoir level data confirms this cycle: a 45.2 m drawdown between
the post-monsoon peak (1,531.5 masl in Ashoj) and the dry-season trough
(1,486.28 masl in Baisakh/Chaitra).

This seasonal shifting is categorically different from the 4–6 hours of
"peaking" pondage that PRoR plants like Marsyangdi and Kali Gandaki A
use for daily load-smoothing. Those plants cannot store water across
months — when the river drops in January, so does their output.
Kulekhani I can.

The **64.5% year-over-year increase** in generation (FY 2080/81 to
2081/82) tells the other half of the story. A reservoir plant is still
governed by the monsoon that fills it. In a good monsoon year, the
reservoir fills early and generation rises; in a drought year, the
drawdown deepens and dry-season output collapses. The 279.8 GWh combined
cascade generation in FY 2081/82 was a function of heavy monsoon filling,
not a guaranteed floor.

## Why 106 MW of Storage Is Not Enough

The cascade page notes that 106 MW across Kulekhani I + II + III is
Nepal's **entire reservoir hydropower base** against a fleet of 3,400+ MW
— a ratio of roughly **3%**. Kulekhani I's operational data makes this
concrete.

The NEA's own Large Generation O&M Department data (page 21 of the
FY 2024/25 report) shows that seven plants totaling 465 MW generated
**2,487 GWh in FY 2081/82** — about 86% of NEA's total annual generation.
Kulekhani I's 170.56 GWh represents only **6.9%** of that large-plant
subtotal.

In dry-season months, when the RoR fleet output collapses to 25–40% of
monsoon peak, Kulekhani I is the only facility that can meaningfully
offset the shortfall. At 60 MW, it cannot close the gap alone. The
reservoir can shift water across months, but the plant's capacity puts
a hard ceiling on what shifting can accomplish. See [[storage-deficit]]
for the system-level arithmetic.

## Sedimentation — Quantified

Kulekhani I was commissioned in **December 1982** (Unit 1) and **May 1987**
(Unit 2), with a reservoir storage volume of **85.3 MCM**. Sediment
monitoring began in June 1989 and has been conducted continuously since.
Multiple post-2010 Differential GPS bathymetric surveys and RUSLE modelling
studies provide updated capacity loss figures.

### A declining rate — but irreversible accumulation

While the absolute capacity loss has deepened, the **rate of sedimentation
entering the reservoir has steadily declined** due to improved watershed
management, afforestation, and land-use changes in the upstream catchment:

| Year | Sedimentation rate (t/ha/yr) |
|---|---|
| 2002 | 13.3 |
| 2010 | 6.6 |
| 2018 | 4.8 |

This is evidence that watershed-level sediment control works — the rate has
dropped by nearly two-thirds over 16 years. But because sedimentation is
cumulative and largely irreversible without economically paralyzing dredging
operations, the **total volumetric loss has continued to deepen even as the
inflow rate slowed**. A 2021 study estimates that over its first 30 years of
operation, Kulekhani forfeited approximately **40% of its total functional
water storage capacity**.

Source: JICA Nationwide Master Plan Study on Storage-type Hydroelectric
Power Development in Nepal, Vol. 1 (2014), pp. 72–73, citing Sangroula (2007);
post-2010 DGPS bathymetric surveys and RUSLE modelling (Hydro-Lab/NTNU/NEA);
2021 reservoir capacity study (40% loss estimate).

### What this means

A **40% capacity loss over ~30 years** implies Kulekhani's effective storage
is now approximately **50–55 MCM** — well under 60% of original. The
"106 MW of storage" claim in planning documents overstates the system's
actual seasonal-shifting capability by a factor of nearly two.

This has two structural implications:

1. **Kulekhani's own future.** Every year of continued sedimentation —
   even at the declining rate — further reduces capacity. The reservoir
   will eventually reach dead storage (capacity below which intake gates
   cannot function) unless dredging or flushing interventions are deployed
   at scale. The sloping intake structure and sediment control dam (see
   Countermeasures below) have slowed but not stopped this trajectory.

2. **Template for future storage.** Tanahu (140 MW), Dudhkoshi (635 MW),
   and Budhigandaki (1,200 MW) are all proposed reservoir projects in
   catchments with comparable or higher sediment yields. The ERC's 2026
   reservoir directive mandates a 50-year design life for new storage
   projects. Kulekhani's 40% loss over 30 years suggests that without
   aggressive watershed management and sediment bypass systems from day
   one, a 50-year reservoir will lose more than half its capacity before
   its design life expires — and the cost-plus tariff written at
   commissioning will not cover the mid-life capacity that no longer
   exists. See [[sediment-as-design-constraint]] and [[storage-deficit]].

### Countermeasures deployed

Following the flood damage, Japan-supported disaster prevention projects
implemented three sediment management measures at Kulekhani:

- **Sediment control dam** constructed upstream on the Kulekhani River
  and at the river mouth in the reservoir to trap sediment before it
  reaches the main reservoir body.
- **Sloping intake structure** to prevent clogging of the intake by
  sediment and enable water withdrawal even as sedimentation levels rise.
- **Hydrosuction Sediment Removal System (HSRS)** under examination —
  uses the water head between upstream and downstream to suck sediment
  from the reservoir bottom and discharge it downstream through a pipeline.

These measures, combined with watershed afforestation, have reduced the
annual sedimentation rate from 13.3 t/ha/yr (2002) to 4.8 t/ha/yr (2018).
But the cumulative capacity loss continues — and no intervention deployed
to date has reversed it.
