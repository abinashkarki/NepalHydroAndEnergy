## Specifications (NEA FY 2081/82)

Marsyangdi is a **peaking run-of-river** plant — 4 hours of daily peaking
capacity from 69 MW of installed generation across three Francis units.
Commissioned in **1989**, it has now operated for **36 years** and
accumulated over **15,000 GWh** of lifetime generation.

## Why Marsyangdi Matters — The Aging Fleet Benchmark

Marsyangdi is one of only a handful of NEA operating plants that **actually
hits its design numbers**. At 97% of design generation (451.8 GWh against
462.5 GWh), it dramatically outperforms the median private IPP — where,
as documented in [[q-design-discharge]], zero of 100+ projects in a recent
Urja Khabar audit exceeded 80% of contracted energy.

This makes Marsyangdi a critical reference point. If a 36-year-old NEA
plant with 1980s turbine technology can achieve 97% of its design energy,
the design-energy gap at newer private IPPs cannot be explained by hydrology
alone. Something else — Q-design optimism, build-quality shortcuts,
commissioning-phase derating, or the PPA structure itself — is driving
the performance divergence. See [[q-design-discharge]] and
[[claim-mw-not-equal-value]].

The seasonal generation charts visible in the NEA FY 2024/25 report
images above confirm the pattern: even a plant that achieves 97%
annually still undergoes the classic RoR seasonal collapse — monsoon
surplus tapering to a fraction of nameplate in January–February. Marsyangdi
cannot escape [[seasonal-mismatch]], regardless of its design-performance
discipline.

## Rehabilitation on the Horizon

At 36 years old, Marsyangdi is the **second-oldest large NEA plant**
still in regular service (after Trishuli, commissioned 1967). The Unit
2 overhaul completed this fiscal year is routine — but the plant
approaches an age where major rehabilitation becomes a binary question:
invest in full electromechanical replacement or accept progressive
derating.

The parallel is [[kali-gandaki-a]] (144 MW, commissioned 2002), where
NEA floated a rehabilitation tender in 2024 to replace all three turbine
runners after 22 years. Marsyangdi, at nearly double that age on the
turbines, faces a larger decision. Every rehab of a fleet anchor plant
introduces outage risk during dry season — precisely when the
[[storage-deficit]] exposure peaks.

The overhaul data from FY 2081/82 is an early signal: the maintenance
scope is expanding. Radial gates, desander basin components, gantry
crane wire rope, shaft seals — these are not routine wear items. They
are the visible surface of an aging electromechanical fleet that NEA's
capital budget will eventually need to confront at scale.
