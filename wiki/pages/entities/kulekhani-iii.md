## Specifications (NEA FY 2081/82)

Kulekhani III is the **smallest and most downstream plant** in Nepal's
only operating reservoir cascade. At 14 MW, it sits below
[[kulekhani-ii]] (32 MW), which sits below [[kulekhani-i]] (60 MW) and
its reservoir. The water Kulekhani III generates from has already been
stored in the Kulekhani Reservoir, shifted from monsoon inflow to
dry-season dispatch, passed through Kulekhani I's turbines, collected in
a forebay, passed through Kulekhani II, and collected again before
reaching the third plant's intake.

plus 7.2 kV vacuum circuit breaker installations in both units.

## The Cascade Tail-End — Efficiency Argument

Kulekhani III's position makes it the cascade's **efficiency argument**:
the value of storage is not just in the first turbine — dispatchable
water can generate at every stage of the cascade before returning to the
natural river system. At 14 MW, the absolute contribution is small. But
the principle matters: reservoir storage is a multiplier on cascade
economics, not just a single-plant advantage.

## 117% of Target, 79% of Design — No Contradiction

In FY 2081/82, Kulekhani III generated at **117% of its internal target** —
one of the highest target-achievement ratios in the documented NEA fleet.
Against design energy, performance was approximately **79%**.

The gap between these two figures reflects deliberate conservatism in
target-setting for a tail-end cascade plant. The target accounts for the
fact that Kulekhani III's inflow is not the river directly — it is
whatever water the two upstream plants have dispatched. If Kulekhani I
draws down the reservoir deeply (as it does in dry years), Kulekhani III
gets less water. The internal target is set to reflect this dependency;
the design energy reflects what the plant *could* do if upstream
conditions were optimal year-round.

This distinguishes Kulekhani III from the [[chilime]] pattern.
Chilime overperforms because its design assumptions were conservative
relative to actual hydrology. Kulekhani III overperforms against its
**internal target** because that target builds in the upstream
dependency — not because its design energy was set too high. The two
mechanisms are different.

## Second-Order Hydrology Dependence

Kulekhani III's generation is twice removed from monsoon quality:

1. **Good monsoon** → Kulekhani Reservoir fills → KL1 dispatches freely
   in dry season → KL2 gets consistent inflow → KL3 runs at high
   utilisation
2. **Poor monsoon** → Reservoir partially fills → KL1 conserves releases →
   KL2 gets less → KL3 runs below capacity

The 64% year-on-year swing at KL1 (FY 2080/81 to 2081/82) is amplified
at the cascade tail-end. A good inflow year lifts all three plants; a
poor year may prompt KL1 to reserve water for dry-season dispatch rather
than let it flow through all three plants at full rate. The cascade
operator must balance immediate generation against storage preservation —
a real-time decision that has no equivalent in RoR operation. Kulekhani III
is simultaneously the cascade's most efficient (% of target) and most
dependent generator.

## Significance for the Storage Thesis

The three-plant cascade — 60 + 32 + 14 = 106 MW — generated 279.8 GWh
in FY 2081/82 from a single reservoir system. Compare with
[[marsyangdi]] (69 MW, pure RoR), which generates in a similar GWh range
from direct river flow. The comparison is not about total energy — it is
about **timing.** The cascade can choose when to generate; a RoR plant
cannot. At 14 MW, Kulekhani III's contribution to that dispatchability
is small but the architecture it demonstrates — reservoir → cascade →
grid — is the template every future storage proposal depends on.

The [[storage-deficit]] page frames the broader argument. Kulekhani III
is the concrete endpoint: the last turbine in Nepal's only storage
system, proving that reservoir-to-cascade generation works at every
stage — and that Nepal has built exactly one such system.
