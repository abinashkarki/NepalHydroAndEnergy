## Not a BPC Project

**Peoples Energy Limited is independent of Butwal Power Company.** A common
handoff assumption collapses the two Khimti projects into a single developer
narrative, but the ownership is entirely separate:

- **Khimti-I** (60 MW, operating since July 2000) — owned by Himal Power Limited,
  now 100% Butwal Power Company (BPC) following Statkraft and Eviny's exit in
  September 2025. See [[khimti-i]].
- **Khimti-II** (48.8 MW, under construction) — developed by Peoples Energy
  Limited, an RM Group company. **BPC holds no equity.** BPC's relevance to
  Khimti-II is limited to its presence as the Khimti-I owner on the same corridor.

The RM Group is a diversified Nepali industrial conglomerate with interests
extending beyond hydropower. PEL was established as a dedicated generation SPV
for the Khimti-II project. This ownership separation — one river, two projects,
two developers, different eras — exemplifies the fragmented IPP landscape in
Nepal's private generation sector.

## The Khimti Numbering — One River, Two Eras

The Khimti Khola hosts two distinct projects separated by a generation gap:

| | Khimti-I | Khimti-II |
|---|---|---|
| Capacity | 60 MW | 48.8 MW |
| Developer | Himal Power Limited (now BPC) | Peoples Energy Limited (RM Group) |
| PPA signed | January 15, 1996 | October 1, 2015 |
| PPA type | USD-denominated BOOT (20 yr) | NPR-denominated standard (30 yr) |
| Commissioning | July 2000 | Under construction (target mid-2024) |
| Full ownership | BPC (Nepali, post-2025) | RM Group (Nepali) |
| Historical significance | Nepal's first large private IPP | Pre-revenue IPO candidate (see below) |

The 1996 Khimti-I PPA was written on a blank slate — USD-denominated, with
no seasonal tariff split and no capacity fee structure. By the time Khimti-II's
PPA was signed in October 2015, the standard-form NPR PPA template was fully
entrenched: wet/dry differential, flat 3% × 8-year escalation, 80% take-or-pay.
Two projects on the same river, built under entirely different commercial
regimes — Khimti-I's developers were currency-hedged; Khimti-II's are not.
See [[ppa-pricing]].

## The PPA — Standard Post-2015 Structure

Khimti-II's 30-year PPA, signed October 1, 2015, is the standard post-2010
NEA template: NPR 4.80/kWh in the wet season, NPR 8.40/kWh in the dry season,
with 80% take-or-pay coverage and 3% annual escalation for the first eight
years only. The PPA provides neither currency indexation nor a demand-charge
component — exactly the structural weaknesses that the Khimti-I 1996 PPA's
USD denomination was designed to avoid.

At 75:25 debt-to-equity, Khimti-II's leverage sits on the lower end of the
Nepal IPP range (most private projects since 2015 have been at 78:22 or
higher). The 10-bank consortium led by **Sanima Bank** reflects both the
project's scale (NPR 8.65 billion total cost, ~NPR 177M/MW) and the
fragmented domestic lending landscape: no single Nepali bank can underwrite
a project above NPR ~2 billion on its own books.

## Construction — Late-Stage but Delayed

Construction reached ~50% completion by April 2023 and ~80% by mid-2024,
with the required commercial operation date (RCOD) originally set for
July 2023 and subsequently extended by one year to July 2024. The target
COD is mid-2024 (2081 BS).

Khimti-II's 6.293 km headrace tunnel — the longest single underground
structure in the project — is the critical-path item typical of Nepali
RoR construction. Three 16.267 MW vertical-axis Pelton turbines (total
48.8 MW installed) operate under a 353.7 m gross head with 16.11 m³/s
design discharge. The Pelton selection reflects the high-head, relatively
low-flow profile of the upper Khimti Khola.

## The Khimti–Dhalkebar Corridor — Shared Risk

Khimti-II evacuates through the **Khimti–Dhalkebar 220 kV double-circuit
line**, the same ~75 km corridor that carries Khimti-I (60 MW), the entire
Likhu cascade ([[likhu-1]], [[likhu-2]], [[likhu-4]], [[likhu-khola-a]] —
combined ~213 MW), and Upper Tamakoshi (456 MW). The line was originally
financed by the World Bank to evacuate Khimti-I's 60 MW and Bhote Koshi's
36 MW. By 2023 it was carrying over 300 MW of connected capacity through
the New Khimti substation.

The congestion that stranded ~40% of [[likhu-2]]'s contracted generation
in FY 2023/24 — caused by an undersized 100 MVA substation transformer —
is the defining transmission risk for every project on this corridor. The
late-2023 transformer upgrade to 300 MVA relieved the immediate bottleneck,
but a single substation failure returns all Khimti–Dhalkebar projects to
stranded-generation status. For Khimti-II, a project still under
construction, commissioning into a corridor that has already demonstrated
congestion failure is a structural risk that no PPA provision addresses.
See [[buildability]] and [[stranded-generation]].

## IPO Connection — Pre-Revenue Fundraising

Peoples Energy Limited sought approval from the Securities Board of Nepal
(SEBON) to issue an **initial public offering (IPO) during the construction
phase** — before a single kilowatt-hour had been generated. This follows
the pre-revenue IPO pattern that has become a defining feature of Nepal's
post-2015 hydropower development: promoters seek public equity to meet
the 25% equity component of 75:25 debt-equity structures, issuing shares
while the project is still a hole in the ground.

The rationale is promoter-side: IPO proceeds reduce the equity burden on
the RM Group's balance sheet and provide a secondary-market exit path before
commissioning risk has been resolved. For public investors, the trade is
buying into a project with zero operational track record, no revenue, and
incomplete construction — at a valuation set by the promoter's cost
projections. The Urja Khabar/MoEWRI audit finding that zero of 100+ small
IPPs exceeded 80% of design energy is the relevant baseline for what
public shareholders are actually buying. See [[ipo-hydropower-bailout]].

## Credit Profile

CARE Ratings Nepal assigned **CARE-NP BB** to Khimti-II's bank facilities
in June 2023, and ICRA assigned **ICRA IR BB-** in July 2024. Both ratings
sit in the speculative grade — one notch below the BBB- assigned to
[[likhu-khola-a]] and [[likhu-2]] (both operational), and above the B+
assigned to [[likhu-4]] (under construction). The ratings reflect:

- **Construction execution risk** — the project was at ~80% completion
  at the time of the ICRA rating, with the tunnel remaining the critical
  path item
- **Single-corridor dependency** — the Khimti–Dhalkebar line is shared
  with substantially larger generators that can crowd out smaller IPPs
  during congestion events
- **Standard PPA weaknesses** — no currency hedge, 3% escalation for
  8 years only, 80% take-or-pay leaving 20% exposed to NEA dispatch
  discretion
- **Mitigants** — the 75:25 gearing is conservative by Nepal standards,
  the 10-bank consortium diversifies refinancing risk, and the Pelton
  turbine configuration on a high-head site implies strong operating
  margins once commissioned
