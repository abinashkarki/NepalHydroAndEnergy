# Nalsyau Gad

Nalsyau Gad is a 410 MW storage candidate in the Karnali basin and the best dry-energy
performer in the JICA promising-storage set used by this wiki. It is pinned in both
[[data-layer-storage-shortlist]] and [[data-layer-priority-watchlist]] because it
contributes the kind of dry-season energy Nepal's run-of-river-heavy system is missing.

## Why It Matters

Nalsyau Gad's value is not just its 410 MW nameplate. The JICA/NEA storage-screening
figures give it **581.8 GWh** of dry-season energy, equal to **41.4%** of annual energy.
That is the strongest dry-energy output in the promising-storage shortlist and one of
the few project records that directly addresses the [[storage-deficit]] rather than
only adding wet-season energy.

### Shortlist Context: How Nalsyau Gad Compares

Among the JICA promising-storage candidates in the wiki, Nalsyau Gad occupies a unique
position — highest dry-energy share by a significant margin:

| Project | MW | Annual (GWh) | Dry (GWh) | Dry Share | Effective Storage (MCM) |
|---------|-----|-------------|-----------|-----------|--------------------------|
| **Nalsyau Gad** | 410 | 1,406.1 | **581.8** | **41.4%** | 296.3 |
| Kokhajor-1 | 112 | 278.9 | 94.1 | 33.7% | 166.1 |
| Madi | 200 | 621.1 | 170.7 | 27.5% | 235.1 |
| Naumure (W. Rapti) | 245 | 1,157.5 | 309.9 | 26.8% | 580.0 |
| Lower Badigad | 380 | 1,366.0 | 354.7 | 26.0% | 505.5 |
| Chera-1 | 149 | 563.2 | 120.6 | 21.4% | 141.1 |
| Andhi Khola | 180 | 648.7 | 137.1 | 21.1% | 238.7 |
| Lower Jhimruk | 143 | 454.7 | 94.4 | 20.8% | 211.6 |
| Sun Koshi No.3 | 536 | 1,883.6 | 335.9 | 17.8% | 555.0 |

What makes the 41.4% figure significant is that it approaches the dry-share range of
seasonal-storage projects with much larger reservoirs. [[kulekhani-cascade]] achieves
~50% dry share with only 106 MW. [[budhigandaki]] projects 42% at 1,200 MW with
3,300 MCM of total storage. Nalsyau Gad is delivering comparable dry-energy
concentration with less than one-tenth the reservoir volume of Budhigandaki and at a
capacity point that is commercially viable for domestic financing.

This is why it appears in the **priority watchlist** despite being survey-stage. A
project can be strategically important before it is construction-ready if it changes
the dry-season balance, especially in a system where [[kulekhani-cascade]] is still
the only operating seasonal-storage anchor and [[tanahu-hydropower]] is the only large
storage project in active construction.

### Reservoir Economics

Nalsyau Gad's **296.3 MCM** effective storage on 410 MW yields a storage-to-capacity
ratio of **0.72 MCM/MW** — lower than Naumure (2.37 MCM/MW) or Sun Koshi No.3
(1.04 MCM/MW), but the project achieves its high dry share through a different
mechanism: favourable basin hydrology in the upper Karnali catchment rather than sheer
reservoir volume. The Karnali basin receives reliable monsoon precipitation and has a
less flashy dry-season recession than eastern basins like the Koshi, meaning a
moderate storage volume can achieve proportionally more dry-season firming.

This hydrological advantage is partly why the Karnali basin dominates the JICA
shortlist: 6 of the 9 candidates (Nalsyau Gad, Lower Badigad, Naumure, Madi,
Andhi Khola, Chera-1) are Karnali-basin projects. The basin's flow regime is
intrinsically more storage-friendly.

## Status And Timing

Nalsyau Gad should be read as a **planning / promising-storage candidate**, not a
confirmed near-term build. The IPSDP milestone ladder groups **Nalsyau Gad STO** in the
**2031-2035** project window. That is a planning sequence, not a construction contract.

Near-term milestones that would need to occur:
- **Feasibility study update** — the JICA IPSDP provides screening-level data; a bankable feasibility study is the next technical gate
- **Developer assignment** — currently no developer is designated (unlike Dudhkoshi/NEA or Tanahu/NEA). VUCL (Vidhyut Utpadan Company Limited) is the most likely candidate given its mandate for Karnali-basin storage ([[mugu-karnali-storage]], [[phukot-karnali]])
- **Environmental and social screening** — the Nalsyau Gad catchment requires baseline data on resettlement footprint, downstream water-use impacts, and biodiversity
- **Financing structure** — whether this follows the NEA/AIIB sovereign model (Dudhkoshi), the VUCL/PPP model (Mugu Karnali), or a new structure
- **Transmission evacuation** — routing from the upper Karnali catchment into the western hill grid and ultimately to the 400 kV backbone. See [[nepal-transmission-landscape-2025]]

## Transmission & Grid Integration

Nalsyau Gad's location in the upper Karnali catchment presents a transmission challenge.
The western Karnali region has limited 220 kV connectivity; the nearest 400 kV
infrastructure is the proposed Karnali corridor. A 410 MW storage plant in this
location would require:
- A dedicated 220 kV transmission line from the powerhouse to the nearest substation
- Integration with the planned Karnali basin transmission backbone (linked to
  [[upper-karnali]] and [[mugu-karnali-storage]] evacuation plans)
- Possibly a collector substation serving multiple Karnali storage projects (Nalsyau
  Gad, Lower Badigad, and Madi are all in the same general catchment)

The transmission constraint is not a deal-breaker — 410 MW is within the range of
standard 220 kV double-circuit capacity — but it adds a material cost component that
the JICA screening-level cost estimates may not fully capture. Transmission costs for
western hill storage are consistently higher per km than eastern corridor equivalents.

## Geological & Site Character

The Nalsyau Gad site is in the upper reaches of the Nalsyau Gad river — a Karnali
tributary draining the southern flank of the Saipal Himal range in far-western Nepal.
The JICA screening identifies the site as having suitable dam-axis topography for a
storage reservoir with ~420 MCM total capacity, suggesting a relatively wide valley
section upstream of a constriction — the classic storage-dam site geometry.

Key geological considerations include:
- **Himalayan foothill geology** — sedimentary and metamorphic rock formations typical
  of the Lesser Himalayan zone, with potential for weak interbedded shales
- **Seismic zone 4+** — within the active Himalayan deformation front
- **Sediment load** — the Nalsyau Gad drains steep terrain; sediment management in
  the reservoir design will be critical for maintaining active storage volume over the
  project life. See [[sediment-as-design-constraint]]
- **GLOF exposure** — lower than the Dudh Koshi or Arun basins, but the upper Karnali
  has glacially-fed tributaries that require assessment

## Limitations & Open Questions

- **Survey-stage data only.** The JICA IPSDP figures are screening-level, not bankable.
  A full feasibility study could shift capacity, energy, or storage numbers materially.
- **No developer assigned.** Unlike Dudhkoshi (NEA), Tanahu (NEA), or Mugu Karnali
  (VUCL), Nalsyau Gad has no institutional sponsor moving it forward.
- **Transmission cost uncertainty.** The western hill grid is less developed than the
  eastern and central corridors. Evacuation from a remote Karnali tributary adds cost.
- **Competing Karnali priorities.** The Karnali basin has multiple storage candidates
  vying for limited development bandwidth: Mugu Karnali (1,902 MW, pre-construction),
  Phukot Karnali (426 MW, survey), Upper Karnali (900 MW, stalled), Karnali Chisapani
  (10,800 MW, conceptual). Nalsyau Gad must compete for developer attention,
  financing, and transmission capacity with larger projects.
- **Downstream water-use impacts.** The Nalsyau Gad feeds the Karnali main stem; flow
  alteration could affect downstream ecosystems and, potentially, cross-border flows
  into India's Ghaghara system. See [[hydro-geopolitics]].
