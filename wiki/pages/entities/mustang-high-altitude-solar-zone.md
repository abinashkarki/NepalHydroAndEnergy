# Mustang / Trans-Himalayan High-Altitude Solar Zone

Not a project, an **entity classification** of the geographic zone with Nepal's best solar resource. Covers the trans-Himalayan rain-shadow valleys of **Mustang, Dolpa, upper Manang, upper Humla, Mugu (upper), and parts of Jumla** — a band of high-elevation (2,500–4,500 m) valleys shielded from the monsoon by the Greater Himalaya.

This zone has **world-class GHI and DNI** (5.8–6.5 kWh/m²/day, 6.0–6.8 kWh/m²/day respectively) with ~260–290 clear-sky days per year. It is also, for the foreseeable future, the **hardest solar zone in Nepal to monetise** because of grid distance, low local demand, and terrain-induced transmission costs.

## Why the zone is strategically interesting

Three genuine reasons, despite the grid-distance challenge:

### 1. It is the only Nepal zone where *winter* is the best solar month.

Winter clear-sky days are more numerous than summer clear-sky days here (summer can have valley-mist + high-altitude storms; winter is cloudless). The complementarity argument in [[solar-hydro-complementarity]] is strongest here: Zone A in winter generates ~110% of its April peak, exactly when Nepal's hydro fleet is at its weakest.

### 2. Off-grid solar + BESS at these sites has extraordinary kWh-value.

Incumbent energy source is mostly: (a) no electricity at all, (b) diesel generation at NPR 60–90/kWh, (c) firewood for heating and cooking. Displacing (b) makes Zone A solar the highest-value per-kWh energy investment in Nepal — 10–15× the utility-tariff-equivalent value.

### 3. It is the long-dated solar export corridor.

The [[ratmate-rasuwagadhi-kerung-400kv]] line, if completed, opens a trans-Himalayan interconnection to Tibet. Zone A solar, transmitted northward, becomes relevant to China's western-grid imports. This is a 15–20 year story, not a near-term strategy, but Nepal's **long-dated Zone A solar optionality** is real and should be preserved through conservation-compatible land zoning.

## Near-term constraints

- **Grid distance:** Nearest 132 kV substation is typically 100–200 km away. Terrain multiplies per-km transmission cost 3–5×.
- **Local demand:** Mustang's total population ~14,000; Dolpa's ~37,000. Grid-scale off-take is absent.
- **Land tenure:** Much of upper Mustang and Dolpa is inside Annapurna Conservation Area and Shey Phoksundo National Park. Utility-scale ground-mount is largely excluded.
- **Access:** Road connectivity seasonal; airlift-only sites in winter.
- **Construction:** Cold-climate installation window is April–October only.

## What Zone A is *for*, realistically

In order of near-term deployability:

1. **Off-grid + BESS for remote institutional and community sites** — the [[karnali-solar-energy-project]] template, extended to Mustang and Dolpa.
2. **Diesel-displacement at trekking lodges and research stations** — 5–50 kWp each, high unit economics.
3. **Pilot research sites** for high-altitude PV degradation / snow-load / bifacial performance — valuable to global solar R&D, underrated in Nepal.
4. **Long-dated grid export optionality** via trans-Himalayan interconnection.

Utility-scale GW-class solar in Mustang is not a 2030 project. It is a 2040 project and the value is in preserving the land zoning and interconnection optionality now.
