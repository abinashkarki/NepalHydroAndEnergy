# Upper Marsyangdi A

Upper Marsyangdi A is a **50 MW operating run-of-river** plant on the
Marsyangdi River in Lamjung District — the **upstream anchor** of the
170 MW Marsyangdi cascade. Developed by Sinohydro-Sagarmatha Power Company
(a Sino-Nepali joint venture), the plant signed its PPA in 2010 and
commissioned around 2016–2017, making it one of the earlier private IPP
projects on the Marsyangdi system.

## The Marsyangdi Cascade — Upstream Anchor

Upper Marsyangdi A sits at the head of one of Nepal's most productive
hydropower corridors. The Marsyangdi River, originating in the Annapurna
massif near Manang, hosts three projects in sequence:

| Plant | MW | Status | Position |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Upper Marsyangdi A** | **50** | **Operating** | **Upstream** |
| [[madhya-marsyangdi]] | 70 | Operating | Middle |
| Marsyangdi Besi | 50 | Under construction | Downstream |
| **Total** | **170** | | |

As the uppermost project, Upper Marsyangdi A's operations — particularly
sediment management at its intake and flow diversion through the headrace —
directly affect water quality and discharge timing for the downstream
plants. This is the cascade dependency that Nepal's hydropower planning
rarely accounts for in project-level PPA economics: the upstream plant's
design discharge determines how much the downstream plant receives, and
the downstream PPA cannot charge back for upstream externalities.

## Learning Curve Asset

Upper Marsyangdi A serves as a benchmark for the cost and performance
assumptions embedded in newer Marsyangdi developments. Because it
commissioned approximately 7–8 years before Marsyangdi Besi (the
downstream under-construction plant), its operational history should
inform the financial models used to justify the downstream project.

Key questions a full analysis would answer:

1. **Build cost vs budget.** The Sinohydro-Sagarmatha joint venture used
   a Chinese contractor model. The actual cost per MW — and how it
   compared with the NPR 200–300 million/MW industry-average range at
   the time — would calibrate the cost assumptions for Marsyangdi Besi.

2. **PLF vs contracted energy.** Madhya Marsyangdi achieves 107.6% of
   design from the same river system. If Upper Marsyangdi A performs
   similarly, the Marsyangdi basin proves to be systematically
   under-designed relative to actual hydrology — the [[chilime]]
   pattern at cascade scale.

3. **Maintenance cost trajectory.** As the plant approaches 10 years of
   operation, the maintenance cost curve — routine overhauls, sediment
   wear on turbine runners, desander basin cleaning frequency — becomes
   a real dataset for predicting lifecycle costs on the same river.

4. **Sediment management.** Upstream plants on steep Himalayan tributaries
   handle the highest sediment loads. How Upper Marsyangdi A manages its
   intake desander — and what passes through to the headrace — determines
   turbine wear at both this plant and Madhya Marsyangdi downstream.

The NEA annual reports cover NEA-owned plants in detail but do not publish
private IPP generation data. Upper Marsyangdi A's actual performance data
would need to come from the developer's annual reports, ICRA Nepal
surveillance ratings, or the DoED generation monitoring database.

## Why the Upstream Plant Matters to the Cascade

When Upper Marsyangdi A diverts river flow through its headrace tunnel,
it reduces the flow available to Madhya Marsyangdi's intake downstream.
If the upstream plant's design discharge is close to the river's dry-season
flow — and both plants are running at full peaking capacity — the downstream
plant may face water scarcity in low-flow months. This is a regulatory
problem, not just an engineering one: Nepal's water allocation framework
does not cascade-bind PPA obligations, so the downstream developer has no
contractual remedy against reduced inflow from upstream operations.

This makes Upper Marsyangdi A a case study in why the Marsyangdi basin —
and Nepal's cascade-heavy river systems generally — needs integrated
water accounting rather than project-level licensing. See [[gandaki-basin]]
and [[seasonal-mismatch]].
