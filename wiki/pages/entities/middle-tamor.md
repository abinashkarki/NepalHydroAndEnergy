## The Stranded Asset — Transmission Before Generation

Middle Tamor is explicitly named in the Urja Khabar/MoEWRI report as one of
18 Nepali hydropower projects whose financial viability has been crippled by
state-side transmission failures. The wording is stark: **"Stranded pending the
completion of the Dhunge-Sanghu Substation."**

The Basantapur–Dhungesanghu 220 kV transmission line was built specifically to
evacuate Middle Tamor's 73 MW. Its 127 towers were erected across eastern Nepal's
difficult terrain, and the first circuit was strung by April 2024. The Dhungesanghu
substation was finally commissioned on July 6, 2025. But the project had already
been waiting for years — generation capacity approved, financed, and built before
the evacuation infrastructure that would make it useful.

This is the classic Nepal hydropower timing failure that the [[buildability]] and
[[stranded-generation]] pages document: private developers mobilise capital and
construction on schedule, only to discover that the public-sector transmission
backbone — promised in the same licensing window — is years behind. The financiers
who underwrote NPR 7.3 billion in project debt did not underwrite the Nepal
Electricity Authority's substation construction programme.

## The Tamor Basin — Eastern Frontier

The Tamor River drains the eastern flank of the Koshi basin, rising near the
Kanchenjunga massif and flowing south through Taplejung district before joining
the Sun Koshi to form the Sapta Koshi. Unlike the crowded Tamakoshi, Khimti, and
Likhu corridors to the west — where multiple IPPs compete for the same
transmission capacity — the Tamor basin is relatively undeveloped.

Middle Tamor is one of the first large projects in the basin. Its 73 MW places
it at a scale comparable to [[likhu-2]] (55 MW) and [[likhu-1]] (77 MW), but
without the benefit of shared corridor infrastructure or the clustering
advantages that the MV Dugar Group exploited in the Likhu Cascade. The Tamor's
isolation cuts both ways: fewer competing offtakers on the line, but no
neighbouring plants to share the political and financial burden of pushing
transmission through.

## Sanima Group — Portfolio Developer

Sanima Middle Tamor Hydropower Ltd (formerly Tamor Sanima Energy Pvt. Ltd.) is
the project SPV, backed by the Sanima Group — one of Nepal's largest private
hydropower developers. Sanima's growing portfolio includes [[sanima-mai]]
(22 MW), which has published Q40 design discharge data used in the
[[q-design-discharge]] analysis.

The group's strategy mirrors the cluster-development approach seen elsewhere in
Nepali private hydro: establish a track record with a smaller project (Sanima Mai),
then scale to larger capacity in a new basin (Middle Tamor). The corporate
continuity — same developer, same financing relationships, same regulatory
experience — reduces execution risk. But it does not solve the transmission
problem. Sanima can build a power station; it cannot build a substation on
land it does not own, using a budget it does not control.

## The Transmission Problem

The Basantapur–Dhungesanghu 220 kV line that will evacuate Middle Tamor's
generation is a single-circuit corridor through eastern Nepal's steepest
topography. 127 towers crossing landslide-prone terrain create single-point
failure risks that the Likhu Cascade's [[khimti-dhalkebar-corridor]] also
faces — but with less redundancy and no neighbouring plants to share the outage
costs.

For the full structural analysis of how transmission planning failures create
systemic IPP risk, see [[buildability]]. For the growing inventory of projects
that commissioned into a grid that could not take their power, see
[[stranded-generation]].
