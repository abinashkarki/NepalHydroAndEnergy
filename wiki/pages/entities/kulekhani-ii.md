## Specifications (NEA FY 2081/82)

Kulekhani II is a **32 MW cascade plant** commissioned in **1986** —
the second link in Nepal's only operating reservoir hydropower system.
It uses tailrace discharge from [[kulekhani-i]] (60 MW, reservoir),
supplemented by flow from the Mandu River and the Rapti Pumping Station,
to generate peaking power when the central grid needs it most.

Each year, the Mandu intake is cleaned post-monsoon and the Rapti
Pumping Station is maintained — operated as needed during the dry
season to augment generation. In FY 2081/82, KL2 completed 7.2 kV
VCB switchgear work, transformer oil filtration, replacement of a
damaged 600 mm² conductor, and civil works at both intakes.

## The Cascade's Middle Link

The Kulekhani system operates as a sequential cascade: KL1 releases
from the reservoir, KL2 generates from the tailrace plus supplementary
sources, and KL3 (14 MW) uses the regulated flow from KL2 combined
with Khani Khola natural flow. The system is coherent — KL1's dispatch
decisions determine what KL2 and KL3 receive.

KL2's **56% year-over-year increase** mirrors KL1's 64.5% rise — both
driven by the same monsoon-filling event. This coordination makes the
cascade efficient when water is abundant but also concentrated: in a
drought year, the entire cascade contracts together, since the
bottleneck is reservoir fill, not turbine capacity at any single plant.

The combined cascade design is 356.5 GWh/yr (KL1: 211 + KL2: 104.6 +
KL3: 40.85). FY 2081/82 combined actual: 279.8 GWh — **78.5% of
design**. This is the hydrology-dependence in practice. The reservoir
allows seasonal shifting but cannot create water; when the monsoon
underperforms, the cascade underperforms as a unit.

## Aging Infrastructure in a Critical Role

At **39 years old** (commissioned 1986), Kulekhani II is among the
oldest NEA plants still in regular service. The FY 2081/82 maintenance
scope — VCB switchgear replacement, conductor repair, transformer oil
filtration — reflects a plant in its fourth decade where electrical
infrastructure becomes the primary maintenance driver.

The cascade's strategic importance magnifies the aging concern. If KL1
requires a major dam or turbine overhaul, KL2's generation suspends
automatically — it has no independent water source. Both plants are
vintage, both have NEA as sole owner/operator, and both are essential
to the dry-season grid. There is no redundancy. See [[storage-deficit]].
