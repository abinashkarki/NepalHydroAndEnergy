## The PPA That Broke the Template

Khimti-I was built under a Power Purchase Agreement signed on **January 15, 1996** —
before Nepal had a standardised PPA regime, before the Electricity Regulation
Commission existed, and before the NPR 4.80/8.40 wet/dry tariff structure that now
governs every new private IPP. Himal Power Limited (HPL) negotiated on a blank
slate.

The result was structurally different from anything signed since:

| Parameter | Khimti-I (1996 PPA) | Standard IPP PPA (post-2010) |
|---|---|---|
| Tariff | Initially US$0.052/kWh → US$0.059/kWh | NPR 4.80/kWh wet, NPR 8.40/kWh dry |
| Currency | **USD-denominated** | NPR |
| Escalation | US CPI index | ~3% annual (first 8 years only) |
| Seasonal split | None (flat take-or-pay demand charge) | Yes (wet/dry differential) |
| PPA structure | BOOT — 50% equity to NEA for NPR 1 at year 20 | Standard PPA, no equity transfer |
| Term | 20 years | 25–30 years |

The USD denomination was the critical difference. As the NPR depreciated over two
decades against the dollar, Khimti-I's effective rupee tariff rose passively — without
any renegotiation. During the same period, projects signed under the NPR regime
watched their real tariffs erode with inflation. Khimti-I's developers were protected
from exactly the currency risk that makes Nepal's standard PPA regime structurally
inadequate for long-tenor debt. See [[ppa-pricing]].

## Generation — 103% of Contracted Over 25 Years

HPL's contracted energy is approximately **350 GWh/year**. Over the 25 years from
commissioning (2000) to 2025, total generation reached approximately **9,000 GWh** —
an average of **~360 GWh/year**, or **~103% of contracted energy**.

| Metric | Value |
|---|---|
| Design (contracted) annual energy | ~350 GWh/yr |
| Actual total (2000–2025) | ~9,000 GWh |
| Average annual actual | ~360 GWh/yr |
| Average % of contracted | ~103% |

Source: HPL official project data; Statkraft press release (2025).

This places Khimti-I in a category occupied by very few Nepali private IPPs: a plant
that has met or exceeded contracted energy over a multi-decade operating life. The
Urja Khabar/MoEWRI audit finding — that zero of 100+ small IPPs exceeded 80% of
design energy — does not apply to Khimti-I, a larger plant on a more reliable river
system in a different generation era. The Khimti Khola, a Sun Koshi tributary in
Dolakha, appears to have been accurately hydrologically characterized for the 1996
design study. See [[q-design-discharge]].

## Ownership — Repatriation in September 2025

HPL was founded in 1993 by Butwal Power Company (Nepal) alongside Norwegian
partners. Until September 2025, the ownership structure was:

| Shareholder | Stake (pre-2025) | Stake (post-2025) |
|---|---|---|
| Statkraft SF (Norway) | ~57% | 0% |
| Eviny AS (Norway) | ~26% | 0% |
| Butwal Power Company (Nepal) | ~17% | **100%** |

In **September 2025**, Statkraft and Eviny sold all shares to BPC. Himal Power
Limited is now fully owned by Butwal Power Company — a Nepali state-adjacent
entity. The repatriation has a structural implication: the original high-tariff USD PPA
that made the project commercially viable for Norwegian investors expired in July 2020.
BPC acquired a **25-year-old, debt-free, cash-generating asset** at whatever price
Statkraft and Eviny accepted to exit.

Whether BPC is now operating under the original PPA terms, a renegotiated successor
PPA, or a new standard-form agreement is not confirmed in public sources. Given that
the BOOT period ended in 2020 with NEA acquiring 50% equity for NPR 1, and BPC
subsequently acquired the Norwegian stakes, the current commercial structure of HPL
is materially different from the 1996 BOOT arrangement.

## The Khimti–Dhalkebar Line — Corridor Consequences

Khimti-I's output is evacuated via the **Khimti–Dhalkebar 220 kV double-circuit
line** (~75 km), financed by the World Bank and commissioned in 2016. This line was
built specifically to evacuate Khimti's 60 MW (and Bhote Koshi's 36 MW) to eastern
Nepal's load centres and the Dhalkebar interconnect with India.

By 2023, this corridor had become critically congested. The rapid commissioning of
the Likhu cascade (Likhu-1, Likhu-2, Likhu-4, Likhu Khola A — combined ~213 MW),
all routing through the New Khimti substation at the head of the Khimti–Dhalkebar
line, saturated the corridor. Likhu-2 alone lost ~40% of its contracted generation
in FY 2023/24 to stranded generation because the substation transformer was
undersized. The line that was built for Khimti-I's 60 MW was trying to carry over
300 MW. See [[buildability]] for the broader grid-planning failure pattern.

## Historical Significance

Khimti-I is the **origin point of Nepal's private hydropower sector**. Every
subsequent IPP PPA — every take-or-pay clause, every AD penalty formula, every
wet/dry tariff differential — was written in the shadow of the 1996 Khimti PPA
and the political controversy it generated. Critics argued that the USD-denominated
take-or-pay structure was too favourable to foreign developers; supporters argued it
was the only way to attract capital into a country with no IPP track record.

Both arguments were partially correct. Khimti-I delivered: 9,000 GWh over 25 years,
103% of contracted energy, zero recorded AD penalty disputes in public sources.
What the post-Khimti standard PPA regime corrected — USD denomination, take-or-pay
across the full contract, no seasonal rate differential — it may have over-corrected.
The current NPR flat-rate structure with 3% escalation for eight years leaves projects
exposed to exactly the long-tenor financial risks that Khimti's currency indexing
and demand-charge structure protected against. See [[ppa-pricing]].
