# Khimti–Dhalkebar 220 kV Corridor

The original "IPP-to-grid" line in Nepal. Built to evacuate **[[khimti-i]]** —
the country's first large IPP (1996 PPA, 2000 commissioning) — toward
the central grid at **Dhalkebar**. Its history mirrors the birth of
private hydropower in Nepal and the long legal and political battles
that surround it.

## Significance

- **First line built for an IPP.** Before [[khimti-i]], Nepal had no
  practice of building high-voltage lines specifically to evacuate
  independent power producers — the Khimti–Dhalkebar project was the
  template.
- **Legal precedent.** The project's land-acquisition lawsuits (2011–15)
  set Nepali case law on the public-interest use of private land for
  transmission.
- **Feeds the export funnel.** Terminates at Dhalkebar, which is also
  the origin of [[hetauda-dhalkebar-inaruwa-backbone]] and
  [[dhalkebar-muzaffarpur]]. Roughly half of Nepal's exportable
  hydropower still routes through it.

## Limitations & Open Questions

- **Capacity is effectively full.** Contracted flow from Khimti-I +
  downstream IPPs exceeds 300 MW in peak monsoon; a second 220 kV
  or 400 kV reinforcement from the Tamakoshi–Khimti cluster has been
  studied but not funded.
- **Sediment disruption risk.** The corridor crosses steep Sindhupalchok
  and Dolakha terrain; landslide damage to tower foundations was
  flagged in the 2023 monsoon assessment.
