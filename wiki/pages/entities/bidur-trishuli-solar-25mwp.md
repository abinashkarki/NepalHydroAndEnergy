# Bidur / Trishuli 25 MWp Solar Farm

Nepal's **first utility-scale NEA-EPC, NEA-owned, NEA-operated solar plant.** 25 MWp ground-mounted grid-tied PV at Bidur, Nuwakot district, commissioned 2022 under the World Bank Grid Solar and Energy Efficiency Project. Interconnected to the Trishuli 33 kV grid.

The plant that moved Nepal from "solar demonstration" to "utility solar." Every subsequent NEA solar programme decision rides on the engineering, procurement, operational, and commercial precedents this project established.

## Institutional firsts

1. **First NEA-EPC utility solar.** Broke the "NEA can't build anything beyond hydro" narrative.
2. **First use of the now-standard NEA utility-solar PPA / O&M template.** Subsequent IPP plants use descendant documentation.
3. **First integration of a utility solar plant into NEA's operational dispatch / Load Dispatch Center.** The forecasting and integration protocols piloted here.
4. **First competitive solar EPC tender at utility scale.** Established the procurement reference for Phase IV.

## Why Bidur/Trishuli sited at Nuwakot is strategically interesting

The site choice is a **near-hydro co-location precedent without the explicit "hybrid" label.** Trishuli hydropower station is ~5 km upstream; the Trishuli 33 kV substation into which Bidur/Trishuli feeds is the same substation used by multiple Trishuli-corridor hydropower projects.

This is an early example of [[hybrid-siting-logic]]'s Archetype 1 — **shared interconnection between solar and hydro** — even though Bidur/Trishuli was not tendered or designed as an explicit hybrid. The economics worked because the land was available, the grid was already there, and the terrain was workable.

## Site characteristics

| Parameter | Value |
|---|---|
| GHI (measured, nearest station class) | ~4.5 kWh/m²/day |
| Specific yield (actual) | ~1,600 kWh/kWp/yr |
| Zone ([[solar-resource-geography-nepal]]) | Zone C2 — Pokhara / Gandaki mid-hills edge |
| Monsoon cloud penalty | ~30% (mid-hill location, moderate) |
| Winter GHI | ~4.0 kWh/m²/day |

Nuwakot's mid-hill location is *not* Nepal's best solar zone — Zone B Terai would produce ~10–15% more per MWp. The site choice reflects **transmission-adjacency priority** (Trishuli 33 kV proximity) over **pure GHI optimisation**. The decision is instructive: for Nepal, grid-adjacency beats resource-optimisation in siting priority, which aligns with the [[hybrid-siting-logic]] general rule.

## Operational performance (FY 2023/24 + FY 2024/25)

- **Expected specific yield:** 1,600 kWh/kWp/yr (design)
- **Actual specific yield:** ~1,520–1,680 kWh/kWp/yr (within ±5% of design)
- **Availability:** high; no recorded extended outages in FY 2023/24
- **O&M:** NEA in-house, with WB Grid Solar & EE Project technical assistance during initial years
- **Monsoon degradation risk:** moderate; 2023 monsoon cycle included no damage events

## What this project teaches

Three lessons forward:

### 1. NEA can build solar on-schedule, on-budget, on-spec.

Bidur/Trishuli was delivered within the planned window and within the WB-project cost envelope. The "NEA procurement takes forever" critique (accurate for large hydro) was not validated here. Utility solar is fundamentally easier to deliver than hydropower; lead times of 3–5 years for NEA-EPC and 2–3 years for IPP are achievable.

### 2. Near-hydro siting works operationally.

Shared substation utilisation, shared access road, shared O&M community, no measurable operational interference between hydro and PV. Validates the [[hybrid-siting-logic]] Archetype 1 thesis at 25 MWp scale; should be replicated at every operating hydropower site with ≥1 ha of adjacent buildable land.

### 3. Mid-hill solar is workable but sub-optimal.

The ~30% monsoon penalty means a Zone C plant like Bidur/Trishuli produces ~10% less annual energy per MWp than a Zone B Terai plant of the same design. For new-build, Terai should be first priority. Bidur/Trishuli's site choice is defensible in hindsight because of the grid-adjacency benefit, but the default for the 960 MW tender is correctly Terai.
