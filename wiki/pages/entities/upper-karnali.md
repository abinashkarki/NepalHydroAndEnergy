# Upper Karnali

A 900 MW run-of-river scheme on the Karnali, licensed to India's GMR Group since 2008 and **still in survey / pre-construction 18 years later**. Upper Karnali is the canonical example of an India-developed Nepali hydropower project that has not been built: repeatedly extended PDA deadlines, stalled financial closure, a Supreme Court that finally refused open-ended extensions, and a Bangladesh export PPA that has never actually closed. In 2026 the government cleared a new path for financial closure — the first credible forward motion in more than a decade.

### Engineering
Upper Karnali is a run-of-river diversion scheme on the Karnali main stem — the same engineering archetype as [[arun-3]] but in the western Karnali basin. At 900 MW, it would be the second-largest RoR plant in Nepal after Arun-3. The project's engineering challenge is not the head or flow regime (the Karnali has reliable hydrology) but the transmission distance: 900 MW from the mid-Karnali to the Dhalkebar-Muzaffarpur export corridor requires a new 400 kV transmission spine through western Nepal.

### Output
Design annual energy of ~3,466 GWh with ~88% planned for export — primarily to India, with a Bangladesh PPA live since 2017 (quoted figures ranging from 200–500 MW). This is the canonical export-first model that [[arun-3]] is executing and Upper Karnali has not.

The Bangladesh component makes Upper Karnali the test case for Nepal's "second neighbour" trade strategy — but no PPA has yet closed. The Bangladesh figures have shifted repeatedly: 250 MW, 300 MW, 500 MW, and 200 MW have all been quoted at different moments between 2017 and 2025. A moving target is not a market — it is a signal that the tri-nation transmission architecture (Nepal → India → Bangladesh) remains unresolved.

### Governance
The equity stack tells the project's evolution: GMR (original 2008 sponsor), plus SJVN, IREDA, and NEA — Indian state entities stepping into a project the original private sponsor could not alone finance. This is a structural change in what "India-developed" means in Nepal. See [[hydro-geopolitics]].

The 2008 international competitive bidding awarded the project to GMR under a Project Development Agreement (PDA) — a bilateral contract between the Government of Nepal and the developer that gave GMR exclusive development rights with defined milestones. GMR failed to meet the financial closure deadline; the government extended it repeatedly. Each extension eroded the credibility of the PDA model: if deadlines are routinely extended, they are not deadlines.

## The GMR Saga: Timeline of an Unbuilt Project

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| **2008** | GMR wins international competitive bidding for 900 MW Upper Karnali; PDA signed with Government of Nepal |
| **2008–2014** | GMR undertakes survey and feasibility studies; financial closure targeted for ~2014 |
| **2014** | High-level committee raises concerns about downstream irrigation impacts on India's Ganga system; objection stalls progress |
| **2015–2017** | PDA deadlines extended; GMR begins discussions with Bangladesh for export PPA |
| **2017** | Bangladesh PPA announced — initially 500 MW, later adjusted to 250–300 MW range |
| **2018–2020** | Further PDA extensions; financial closure repeatedly deferred |
| **2020–2022** | COVID-19 disruptions; project enters "force majeure" discussions |
| **2023–2024** | Supreme Court of Nepal intervenes: rules that open-ended PDA extensions are not permissible; grants only 186-day allowance for time lost under court's own interim orders |
| **2025** | SJVN and IREDA join equity stack alongside GMR; NEA takes a stake |
| **2026** | Government clears path for revised financial closure; first credible forward motion in a decade |

This timeline illustrates the structural problem with the PDA model for large Nepal-India projects. The developer (GMR) cannot finance the project without a bankable PPA; the PPA cannot be finalized without transmission access; the transmission requires coordinated investment from Nepal and India; and none of this can happen while the PDA deadlines are in legal limbo. The 2026 restructuring — adding SJVN, IREDA, and NEA to the equity stack — is an attempt to break this deadlock by bringing in entities with the balance-sheet strength and political capital to close the financing.

## The Supreme Court Intervention

The Supreme Court's ruling is significant beyond Upper Karnali. By limiting extensions to 186 days (for time lost under the court's own interim orders), the court signaled that the PDA-extension cycle — where projects are awarded, deadlines are missed, extensions are granted, and nothing is built — is not legally sustainable. This has implications for:
- **Other stalled PDAs:** [[west-seti]] (750 MW, NHPC), [[arun-3]] (already executing, less relevant)
- **Future licensing:** the Investment Board Nepal's PDA framework may need tighter milestone enforcement
- **Investor confidence:** the ruling cuts both ways — it disciplines developers to meet deadlines, but also makes Nepal a riskier jurisdiction if deadlines are seen as infeasible

## The Bangladesh Question

Upper Karnali's Bangladesh PPA has been "live" since 2017 but has never closed. The underlying problem is not the commercial terms — Bangladesh needs power and Nepal has it — but the **transmission architecture**. Exporting from Nepal to Bangladesh requires wheeling power through India's grid, which requires:
1. A Nepal-India cross-border transmission agreement (Upper Karnali → Muzaffarpur or equivalent)
2. An India-Bangladesh cross-border transmission agreement (existing Baharampur–Bheramara link, 1,000 MW, operational since 2013)
3. A tri-nation wheeling agreement (Nepal → India → Bangladesh) — no precedent exists at this scale

The India-Bangladesh link exists, but the Nepal-India link for Upper Karnali does not. Until the 400 kV Karnali corridor is built from the project site to the Indian border, the Bangladesh PPA is a promise without a delivery mechanism. See [[nepal-transmission-landscape-2025]].

## Significance

Upper Karnali is the twin case to [[arun-3]] — same licence era, similar capacity, same India-developer / export-first model — but with the opposite outcome. Arun-3 is at 74% physical progress; Upper Karnali has been at "about to reach financial closure" for a decade. Reading the two projects together is how you understand the variance in Nepal's India-developed export class: the contract is not the product, execution is.

The project also marks how [[hydro-geopolitics]] has widened in Nepal's hydropower conversation. The original export target was India, but a Bangladesh PPA has been live in some form since 2017 — making Upper Karnali the test case for Nepal's tri-nation energy trade ambition.

Finally, the 2026 financing stack — IREDA, SJVN and Nepali banks stepping in alongside GMR — is a pattern worth noting: Indian state-owned entities are now effectively taking equity in Nepali projects their original sponsor could not alone finance. That is a structural change in what "India-developed" means, and Upper Karnali is the first at-scale instance.

## Limitations & Controversies

**Eighteen years without a turbine.** The 2008 licence award predates the current [[nea]] grid code in some dimensions; deadlines have been extended repeatedly. The Supreme Court ultimately ruled against open-ended extensions, granting only a 186-day allowance for time lost under its own interim orders — a signal that the judiciary no longer accepts the PDA-extension cycle as routine.

**Bangladesh transmission dependency.** Even if a final PPA closes, the 200–500 MW export to Bangladesh depends on a Nepal–India–Bangladesh transmission corridor that is still under development. Upper Karnali's export model contains an unresolved infrastructure prerequisite that is not on its balance sheet but is on its critical path. The India-Bangladesh Baharampur–Bheramara link exists, but the Nepal-India segment does not.

**Downstream irrigation overhang.** A 2014 high-level committee raised concerns about impacts on downstream irrigation in India's Ganga system. That objection was never fully resolved, only procedurally managed, and resurfaces in each PDA extension debate. The concern is structural: 900 MW of Karnali diversion reduces flow in the Ghaghara, which is a major irrigation source for Uttar Pradesh.

**GMR Group's execution posture.** Unlike SJVN, GMR is a private Indian conglomerate whose project pipeline has shifted substantially since 2008. GMR's energy portfolio now includes domestic Indian thermal and renewable projects, airport infrastructure, and highways — hydropower in Nepal is one of many competing capital allocations. Whether GMR remains the right primary sponsor — now that SJVN and IREDA are in the equity stack — is an open strategic question that Nepal has not formally asked.

**Transmission gap.** The 400 kV Karnali corridor from the project site to the Dhalkebar-Muzaffarpur export point does not exist. Building it requires coordinated investment from Nepal and India across some of Nepal's most rugged terrain. Until this corridor is committed, Upper Karnali is [[stranded-generation]].
