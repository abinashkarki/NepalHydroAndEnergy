## The Cascade Leader

Likhu-1 is the largest plant in the 213 MW Likhu cascade, and the only one
with a **better-than-standard PPA structure**: 80% of contracted energy on
take-or-pay terms (versus 75% at [[likhu-2]]). This means the developer
carries less revenue risk from NEA curtailment — a meaningful protection
in a cascade where the New Khimti substation bottleneck stranded 40% of
Likhu-2's generation in FY 2023/24.

The plant diverts 26.4 m³/s through a 5.25 m weir and a 6.57 km headrace
tunnel to a surface powerhouse equipped with horizontal Francis turbines
from Andritz Hydro. At 77 MW, it is the largest plant in the cascade and the
second-largest private RoR IPP in the Likhu basin after the MV Dugar Group's
77 MW completed this project while simultaneously developing Likhu-2 and
Likhu Khola A — a cluster investment model that concentrated three SPVs
under shared equity and shared construction management.

## Financial Recovery

Likhu-1's credit trajectory tells the same transmission-bottleneck story as
[[likhu-2]], but from a better starting position:

- **FY 2023/24** (commissioning year): constrained by the same New Khimti
  substation bottleneck that limited the entire cascade. CARE Nepal assigned
  a **BB** rating.
- **FY 2024/25**: following the 200 MVA transformer upgrade, the corridor
  unblocked. Operating income reached **NPR 1,060 million**. CARE upgraded
  the rating to **BB+** in March 2025.

The BB+ rating is below Likhu-2's BBB- — reflecting higher leverage or
lower debt service coverage. But the upward trajectory mirrors the cascade
pattern: the asset performs when the grid permits.

## The MV Dugar FDI Model

Likhu-1, Likhu-2, and Likhu Khola A share a common equity architecture:
the **MV Dugar Group** (a Nepali conglomerate) paired with **Mogha Energy
DMCC**, a UAE-based investment firm. This FDI structure — a domestic group
clustering multiple projects under shared foreign capital — is a departure
from Nepal's traditional single-project domestic bank consortium model.

Butwal Power Company (BPC) holds **no equity** in any cascade plant. The
market misattribution (BPC as cascade developer) persists because BPC's
subsidiary HCEL provided engineering consultancy during construction. The
actual equity is MV Dugar + UAE FDI.

For the UAE investors, the Likhu cluster offers a risk-diversified entry:
three projects (77 + 55 + 29 = 161 MW cumulative under Dugar control) share
the same river basin, the same transmission corridor, and the same regulatory
regime — but operate as separate SPVs with separate PPAs and separate debt.
If one underperforms, the other two are not legally affected. This
structuring is standard for the Dugar approach and represents a model other
Nepali conglomerates could replicate.

## Transmission Dependency — The Single Corridor Risk

All four cascade plants share **one evacuation route**: the New Khimti
substation into the Khimti–Dhalkebar 220 kV line. When the substation had
only a 100 MVA transformer, the entire 213 MW cascade competed for
approximately half its rated corridor capacity. The 200 MVA upgrade (late
2023) resolved the immediate bottleneck — but the structural vulnerability
remains: a single substation fire, 220 kV line fault, or transformer failure
returns the entire cascade to stranded-generation status.

Following the upgrade, NEA secured Central Electricity Authority (CEA)
approval to wheel power from Likhu-2 and Likhu-4 across the Dhalkebar–
Muzaffarpur 400 kV interconnector into the Indian day-ahead market. This
transformed the same corridor that caused stranded generation in 2023 into
a dual-market export pathway — domestic dispatch in dry season, Indian
export in monsoon surplus. See [[buildability]].
