## From Export Megaproject to Domestic Afterthought — A Regulatory Case Study

Likhu-4 was originally conceived as a **120 MW export-oriented** hydropower
project, intended to wheel power directly to India across the Dhalkebar–
Muzaffarpur cross-border interconnector. A dispute over **cross-border
transmission wheeling charges** — the tariff India would charge for
conveying Nepali-generated power across its grid to Indian buyers — caused
Green Ventures Ltd (GVL) to abandon the export model entirely.

The project was **completely redesigned and downscaled to 52.4 MW** to
cater exclusively to the domestic grid via a standard PPA with NEA. This
represents a **57% capacity reduction** from original design — a
regulatory-risk write-down that no developer voluntarily accepts unless
the alternative (persisting with the export model) was financially
non-viable.

The Likhu-4 redesign is Nepal's clearest documented example of
cross-border transmission policy directly determining project scale. The
wheeling dispute with India did not kill the project — a 52.4 MW plant
was still viable. But it killed two-thirds of it. Every other proposed
export project in Nepal's pipeline (Upper Karnali, West Seti, Arun-3
free-power component, 28,500 MW target with 15,000 MW for export) faces
the same wheeling charge risk, and Likhu-4 is the only completed project
that has already absorbed it. See [[buildability]] and [[stranded-generation]].

## The Desanding Basin Collapse — Commissioning Catastrophe

In **May 2021**, on the eve of production testing, a wall of Likhu-4's
**desanding basin suffered a catastrophic structural collapse**. The
developer publicly blamed the Indian civil contractor, **SSNR Projects**,
alleging construction defects.

A desanding basin failure at a RoR plant on a Himalayan sediment-loaded
river is among the most consequential possible civil-engineering failures.
The basin is the first line of defence protecting the headrace tunnel and
turbines from abrasive sediment. A structural collapse on the eve of
commissioning meant the plant could not operate safely — sediment-laden
water entering the headrace would destroy the Francis turbine runners
within weeks.

The incident delayed full commissioning and required emergency repairs.
The event remains one of Nepal's best-documented examples of how
construction-phase quality failures at the contractor interface convert
directly into revenue loss — and how the limited-recourse SPV structure
concentrates that loss on equity while debt continues to capitalise
interest. The plant eventually commissioned in November 2021, but the
delayed revenue and repair costs pushed GVL's capital structure deeper
into leverage territory.

## Financial Position — Constrained by Legacy Costs

Green Ventures currently carries a **CARE-NP BB** rating — two notches
below investment grade and below both Likhu-2 (BBB-) and Likhu-1 (BB+).
The rating is constrained by:

1. **High capital costs** — the redesign from 120 MW to 52.4 MW did not
   fully recapture sunk engineering and licensing expenditure from the
   export-design phase
2. **Commissioning delay** — the six-month delay from the desanding basin
   collapse meant six months of capitalized interest without revenue
3. **Independent developer structure** — unlike the Dugar-controlled
   plants (Likhu-1, Likhu-2, Likhu Khola A) which benefit from shared
   management and cross-SPV expertise, GVL operates as a standalone entity

The BB rating, while below the cascade average, is not a default warning —
it reflects the margin pressure of a project that absorbed extraordinary
regulatory and construction-phase costs before generating a single kWh.

Unlike the Dugar-controlled cascade plants, Likhu-4 has **no UAE FDI
component**. The equity is held by Triveni Group (Nepal) and Bhilwara
Energy (India) — making Likhu-4 the only Likhu cascade plant with Indian
equity exposure. Indian EPC contractor failures (SSNR) combined with
Indian policy disputes (wheeling charges) affecting an Indian-invested
project is a concentration pattern that would concern any project finance
credit committee.

## Transmission — Same Corridor, Different Story

Like the rest of the cascade, Likhu-4 evacuates through New Khimti.
Following the late-2023 200 MVA transformer upgrade, Likhu-4 was among
the first cascade plants to receive CEA export approval for
Dhalkebar–Muzaffarpur wheeling into the Indian day-ahead market — a
remarkable reversal for a project that abandoned export at the design
stage. The transmission infrastructure that did not exist when the
original 120 MW export model was conceived now exists, and Likhu-4
benefits from it at 52.4 MW.

This is not a vindication of the Indian wheeling-charge regime. It is
evidence that cross-border transmission policy evolves faster than a
RoR hydro project can be redesigned and built. A project conceived for
one regulatory environment commissions into a different one. Likhu-4
is the case study.
