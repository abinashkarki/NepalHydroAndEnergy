# Chameliya Hydropower Project

30 MW Peaking Run-of-River (PROR) project on the Chameliya River, Darchula District, Sudurpashchim Province. Directly owned and operated by the Nepal Electricity Authority — not an independent corporate entity. This page exists as the wiki's primary case study in state capital inefficiency and the structural asymmetry between NEA's internal cost accounting for its own assets versus the rigid PPA caps it imposes on private developers.

### Engineering
A peaking run-of-river design with 6-hour regulating pondage. The 103.7 m gross head and 36 m³/s design discharge define its hydraulic envelope. But Chameliya's engineering significance is not in its design parameters — it is in the catastrophic failure of its headrace tunnel construction.

### Financial
Chameliya is the wiki's primary case study in state capital inefficiency. Built at USD 226 million for 30 MW — **$7.5 million/MW**, roughly 3–5× the global benchmark. For context, [[sahas-urja]] (86 MW, private IPP) was built at ~NPR 172–175 million/MW (~$1.3–1.4 million/MW). The headrace tunnel collapse and decade-long delay drove costs to levels no private developer could survive.

## The Decade-Long Construction Crisis

Chameliya represents one of the most troubled infrastructure developments in Nepal's modern history:

- Civil works began: 2007–2008 (China Gezhouba Group Corporation — civil; Korea Hydro & Nuclear Power — electro-mechanical)
- Original COD target: June 2011
- Initial estimated cost: ~NPR 8 billion (~$100M at historical exchange rate)
- **Actual COD: February 10, 2018** — nearly a decade behind schedule
- **Final cost: ~NPR 15–16 billion ($226M)** — capital intensity of **$7.5 million per MW** vs global benchmark of $1.5–2.5M/MW

The primary engineering failure was an 843-meter section of the headrace tunnel where acute geological squeezing deformed the tunnel walls, requiring extensive unplanned re-excavation and structural reinforcement. Contractual disputes over variation orders triggered work stoppages, and the parliamentary Public Accounts Committee (PAC) intervened over corruption allegations and irregular variation approvals. Source: Kathmandu Post; myRepublica.

## Internal Generation Cost vs Commercial Reality

Chameliya has **no commercial Power Purchase Agreement.** As a directly NEA-owned asset, its energy is accounted for via internal transfer pricing. For FY2080/81:

| Metric | Value |
|---|---|
| Total generation | 133,968 MWh |
| Operation cost | NPR 58.32M (NPR 0.44/kWh) |
| Royalties | NPR 27.07M |
| Depreciation | NPR 321.27M |
| Total allocated cost | NPR 406.66M (NPR 3.04/kWh) |

Source: NEA Generation Directorate Report 2080/2081 (16th Issue, 2024).

The NPR 3.04/kWh internal generation cost is an accounting artifact, not an economic price. The allocated annual depreciation of NPR 321.27M is mathematically insufficient to amortize a ~NPR 15 billion asset over a 25–30 year lifecycle without substantial unrecorded impairments, sovereign bailouts, or aggressive debt write-downs. The NEA's sovereign backing absorbs the capital failure while the utility's internal books present a deceptively low unit cost.

## Why Chameliya Matters to This Wiki

Chameliya is the structural mirror-image of [[ppa-pricing]]: a state utility that tolerates $7.5M/MW capital costs on its own projects — and records the resulting energy at NPR 3.04/kWh — simultaneously enforces NPR 4.80/8.40 PPA caps on private IPPs that must fully amortize commercial debt at double-digit interest rates.

If a private developer faced the geological squeezing and multi-year delays seen at Chameliya, the inflexible PPA structure would force immediate bankruptcy. The private sector bears 100% of construction risk while the NEA's own construction failures are absorbed into the sovereign ledger.

This duality — **socializing the massive capital failures of public projects while strictly capping the revenue potential of private, highly efficient projects** — is the quantitative core of the [[nea-triple-authority]] governance problem. The same institution that cannot deliver a 30 MW project on budget in a decade sets the revenue terms for the entire private sector.

## Disambiguation from Upper Chameliya

Chameliya (30 MW, NEA-owned, COD 2018) must not be confused with **Upper Chameliya** (40 MW, Api Power Company Limited, private IPP, COD August 2023). Upper Chameliya operates under a commercial PPA with a 6-month wet / 6-month dry seasonal modality and 3% annual escalation. These are entirely separate assets on the same river. Conflating the internal cost data of the NEA asset with the commercial PPA of the private IPP is a frequent error in automated research sweeps.

## Prior Research Error — Retracted

Earlier research cycles carried an erroneous PPA rate of NPR 8.85/kWh (wet) and NPR 5.40/kWh (dry) for Chameliya. These figures are a **data hallucination** — they derive from misattributed retail consumer tariffs (NEA Annual Report FY2016/17: "Street Light" at NPR 8.80, "Other Water Supply" at NPR 5.40) and a growth metric text reference ("8.85% activities"). The figures were algorithmically stripped of context and assigned as PPA rates without understanding Nepal's hydro-economic framework, where the dry-season rate must always exceed the wet-season rate. Chameliya has no PPA and never did. These figures have been retracted from the wiki.
