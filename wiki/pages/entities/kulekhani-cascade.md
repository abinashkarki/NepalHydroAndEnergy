Nepal's **only operating reservoir hydropower system**. A cascade of three plants on the Kulekhani River, approximately 50 km south of Kathmandu in Makwanpur district. Built with World Bank support in the 1970s–1980s, it remains — 40+ years later — the single operational counterexample to Nepal's otherwise entirely run-of-river fleet.

### Engineering
Kulekhani I is a seasonal-storage reservoir: a 114 m rockfill dam creates a headpond that captures monsoon inflow (~June–September) and releases it through the dry season (~November–May) to Kulekhani II and III downstream. This is the only facility in Nepal where water can be stored in one season and released in another for power generation. Every other plant is [[run-of-river-hydropower]].

The cascade operation means KL-I releases become KL-II inflow, and KL-II releases become KL-III inflow — a true cascade, not three independent plants. This operational interdependence means Kulekhani's value is in the system, not any individual turbine.

### Output
Kulekhani's seasonal generation pattern is what distinguishes it from RoR. While RoR plants generate ~70–80% of their annual energy in the four monsoon months, Kulekhani shifts a substantial portion into the dry season. The combined 279.8 GWh in FY 2081/82 is modest by total-hydro standards (~3% of national generation), but its timing — concentrated in the dry months and peak evening hours — provides [[firm-power]] that no RoR plant can match.

### Grid Services
Beyond energy, Kulekhani provides services that RoR plants cannot:
- **Black start capability** — KL-I can restart the Kathmandu valley grid without external power, a critical grid-resilience function
- **Peak-hour dispatch** — the reservoir can ramp generation to match evening peak demand (~6–10 PM), when RoR plants are output-limited by declining diurnal flows
- **Frequency regulation** — turbine response to load changes supports grid frequency stability in a system with growing variable solar

These services are not priced in the PPA, which pays the same NPR/kWh as any RoR plant. The value of storage is captured in grid reliability, not in Kulekhani's revenue line. This is part of the broader [[ppa-pricing]] challenge: Nepal's tariff structure does not compensate storage for the services it uniquely provides.

### Schedule
Kulekhani I was commissioned in 1982, making it Nepal's first major storage hydropower project — and, 40+ years later, still its only one. The cascade additions (KL-II in 1986, KL-III in 2019) extended the system but did not add new reservoir capacity. Compare with the pipeline: [[tanahu-hydropower]] (140 MW storage, ~67% complete) and [[dudhkoshi-storage]] (635 MW, pre-construction) are the next storage projects that would break this four-decade monopoly.

## Historical Context

Kulekhani was built with World Bank financing in the late 1970s as part of Nepal's first generation of utility-scale hydropower. At the time, Nepal had fewer than 100 MW of installed capacity, nearly all run-of-river. The choice to build a storage reservoir 50 km from Kathmandu — rather than another RoR plant on a larger river — reflected a specific calculation: the capital city needed reliable dry-season power, and proximity to load mattered more than scale.

The project was completed on time and on budget, and has operated continuously for over 40 years. But the model was never replicated. Every subsequent NEA project — [[kali-gandaki-a]] (144 MW, 2002), [[upper-tamakoshi]] (456 MW, 2021) — chose the RoR engineering model. The reasons are instructive: storage dams require larger upfront capital, longer construction timelines, resettlement programs, and environmental approvals that RoR diversion schemes avoid. In a system where NEA's capital budget was always constrained and political cycles are short, the RoR model won every time after Kulekhani.

The result: 106 MW of storage in 1982, 106 MW of storage in 2026. Four decades of hydropower expansion added ~3,300 MW of RoR capacity with zero additional reservoir storage. Kulekhani is not the exception that proves the rule — it is the exception that **became** the rule, a data point that should have been the first of many and instead became the only one.

## The Storage Paradox

The [[storage-deficit]] page describes the arithmetic: Nepal's ~3,400+ MW of installed hydro is over 95% run-of-river, generating ~70–80% of annual energy in the four monsoon months. Kulekhani — 106 MW, ~3% of installed capacity — is the only plant that can time-shift water across seasons.

This ratio (106:3,400) is the number behind every argument in this wiki about [[seasonal-mismatch]] and [[firm-power]]. It means that in the driest months (February–April), when national demand peaks and river flows bottom, the entire storage-dependent portion of the grid is 106 MW. Everything else depends on whatever the rivers are carrying that day.

The pipeline projects that would change this ratio — [[tanahu-hydropower]] (140 MW), [[dudhkoshi-storage]] (635 MW), [[budhigandaki]] (1,200 MW) — would bring the storage-to-RoR ratio from 1:32 to roughly 1:3 if all are completed. But Kulekhani has been carrying this burden alone since 1982.

## Sedimentation

As Nepal's oldest operating reservoir, Kulekhani is also the best data point for [[sediment-as-design-constraint]]. The Kulekhani catchment drains the Mahabharat range, and monsoon sediment inflow has been gradually reducing the reservoir's active storage volume. The original design assumed a 50-year sediment life, meaning the reservoir would lose roughly 1–2% of active storage per year to silt accumulation.

If this assumption holds, Kulekhani I's active storage has declined from ~85 MCM at commissioning to approximately 50–60 MCM today — a loss of roughly one-third of its original regulation capacity. This is not a crisis (the reservoir still functions), but it is a warning for every planned storage project with a 50–100 year design life: Himalayan sediment loads make "dead storage" a dynamic quantity, not a design-time constant.

## Significance

Kulekhani is the outlier that proves the rule. It is the only facility in Nepal that can shift water from wet to dry season for power generation. Everything else is [[run-of-river-hydropower]]. The fact that 106 MW of storage represents Nepal's entire reservoir base — against 3,400+ MW of total hydro — encapsulates the [[storage-deficit]].

But Kulekhani is also a demonstration that storage works in Nepal. A 40-year operating history with World Bank financing, on-time construction, and continuous dry-season service proves the model is viable. The question is not whether Nepal can build storage — it already did. The question is why it stopped.
