# Dhalkebar–Muzaffarpur 400 kV Interconnection

Nepal's first and — at the time of writing — only operational 400 kV
cross-border link. Every exported MWh, including the
[[bangladesh-trade-route]] allocation, transits this line. It is the
linchpin of Nepal's current export strategy.

## Key Facts

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| Voltage | 400 kV (operated initially at 220 kV, upgraded 2018) |
| Status | Operational |
| Commissioned | February 20, 2016 |
| Nepal terminal | Dhalkebar substation (Dhanusha) |
| India terminal | Muzaffarpur (Bihar) |
| Length | ~140 km (≈40 km Nepal + ≈100 km India) |
| Developers | Cross Border Power Transmission Company (CBPTC) — NEA + Indian JV |
| Financing | ADB, KfW |
| Current transfer | ~800–1,200 MW monsoon, ~300–500 MW dry season |
| Interconnection ID | `dhalkebar_muzaffarpur` |

## Significance

- **Export artery.** Until [[gorakhpur-butwal-interconnection]] and
  [[inaruwa-purnea-interconnection]] commission, this is the only
  400 kV door out. Roughly 95% of Nepal's 2024–25 export revenue
  moved through it.
- **IEX access.** Unlocks the Indian Energy Exchange market — the
  day-ahead pricing that has made monsoon exports profitable for the
  first time in Nepal's history.
- **Bangladesh corridor.** Also the Nepal-side leg of
  [[bangladesh-trade-route]], which then transits through India on
  the Baharampur–Bheramara link.

## Limitations & Open Questions

- **Single point of failure.** No 400 kV backup cross-border exists yet.
  An outage collapses export revenue immediately.
- **Indian-side evacuation.** Muzaffarpur terminus's onward capacity
  into India's Eastern Regional Grid is approaching saturation under
  peak Nepal export scenarios.
- **Dispatch veto.** Scheduling is set by India's NRLDC — Nepal cannot
  unilaterally increase flow even when physical capacity exists.
