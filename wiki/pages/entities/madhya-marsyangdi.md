# Madhya Marsyangdi

Madhya Marsyangdi (Middle Marsyangdi) is a 70 MW operating hydropower project on the Marsyangdi River — one of Nepal's established generation assets with a multi-decade operational track record.

## Marsyangdi Cascade

Madhya Marsyangdi sits in the middle of one of Nepal's most productive hydropower corridors. The Marsyangdi River, a tributary of the Trishuli/Narayani system in the Gandaki basin, hosts a cascade of projects: Upper Marsyangdi A (50 MW), Madhya Marsyangdi (70 MW), and Marsyangdi Besi (50 MW, under construction). This ~170 MW cumulative capacity makes the Marsyangdi cascade a significant block of the national generation portfolio.

## Operating History

As an operating plant, Madhya Marsyangdi provides a different lens from the pre-COD and under-construction projects that dominate the pipeline: actual generation data, real maintenance costs, and historical PLF performance under the standard PPA framework. Operating projects reveal whether the financial models in IPO prospectuses materialize in practice — and at what cost.

## Transmission

The Marsyangdi valley's generation feeds into the national grid via the Marsyangdi corridor infrastructure, connecting to the east-west backbone. The Upper Marsyangdi 220 kV line provides the key evacuation route for this cascade.

## Specifications (NEA FY 2081/82)

Middle Marsyangdi (MMHPS) is a **70 MW peaking run-of-river** plant in
Lamjung District, Gandaki Province — the middle anchor of the 170 MW
Marsyangdi cascade. Commissioned in December 2008, it diverts flow from
the Marsyangdi River originating in glacier-fed Tilicho Lake and can
reserve flow for **up to five hours of peaking**, even during low
discharge.

In FY 2081/82, MMHPS completed overhaul of Unit 2, restored desander
basins in-house, rebuilt spillway chute no. 1 with M60 concrete and
steel lining, and installed a digital governor panel, 110V battery bank,
and RTU for real-time Load Dispatch Centre communication.

## The Marsyangdi Cascade — 170 MW in One Valley

MMHPS sits between **Upper Marsyangdi A** (50 MW, operating, upstream)
and **Marsyangdi Besi** (50 MW, under construction, downstream) — a
cumulative **170 MW** in the Marsyangdi valley of the Gandaki basin.
This is one of Nepal's most concentrated generation corridors. The
Marsyangdi River, a glacier-fed tributary of the Narayani system,
provides more reliable base flow than the monsoon-dominated rain-fed
tributaries where most private IPPs are located.

The cascade's cumulative water use is increasingly significant as
Marsyangdi Besi approaches commissioning. Environmental flow
requirements and sediment management decisions at Upper Marsyangdi A
directly affect MMHPS and the downstream plant. The Marsyangdi corridor
connects to the national grid via the Upper Marsyangdi 220 kV
transmission line, feeding the east-west backbone.

## The Contrast That Matters — Design vs Actual

MMHPS's **107.6% of design generation** (427.9 GWh against 398 GWh) is
the highest performance ratio in the NEA large-plant fleet. Compare:

| Plant | MW | Design (GWh) | FY 2081/82 (GWh) | % of Design |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Middle Marsyangdi | 70 | 398 | 427.9 | **107.6%** |
| Kali Gandaki A | 144 | 842 | 863.5 | 102.5% |
| Marsyangdi | 69 | 462.5 | 451.8 | 97.0% |
| Chameliya | 30 | 184.2 | 142.4 | 77.3% |
| Upper Trishuli 3A | 60 | 489.8 | 356.0 | 72.7% |

The divergence at the bottom of the table is [[q-design-discharge]] in
action: the plants that underperform by 20–30% are the ones whose design
hydrology assumptions were too aggressive. MMHPS's opposite result
suggests the Tilicho-fed Marsyangdi basin has more reliable baseflow
than the monsoonal rain-fed catchments where design overestimation is
endemic — or that the 2008-era design hydrological study was
appropriately conservative.

This is the structural pattern the [[chilime]] page documents at 22 MW:
conservative Q-design (Q65+) in a snow/glacier-fed basin produces actual
generation above contracted energy. MMHPS demonstrates the same mechanism
at 70 MW — and at three times the scale. See [[q-design-discharge]] for
the full generation performance comparison across the RoR fleet.

## Renewal Cycle — A Younger Plant with a Different Problem

At 16 years post-commissioning, MMHPS is entering the phase where
routine overhauls begin to graduate toward major rehabilitation.
The FY 2081/82 work scope — Unit 2 overhaul, desander basin restoration,
spillway chute rebuild with M60 concrete and epoxy coating — is heavier
maintenance than the plant would have required in its first decade.

The digital governor panel and RTU installation signals a shift from
mechanical to digital control, the modernization trajectory that aging
plants like [[kali-gandaki-a]] are now facing at greater cost and
outage risk. MMHPS's relatively young age means this transition can
be done incrementally rather than as an emergency rehab — but it also
means it is a test case for what that incremental path looks like.
Every NEA plant eventually follows it.
