## The Finishing Line — First to Commission

Likhu Khola A was the first MV Dugar project to reach the finish line, commissioning
on February 8, 2022 — nearly 18 months before [[likhu-2]] (July 2023) and over two
years before [[likhu-1]] (April 2024). For a conglomerate simultaneously developing
three generation assets in the same river basin, getting the smallest project
operational first was strategically essential: it established the developer's
creditworthiness with lenders, demonstrated construction capability to the regulator,
and generated early cash flow to support the larger builds.

At NPR 5,996 million (~NPR 206M/MW), Likhu Khola A's capital cost sits in line with
Nepal's post-2015 private IPP benchmark. The 75:25 debt-equity ratio is lower than
the cascade's other Dugar-controlled plants ([[likhu-1]] at 80:20, [[likhu-2]] at
78:22), reflecting the conservative leverage typical of first-mover projects in an
unproven basin.

## The MV Dugar FDI Model

Likhu Khola A shares the cascade's defining equity architecture: the **MV Dugar
Group** (a Nepali conglomerate with interests in banking, insurance, automotive
trading, and infrastructure) paired with **Mogha Energy DMCC**, a UAE-based
investment firm. Mogha Energy holds a confirmed ~7% institutional promoter stake
alongside Dugar family majority holdings.

This FDI model — a domestic group clustering multiple projects under shared foreign
capital through separate SPVs — is a departure from Nepal's traditional
single-project domestic bank consortium structure. For the UAE investors, the
cluster offers risk diversification: three projects (77 + 55 + 29 = 161 MW
cumulative under Dugar control) share the same basin, corridor, and regulatory
regime, but operate as legally distinct entities with independent PPAs and debt.

Butwal Power Company (BPC) holds **no equity** in Likhu Khola A. BPC's involvement
in the Likhu basin is exclusively technical — its subsidiary HCEL provided
engineering consultancy and construction supervision, generating consulting
revenue, not generation revenue.

## PPA Structure — Partial Curtailment Exposure

Likhu Khola A's PPA has a distinctive two-tier structure. Of the 29.04 MW installed
capacity, only **24.2 MW (83.4%)** is contracted on strict take-or-pay terms,
yielding 135.68 GWh/year with no NEA curtailment risk. The remaining **4.84 MW
(16.6%)** was built targeting future uncontracted take-and-pay off-take, exposed
to NEA dispatch discretion.

This contrasts with [[likhu-1]], where 80% is take-or-pay, and [[likhu-2]], where
75% is take-or-pay. Likhu Khola A sits between the two on curtailment protection:
better than Likhu-2's 25% exposure, below Likhu-1's 20%.

## Financial Performance

CARE Ratings Nepal reaffirmed the project's bank facilities at **CARE-NP BBB-**
in March 2025, supported by:

- **PBILDT margins averaging 82% to 91%** — strong operating cash generation
- **Improving debt coverage ratios** as the project seasons past its commissioning
  ramp-up
- Revenue stability from the high take-or-pay share (83.4%)

The BBB- rating places Likhu Khola A in the same CARE tier as [[likhu-2]]
(BBB- Is), above [[likhu-4]] (BB). The rating reflects the project's
conservative leverage structure and diversified lending consortium (5 banks),
mitigated by the single-corridor transmission risk shared by the entire cascade.

## Cascade Position — Smallest, but Structurally Identical

At 29.04 MW, Likhu Khola A is dwarfed by [[likhu-1]] (77 MW), [[likhu-2]] (55 MW),
and [[likhu-4]] (52.4 MW) in capacity terms. But it shares the cascade's structural
vulnerabilities:

- **Hydrological interdependence:** as an upstream plant in the Likhu system, its
  tailwater discharge affects downstream operators. Emergency shutdowns or
  desilting operations at Likhu Khola A alter inflow parameters for lower
  cascade plants — a cascade externality that no standalone PPA accounts for.
- **Single corridor dependency:** all four cascade plants evacuate through the
  New Khimti substation into the Khimti–Dhalkebar 220 kV line. The same 100 MVA
  transformer that bottlenecked [[likhu-2]] at 60% PLF in FY 2023/24 constrained
  the entire cascade. The late-2023 upgrade to 300 MVA resolved the immediate
  crisis, but a single substation failure returns all four plants to
  stranded-generation status. See [[buildability]] and [[stranded-generation]].
