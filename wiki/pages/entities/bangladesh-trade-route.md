# Nepal–Bangladesh Electricity Trade Route

Nepal's first electricity export to a country other than India. A trilateral arrangement routed through India's grid — making India simultaneously Nepal's competitor, buyer, and logistics gatekeeper.

## Key Facts

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| Tripartite PSA signed | October 3, 2024 |
| Parties | NEA, Bangladesh Power Development Board (BPDB), NTPC Vidyut Vyapar Nigam (India) |
| First export | 12 hours on November 15, 2024 (symbolic) |
| Full-scale exports began | June 15, 2025 |
| Export window | June 15–November 15 each year (5 months) |
| Duration | 5-year agreement (until Oct 2029) |
| Current capacity | 40 MW |
| Agreed expansion | **60 MW** from June 2026 (+20 MW, pending India's CEA approval) |
| Tariff | **6.40 US cents/unit** |
| Currency | **USD** (unlike India trade in INR) |
| Route | Nepal → Dhalkebar–Muzaffarpur 400 kV → Baharampur–Bheramara 400 kV → Bangladesh |

## Projects Supplying Bangladesh

NEA currently allocates output from **Trishuli (25 MW)** and **Chilime (22 MW)** — older NEA-owned plants — to the Bangladesh export quota. India's CEA has approved these specific 40 MW until October 2, 2029.

## Expansion Trajectory

- **Nov 2025 (7th JSC meeting):** Nepal and Bangladesh agreed to add 20 MW (total 60 MW) from June 2026
- **Bangladesh's long-term ambition:** import **9,000 MW** from Nepal by 2040
- **Alternative routes under study:** Barapukuria, Panchagarh/Thakurgaon, Bheramara, Comilla corridors through India
- **Sunkoshi III (683 MW):** Expert committee formed to evaluate this project specifically for Bangladesh supply
- **Upper Karnali (900 MW):** GMR-developed project had a planned 500 MW PPA to Bangladesh — status uncertain after Sheikh Hasina's fall (Aug 2024)

## Strategic Significance

1. **Buyer diversification.** Reduces Nepal's monopsony dependence on India's IEX for export revenue
2. **USD pricing.** Bangladesh trade in dollars hedges against INR-NPR forex risk — significant given [[nea]]'s NPR 2.5B forex losses in FY 2024/25
3. **India as gatekeeper.** Every MW to Bangladesh requires India's Central Electricity Authority approval and transits Indian lines — India retains structural veto over Nepal's market diversification
4. **Seasonal limitation.** Exports only run Jun–Nov, perfectly overlapping Nepal's surplus window — does nothing for dry-season revenue. The arrangement is complementary to the [[seasonal-arbitrage-trap]], not a solution to it
5. **Political fragility.** Bangladesh's political transition (post-Hasina) has stalled larger pipeline projects (Upper Karnali PPA, direct transmission studies)
