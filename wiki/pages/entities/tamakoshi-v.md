## The Cascade Model — Paired with Upper Tamakoshi

Tamakoshi V is the first cascade project explicitly designed to operate in
tandem with [[upper-tamakoshi]]. At 99.8 MW, it captures energy from the
tailwater discharge of the 456 MW PROR powerhouse upstream — effectively
extracting additional generation from the same Tama Koshi water that has
already passed through Upper Tamakoshi's turbines.

This cascade pairing is structurally significant for three reasons:

1. **Hydrological efficiency:** Instead of spilling Upper Tamakoshi's tailwater,
   Tamakoshi V captures it for secondary generation. This is the same
   cascade principle as the [[kulekhani-cascade]], but at 10× the scale.

2. **Shared transmission:** Tamakoshi V plugs into the same New Khimti →
   Khimti-Dhalkebar 220/400 kV evacuation corridor that Upper Tamakoshi
   depends on. The corridor's 400 kV GIS building and transformer banks at
   New Khimti were designed with this combined load in mind.

3. **Operational interdependence:** Any curtailment, maintenance shutdown, or
   flood closure at Upper Tamakoshi immediately zeroes Tamakoshi V's inflow.
   The downstream plant is not an independent asset — it is a rider on the
   upstream plant's operational schedule. This interdependence is not
   captured in standalone PPA terms.

## The EPF Financing Model

Tamakoshi V is financed by Nepal's **Employers Provident Fund (EPF)** — the
country's largest pension fund. This marks a departure from the commercial
bank consortium model that dominates private IPP financing.

The EPF model carries distinct structural implications:

- **Long-term liability matching:** EPF's pension obligations span decades,
  making its capital well-suited to infrastructure with long depreciation
  schedules. Commercial banks struggle with the 15-year tenors typical of
  hydro lending.

- **Lower cost of capital:** As a mandatory social security institution, EPF
  can deploy capital at rates below commercial lending — reducing the project's
  DSCR pressure relative to commercially-financed IPPs of equivalent scale.

- **Public accountability:** EPF's investment decisions are ultimately
  answerable to Nepal's workforce. The financial performance of Tamakoshi V
  will directly affect millions of pension accounts — a political dimension
  absent from private bank consortiums.

- **Precedent value:** If Tamakoshi V succeeds under EPF financing, it
  establishes a template for domestic institutional capital to fund Nepal's
  hydropower pipeline without the currency risk and short tenors of
  international project finance.

## The NEA Subsidiary Ecosystem

Tamakoshi Jalvidyut Company Limited (TKJVCL) sits within the same NEA
subsidiary architecture that produced [[upper-tamakoshi]] (via UKHLL), but
with a distinct developer entity and financing source. The relationship:

| Project | Capacity | Developer | Financing | Status |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| [[upper-tamakoshi]] | 456 MW | Upper Tamakoshi Hydropower Ltd. (UKHLL) | Consortium (NEA + EPF + NTC + others) | Operating (2021) |
| Tamakoshi V | 99.8 MW | Tamakoshi Jalvidyut Company Ltd. (TKJVCL) | EPF | Under construction |
| [[tamakoshi-3]] | 650 MW | TBI Holding Co. Ltd. | — | Survey |

The Tama Koshi basin in Dolakha now carries three NEA-linked projects at
different delivery stages — from the operating 456 MW anchor to the 650 MW
survey-stage aspirational — with Tamakoshi V as the bridge asset connecting
current operations to future basin expansion.

## Construction Status

As of the NEA FY 2024/25 annual report, Tamakoshi V has reached **15.34%
construction progress**. Key milestones:

- Contractor's camp near headworks and powerhouse area completed
- Bunker near headworks area completed
- Batching and crusher plant installed and operational
- Road access from Singati Bazar (near Upper Tamakoshi) established

The project's RCOD target under the JICA IPSDP milestone ladder is 2025/2026,
though current progress suggests a realistic completion window beyond that
date. The NEA annual report lists Tamakoshi V alongside Trishuli 3B (37 MW),
Rolwaling (22 MW), and Upper Modi (60 MW) as NEA-linked projects "in
different stages of construction."

## Transmission and Corridor Risk

Tamakoshi V shares the Tama Koshi transmission corridor with the largest
concentration of generation capacity in Nepal. The New Khimti substation —
originally sized for Upper Tamakoshi (456 MW) — now serves the entire
Likhu cascade (213 MW) plus Tamakoshi V (99.8 MW), for a combined corridor
load exceeding 768 MW.

The 200 MVA auto-transformer upgrade completed in late 2023 (which resolved
[[likhu-2]]'s stranded generation crisis) was a necessary but not sufficient
fix. As Tamakoshi V approaches COD, the corridor will face renewed capacity
pressure — and the cascade interdependence with Upper Tamakoshi means any
upstream NEA dispatch decision directly determines Tamakoshi V's revenue.

## Cascade Interdependence and Risk

The tandem operation model — Tamakoshi V catching Upper Tamakoshi's
tailwater — is hydrologically elegant but operationally fragile:

- **Upstream curtailment:** If NLDC curtails Upper Tamakoshi for grid
  stability, Tamakoshi V's inflow drops to zero. The downstream PPA
  contains no compensation mechanism for upstream operational decisions by
  the same NEA that signs both contracts.

- **Flood exposure:** The September 2024 flood that shut Upper Tamakoshi
  for 88 days (reducing generation to 67% of design) would have zeroed
  Tamakoshi V for the same period. Unlike [[chilime]]'s snow-fed buffer,
  the Tama Koshi is a high-discharge monsoon system with extreme seasonal
  volatility.

- **Single corridor dependency:** A transformer failure at New Khimti
  strands both plants simultaneously — 555.8 MW of combined NEA-subsidiary
  generation offline in a single event. See [[buildability]] and
  [[stranded-generation]].
