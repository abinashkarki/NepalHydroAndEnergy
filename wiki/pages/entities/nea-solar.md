# NEA Solar Programme

The **utility-scale grid-tied solar programme** operated by Nepal Electricity Authority. Sits inside NEA as a distinct programme line that has grown from a single 25 MWp NEA-EPC plant (Bidur/Trishuli, 2022) to a ~1.1 GWp pipeline as of FY 2024/25.

## Programme evolution

| Phase | Years | Character | Key output |
|---|---|---|---:|
| Pre-programme | 2010–2015 | Isolated small pilots | <1 MWp demonstration sites |
| Phase I — first IPP auctions | 2016–2018 | 5-developer competitive bid | 24 MWp awarded, 3 plants commissioned 2019–20 |
| Phase II — NEA self-build | 2019–2022 | WB Grid Solar & EE Project | **25 MWp Bidur/Trishuli commissioned 2022** |
| Phase III — IPP rounds | 2023–2024 | Competitive-bid IPP PPAs | **170 MWp PPAs signed (8 plants)** |
| Phase IV — the 960 MW tender | 2024–2025 | National-scale IPP portfolio | **960 MWp awarded (63 projects)** ([[nea-960mw-solar-tender]]) |
| Phase V (emerging) | 2025– | Hybrid / floating / grid-impact | Reservoir floating PV, grid-impact studies, ~585 MW additional study-stage |

## Key Facts (FY 2024/25)

- **Operating utility-scale solar:** 141.74 MWp across 25 DoED-listed plants (all grid-tied; NEA-owned + IPP-owned combined)
- **NEA-owned share:** ~24.36 MWp (Bidur/Trishuli / Devighat blocks)
- **IPP / public-utility-owned share:** ~117.38 MWp (commissioned under early IPP rounds and institutional utility plants)
- **PPA-signed, pre-COD:** 170 MWp (8 plants, FY 2024/25 signings)
- **Tender-awarded, pre-PPA:** 960 MWp (63 projects from 2024/25 tender)
- **Under grid-impact study, beyond 960 MW:** ~585 MWp
- **Annual PV generation (FY 2024/25):** ~220 GWh (CF ~18%)

## Tariff structure

NEA's **tariff-based competitive bidding** framework for utility solar:

- **Phase I (2016–18):** NPR 7.30–8.40/kWh, single-round competitive
- **Phase III (2023–24):** NPR 5.94–6.50/kWh, competitive bidding with ceiling
- **Phase IV (960 MW, 2024–25):** NPR 4.99–5.54/kWh, national tender with province-balanced allocation

See [[data-solar-hydro-lcoe]] for the full time-series and [[solar-lcoe-crossover]] for the cross-over narrative.

## Programme organizational structure

NEA's utility solar programme is scattered across several NEA directorates — a structural weakness:

- **Generation Directorate** — NEA-owned plant operation (Bidur/Trishuli)
- **Project Management Directorate** — tender design, PPA negotiation
- **Transmission Directorate** — grid-impact studies and interconnection
- **Distribution Directorate** — net-metering for rooftop
- **Engineering Services Department / NEC** — EPC supervision

No single dedicated "solar cell" exists at senior-management level. The 960 MW tender exposed coordination friction across these five lines; a consolidated Renewable Energy Directorate is the obvious institutional fix.

## Institutional challenges

### 1. Land acquisition is the new bottleneck

Phase I–III plants were small enough (0.5–25 MWp) that land was project-specific. Phase IV (960 MW) triggers the land-at-scale question that [[agrivoltaics-and-land]] addresses. NEA does not have a land-programme office; this function needs to move to a new body or to AEPC.

### 2. Grid integration at 1+ GW solar

The cumulative grid impact study NEA completed under Phase IV anticipates **4–6% instantaneous-penetration events by 2027** when 960 MW is partially commissioned. Forecast management, BESS requirements, and reactive-power compensation need to be designed in now.

### 3. PPA exposure balance

NEA's PPA liability has grown to **NPR 77.1B annual electricity purchase cost** (see [[nea]]). Solar PPAs are adding ~NPR 6–8B/year to this. The single-buyer model ([[nepal-power-trade-schemes]]) plus fixed-price solar PPAs creates a structural interest-rate-sensitive liability that needs transparent treatment.

### 4. Talent depth

NEA's solar engineering depth is thin; the Grid Solar & EE Project invested in training but the talent base for an additional 1 GW programme is a 3–5 year capacity-build, not a tender-cycle fix.
