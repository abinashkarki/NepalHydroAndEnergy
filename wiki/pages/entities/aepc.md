# Alternative Energy Promotion Centre (AEPC)

Government of Nepal institution responsible for **rural electrification through renewables**. Technically a semi-autonomous body under the Ministry of Energy, Water Resources & Irrigation (MoEWRI); operationally the country's primary administrator of the off-grid solar, biogas, improved cookstove, and community mini-grid programmes. Standing alongside (not inside) NEA.

## Role summary

| Function | AEPC | NEA |
|---|---|---|
| Grid-tied utility-scale solar | Technical partner on donor-track | Lead operator |
| Rooftop / distributed solar | Subsidy channel | Net-metering administration |
| Solar home systems (SHS) | **Primary operator** | Not involved |
| Community mini-grids | **Primary operator** | Grid-edge interconnect when reached |
| Biogas (~400,000 plants) | **Primary operator** | Not involved |
| Improved cookstove (~1M+ units) | **Primary operator** | Not involved |
| Off-grid institutional solar | **Primary operator** | Not involved |

## Key Facts

- **Founded:** 1996, under the Ministry of Science, Technology and Environment originally; relocated to MoEWRI
- **Cumulative solar home systems installed:** ~**1.1 million** (as of ~2021 with ongoing additions)
- **Cumulative SHS capacity:** ~**80–100 MWp**
- **Community mini-grids operational:** ~**30** sites, ~1.1 MWp aggregate
- **Institutional solar sites:** ~**25,000**, ~15 MWp aggregate
- **Geographic footprint:** strongest in Karnali, Sudurpaschim, remote hill districts of Koshi / Bagmati / Gandaki (see [[data-rooftop-shs-deployment]])
- **Donor partners:** ADB, SREP (CIF), World Bank, EIB, UNDP, GIZ, DANIDA, various bilateral channels

## Financing structure

AEPC's renewable programmes run on a **subsidy-plus-concessional-finance** model:

- **Grant element:** 30–50% of capex for remote / difficult districts; lower for semi-urban
- **Microfinance element:** remainder, via Nepal Rastra Bank-approved cooperative lenders
- **Community equity element:** labour + small cash contribution in most mini-grids
- **Donor-funded programme lines** set the budget envelope (ADB, WB, EIB all operate through AEPC)

The total annual AEPC renewable-programme budget has historically been in the NPR 3–8 billion range (highly donor-dependent).

## Why AEPC is strategically under-weighted

Three reasons AEPC is usually missed in Nepal energy analysis:

1. **MW under-counting.** AEPC's 100+ MWp cumulative solar is spread across a million systems; it doesn't appear on NEA's installed-capacity table, so gets omitted from "Nepal has 142 MW of solar" soundbites. The real number is ~2.3× larger if you count both.

2. **The value-weight is higher than MW-weight.** Off-grid solar displaces NPR 60–90/kWh diesel, not NPR 8/kWh grid; the *value* of AEPC's fleet is disproportionately high — 10–15× the utility-equivalent kWh value (see [[data-solar-hydro-lcoe]]).

3. **AEPC is the institutional carrier of the rural-electrification political coalition.** Which is the coalition most likely to sustain Nepal's solar policy past any single electoral cycle. Utility solar depends on NEA tender capacity; rural solar depends on AEPC cooperative networks — and the latter has been more durable institutionally (AEPC has run continuous SHS programmes for ~28 years).

## The AEPC strategic expansion thesis

The wiki argues that AEPC, not NEA, is the natural home for three emerging solar programmes:

### 1. Agrivoltaic programme administration

[[agrivoltaics-and-land]] requires lease-template law, revenue-sharing contracts with smallholder cooperatives, and crop-verification protocols. These are AEPC-format skills, not NEA-format skills. Terai solar at scale likely runs through AEPC's cooperative networks even when the physical plant is a 10–50 MWp ground-mount feeding the NEA grid.

### 2. Off-grid to grid-edge integration

As NEA grid reaches former off-grid villages (~8.6% of households still grid-absent, [[nea]]), each AEPC mini-grid becomes a grid-edge asset. AEPC-NEA integration protocol (who owns what, tariff structure, BESS-sharing) is currently ad-hoc and should be formalized. [[rooftop-minigrid-offgrid]].

### 3. Solar + BESS rural hybrids

The NEA FY 2024/25 annual report explicitly references off-grid solar + BESS deployments in Karnali as an emerging programme line. This is AEPC's home ground technically; NEA would be the grid-edge integrator but not the programme operator. Formalising this channel is one of the highest-leverage institutional moves in Nepal solar policy.

## Institutional challenges

- **Donor dependence.** AEPC's budget oscillates with donor cycles; the 2017–2020 SREP cycle had ~2× the budget of the 2022–2024 period. Programme continuity depends on donor pipeline management.
- **Staff capacity.** Rural technical staff are overstretched; each engineer covers multiple districts in Karnali / Sudurpaschim. Community mini-grid O&M quality varies with staffing continuity.
- **Mandate overlap.** AEPC's mandate edges into MoEWRI rural electrification and NEA distribution; interagency coordination has historically been ad-hoc.
- **Data / registry infrastructure.** Cumulative deployment numbers are known at the national roll-up but district-level registries are partial post-2021.
