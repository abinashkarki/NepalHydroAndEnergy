# Kali Gandaki A

Nepal's **largest operating power plant of any kind** (144 MW), the
Kali Gandaki A run-of-river scheme at Mirmi has anchored the national
grid since 2002. It is Nepali-owned (NEA), ADB/JBIC-financed, and a
useful foil to the newer India-developed export projects: it shows
what a multilaterally-funded, domestically-dispatched fleet backbone
looks like — and also what two decades of wear do to one.

## Specifications

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| Capacity | 144 MW (3 × 48 MW Toshiba Francis units) |
| Status | Operating since 2002 |
| Type | Run-of-river with pondage |
| River | Kali Gandaki at Mirmi ([[gandaki-basin]]) |
| District | Palpa / Syangja (powerhouse at Mirmi, Syangja; dam upstream in Palpa) |
| Owner / Operator | [[nea]] |
| Gross head | 115 m |
| Design energy | ~860 GWh / year |
| Actual avg output | ~592 GWh / year |
| Construction cost | USD 354.8 million (ADB + JBIC) |

## Significance

Kali Gandaki A still matters out of proportion to its size. At
144 MW it is only about 4% of Nepal's 2025 installed fleet, but it
was for two decades the single largest plant on the grid — and in
most years it produced more firm, dispatchable energy than any other
asset NEA operates. It remains the reference point when discussing
what a Nepali-utility-run, multilaterally-financed project can look
like on the buildability spectrum.

Compare [[kali-gandaki-a]] to two neighbours. [[upper-tamakoshi]]
(456 MW, 2021) is larger, newer and also Nepali-developed, but pure
RoR without pondage. [[arun-3]] (900 MW, under construction) will be
nameplate-bigger but peaking-design and India-dispatched.
Kali Gandaki A sits between them in era and design philosophy —
shared ADB lineage with Tanahu, NEA ownership like Upper Tamakoshi,
and RoR-with-pondage shape that smooths evening load.

## Limitations & Controversies

**The design-energy gap.** The project was designed for roughly
860 GWh/year but has averaged closer to 592 GWh — ~30% below design.
The ADB's own performance evaluation flagged sedimentation, variable
hydrology and aging electromechanical equipment as factors. That gap
is a real-world reminder that Nepal's nameplate capacity is not the
same as its delivered energy, a distinction central to
[[firm-power]] and [[seasonal-mismatch]] analysis.

**Rehabilitation overhang.** In 2024 NEA floated a tender for design
and supervision consultants to rehabilitate all three units. Any
rehab of a fleet anchor plant introduces real derating risk during
outage windows — exactly when the dry-season [[storage-deficit]] bites
hardest. Timing the rehab around Nepal's import / export contracts
with India is a non-trivial coordination problem.

**Pondage vs storage.** Kali Gandaki A is not a reservoir project.
The pondage it does have smooths daily load, not seasonal, so when
monsoon flows fall off in January–May, Kali Gandaki A's contribution
falls with them. Nepal's structural answer to that problem still sits
in pages like [[dudhkoshi-storage]], [[tanahu-hydropower]] and
[[budhigandaki]] — not here.
