# Mountain Hydro System Comparison

Nepal's hydropower system benchmarked against relevant mountain/hydro-dominant peers. The comparison exposes what separates "having rivers" from "having a functional hydro economy."

Nepal has **~1/65th** the renewable W/capita of Norway, and **~1/27th** of Bhutan. Even Laos — often treated as a development peer — has **11x** Nepal's per-capita figure.

Nepal's **potential** per capita (29,400 kWh/yr/person) vastly exceeds its **actual production** (106 kWh/yr/person) — a **277:1 gap** between what the rivers could produce and what the country currently generates. This is among the highest potential-to-actual ratios globally.

## What Separates Norway from Nepal

| Factor | Norway | Nepal |
|--------|--------|-------|
| Storage | 1,401 MW pumped + extensive reservoir capacity; ~87 TWh reservoir storage | **Zero pumped storage**; 85 MCM active reservoir (Kulekhani only) |
| Grid | Mature 420/300/132 kV grid; fully interconnected with Nordic market (NordPool) | Incomplete 400 kV backbone; single cross-border link operational |
| Market | Liberalized energy market since 1991; spot + futures trading | Single-buyer model; no domestic wholesale market |
| Fleet mix | ~50% reservoir, ~50% RoR — dispatchable at will | **>90% RoR/PRoR** — output follows river, not demand |
| Sediment | Glacial, but low-sediment Scandinavian geology | Extreme Himalayan sediment loads; 3–5x Norway's concentrations |
| Institutional age | NVE established 1921; >100 years of continuous regulation | NEA established 1985; regulatory commission still finding its footing |
| Climate risk | Moderate — warming may increase winter precipitation | Severe — glacial retreat, GLOF risk, monsoon intensification |

## The Bhutan Model: Why It's Not Copyable

[[bhutan-hydropower-model]] works because:

1. **Population:** 0.8M vs Nepal's 30M — surplus per capita is massive
2. **Single buyer:** India absorbs virtually all Bhutanese exports under guaranteed bilateral PPAs at fixed rates
3. **Fiscal structure:** Hydro royalties + dividends fund ~25% of Bhutan's government budget
4. **Governance:** Constitutional monarchy with policy continuity; Nepal has had 18+ governments in 30 years

Nepal cannot replicate the Bhutan model because it would need to export ~37x the volume to achieve equivalent per-capita revenue — far beyond any plausible corridor capacity — and its domestic demand and industrial base are large enough to be the primary consumption story rather than a rounding error.

## The Laos Cautionary Tale

Laos has expanded aggressively (9,760 MW installed) through Chinese and Thai investment, targeting 13 GW by 2030. But:

- **Debt burden:** Multiple dam projects are debt-financed by Chinese state banks; repayment dependent on Thai electricity purchases
- **Environmental cost:** Mekong mainstream dams alter sediment and fish migration for 60+ million downstream people
- **Ownership:** Much capacity is foreign-owned, with limited fiscal capture by the Laotian state
- Nepal risks a similar "building for others" pattern if export-led strategy prioritizes MW over domestic productive use

## Off-River Pumped Hydro Opportunity

A 2021 Oxford study identified **~2,800 off-river pumped hydro sites** in Nepal with combined storage capacity of **~50 TWh**. Nepal needs only **~1.5 TWh** of storage to balance a 100% renewable system at advanced-economy consumption levels. This means Nepal has **17x more pumped-hydro potential than it will ever need** — an option that bypasses the valley-shape paradox of on-river reservoir storage (see [[data-storage-comparison]]).
