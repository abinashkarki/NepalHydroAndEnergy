# Agricultural Energy Consumption

Agricultural energy use in Nepal — small in total volume, but strategically central to food security, diesel displacement, and rural electrification outcomes. Agriculture employs ~60% of the workforce but accounts for only 0.95% of final energy — because most farm work is manual and most farm energy is human and animal labour, not fuel.

## Headline Numbers (FY 2079/80, WECS)

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Agricultural final energy consumption | **5,040 TJ** (0.95% of total 532,420 TJ) |
| Diesel share of agricultural energy | **75.8%** (3,819 TJ) |
| Electricity share | **22.5%** (1,134 TJ ≈ 315 GWh) |
| Petrol share | 1.2% (61 TJ) |
| Solar / micro-hydro / wind share | 0.5% (26 TJ) |

> [!note] Classification caveat
> The WECS energy balance classifies agro-processing (sugar mills: 16,337 TJ bagasse; tea dryers; cardamom dryers; food processing) under **"Industrial"**, not "Agriculture." The 5,040 TJ figure covers on-farm energy use only — tractors, tubewells, threshers, pumps. Including agro-processing would roughly double the figure. See [[data-industrial-electricity-consumers]] for the agro-processing component.

## The Diesel Dependence Problem

Agriculture is the sector where diesel displacement from electricity would have the clearest and most immediate payoff:

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Diesel consumed in agriculture (FY 2079/80) | **3,819 TJ** (~106 million liters) |
| Equivalent electricity if fully electrified | ~1,272 GWh (assuming 3× electric motor efficiency advantage) |
| Diesel cost at retail (~NPR 130/L) | ~NPR 13.8 billion/yr in imported fuel |
| Equivalent grid electricity cost at NPR 7.50/kWh | ~NPR 9.5 billion/yr |
| Annual foreign exchange savings from full electrification | **~NPR 13.8 billion** (plus ~NPR 4.3 billion cheaper per-unit cost) |

> [!finding] Each GWh of agricultural diesel displacement saves Nepal ~NPR 10.8 million in import costs
> The per-unit economics are compelling: diesel delivers ~3 kWh of useful shaft work per litre at 25% engine efficiency; an electric pump delivers the same work at 85% motor efficiency for ~NPR 7.50 of grid electricity vs NPR 130 of diesel. The saving is ~5× per unit of useful work.

## Geographic Concentration

Agricultural energy use follows the farming geography:

1. **Terai belt (Madhesh, Lumbini, Koshi, Sudurpaschim southern districts):** ~80% of tubewell irrigation. Shallow and deep tubewells concentrated in the eastern and central Terai where groundwater is accessible. Diesel pumps dominate (~70%+ of tubewells), electric pumps are growing as grid extends.

2. **Mid-hills (Gandaki, Bagmati outside valley):** Surface lift irrigation from rivers and streams. Smaller schemes, many diesel-powered. Micro-hydro serves some milling and pumping.

3. **Mountain region (Karnali, northern districts):** Minimal commercial energy use in agriculture — largely rain-fed, manual, and animal-traction.

> [!cite] wb-water-sector-diagnostic
> Total water demand is projected to double by 2050 in the high-growth scenario, driven by domestic and industrial demand. Agricultural water demand is the single largest component, and irrigation modernization is essential to a strong poverty reduction strategy.

## The Tubewell Electrification Opportunity

Nepal has an estimated **~100,000+ shallow tubewells** (STWs) and several thousand deep tubewells (DTWs) in the Terai, predominantly diesel-powered. The government's policy direction — mentioned in NEA's FY 2024/25 report — is to encourage electricity use for irrigation alongside cooking and transportation.

Key enablers for electrification:
- **Grid extension into agricultural feeders** — NEA is actively extending 33/11 kV lines into rural Terai
- **Dedicated agricultural tariff** — Nepal has historically had separate irrigation tariffs, though their adequacy is debated
- **Solar irrigation pumps** — AEPC has deployed thousands of solar irrigation pumps under alternative energy programs (see [[data-rooftop-shs-deployment]] for the off-grid solar context)

Key barriers:
- **Capital cost of electric pumps** — upfront cost is higher than diesel pump sets
- **Connection charges** — NEA requires transformer and feeder costs for high-load connections
- **Reliability in remote areas** — power quality and outage frequency in rural feeders remain worse than urban
- **Diesel subsidy legacy** — diesel has been subsidized or price-controlled, undermining the economic case for switching

## Agricultural Electricity in NEA Sales

NEA does not report agricultural electricity separately — it falls within the "Others" category (10.48% of FY 2024/25 sales, ~1,183 GWh) which also includes street lighting, temporary connections, and water supply pumping. The actual agriculture-only slice within "Others" is estimated at **~200–350 GWh** based on:
- WECS cross-tab showing 315 GWh-equivalent (1,134 TJ) agricultural electricity consumption in FY 2079/80
- Some of that 315 GWh includes water supply pumping for municipal use (classified differently in NEA categories)

## The Water-Energy-Food Nexus

Agriculture in Nepal is where the water-energy-food nexus is most visible:

- **75% of Nepal's water withdrawals** go to agriculture (irrigation), nearly all from surface sources
- **Only ~38% of irrigable land** is currently irrigated year-round (WB Water Sector Diagnostic)
- **Groundwater irrigation** is concentrated in the Terai, where 1.9 BCM is consumed vs 8.8 BCM total recharge — significant untapped potential
- **Storage hydropower** could enable seasonal irrigation releases that currently can't be scheduled without reservoirs — linking [[storage-deficit]] to food security

The [[budhigandaki]], [[west-seti]], and [[pancheshwar]] projects all have irrigation components in their design mandates, but none are currently under construction. The gap between potential multipurpose storage and actual irrigation delivery is one of Nepal's largest infrastructure underproviders.

## Growth Outlook

Agricultural energy demand is likely to grow from a very low base for several reasons:

1. **Irrigation expansion** — government targets to increase year-round irrigated area from ~38% to >60% by 2035
2. **Pump electrification** — tubewell diesel-to-electric switching driven by both reliability and cost
3. **Climate adaptation** — more frequent dry spells require more pumped irrigation to stabilize crop yields
4. **Mechanization** — tractors, threshers, and powered equipment replacing animal and manual labour as farm labour migrates to cities and abroad

The counter-force is agricultural employment decline — as people leave farming, the energy-per-worker may rise but total agricultural energy could plateau if cultivated area contracts.
