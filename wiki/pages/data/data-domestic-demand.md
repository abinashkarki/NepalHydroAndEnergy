# Domestic Electricity Demand Distribution

Where Nepal's electricity actually goes — by sector, by consumer category, and how fast it's growing. Central evidence for [[domestic-led-hydro-strategy]] and the [[seasonal-arbitrage-trap]] value-capture argument.

## Sectoral Demand Split — FY 2024/25 (NEA)

Total electricity sales: **11,288 GWh** (including internal consumption). Total consumers: **5,707,528**. Source: NEA Annual Report FY 2024/25, Distribution & Consumer Services Directorate.

| Category | Share of Consumers | Share of Sales | Share of Revenue | Sales (GWh approx.) |
|----------|------:|------:|------:|------:|
| Domestic (residential) | **91.08%** | **42.02%** | 39.05% | ~4,742 |
| Industrial | 1.23% | **36.64%** | 36.52% | ~4,136 |
| Commercial | 0.77% | 7.79% | 11.33% | ~879 |
| Non-Commercial | 0.72% | 3.07% | 4.47% | ~347 |
| Others (streetlight, irrigation, temp, etc.) | 6.21% | 10.48% | 8.62% | ~1,183 |

> [!finding] Industry is 1.23% of customers, 36.64% of consumption
> The revenue and sales shares are nearly identical (36.64% vs 36.52%), indicating industrial customers face average tariffs close to the system average (~NRs 9.44/kWh). Combined, commercial + industrial = 44.43% of sales from only 1.99% of customers. This concentration makes industrial load the highest-leverage demand lever: winning or losing a few large industrial users has an outsized system effect. See [[data-industrial-electricity-consumers]] for the sub-sector breakdown.

## Sectoral Shift — FY 2020/21 vs FY 2024/25

| Category | FY 2020/21 Sales | FY 2024/25 Sales | Change | CAGR |
|----------|------:|------:|------:|------:|
| Domestic | ~3,138 GWh (43.1%) | ~4,742 GWh (42.0%) | +51% | ~10.8% |
| Industrial | ~2,816 GWh (38.7%) | ~4,136 GWh (36.6%) | +47% | ~10.1% |
| Commercial | ~511 GWh (7.0%) | ~879 GWh (7.8%) | +72% | ~14.5% |
| Non-Commercial | ~204 GWh (2.8%) | ~347 GWh (3.1%) | +70% | ~14.2% |
| Others | ~614 GWh (8.4%) | ~1,183 GWh (10.5%) | +93% | ~17.8% |
| **Total** | **~7,277 GWh** | **~11,288 GWh** | **+55%** | **~11.6%** |

> [!note] Data comparison notes
> FY 2020/21 figures are from NEA Distribution and Consumer Services Directorate as cited in earlier wiki pages (sectoral shares applied to ~7,277 GWh sales). FY 2024/25 uses the same source methodology (NEA DCSD). The two data points span approximately 4 Nepali fiscal years. Per-capita consumption rose from ~370 to **465 kWh** over the same window.

Three pattern observations:

1. **Domestic and industrial grew at roughly the same pace** (~10-11% CAGR). This is notable given the WB CEM finding that manufacturing output *fell* to ~6% of GDP — implying industrial electricity growth may reflect process switching (electric vs thermal) and/or new capacity rather than manufacturing expansion.

2. **"Others" is the fastest-growing category** (+93%), driven by streetlight electrification, irrigation pump connections, and temporary construction supply — the long tail of grid extension.

3. **Domestic share is gently declining** (43.1% → 42.0%) while industrial share is also declining (38.7% → 36.6%). The growth is in commercial, non-commercial, and others — the smaller categories gaining share as electrification deepens beyond basic household connections. This is structurally healthy: it means the grid is reaching firms, institutions, and public infrastructure, not just homes.

### FY 2020/21 (for comparison)

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Total consumption | 7,319 GWh |
| NEA own generation share | 31.66% |
| IPP generation share | 36.51% |
| **Import from India share** | **31.83%** |
| System loss | 17.18% |

> [!warning] The import-to-exporter transformation is real but recent
> In FY 2020/21, Nepal imported nearly one-third of its electricity. By FY 2024/25, imports fell to 11% of available energy while exports hit 2,380 GWh — a structural shift driven by ~1,400+ MW of new IPP hydro commissioned in the intervening years. However, this net-export status is monsoon-weighted: dry-season imports persist at the margin. The transformation is fragile and depends on continued generation additions outpacing demand growth. System losses also improved from 17.18% (FY 2020/21) to **10.39%** at the distribution level in FY 2024/25 — a meaningful gain.

> [!note]
> At 465 kWh/capita, Nepal is still roughly **5–7× below** middle-income norms (2,000–3,000 kWh/yr) and **~60× below** its theoretical hydropower potential per capita (~29,400 kWh/yr). The gap between current consumption and a plausible electrified economy is the central demand-creation opportunity in [[domestic-led-hydro-strategy]].

## Load Profile — Evening Peak Dominates

Monthly operational reports show peak demand consistently hitting **18:30–19:25** — the daily cooking-and-lighting window. This pattern persists in FY 2024/25:

- **System peak of 2,901 MW** recorded on July 1, 2025 (13 Jestha 2082) — of which 2,214 MW was internal demand and 687 MW exported
- **National peak of 2,409 MW** recorded on June 10, 2025
- Winter peak hours still require import support: structural mismatch between RoR flat-ish output and evening spike

The structural features have not changed since FY 2020/21:

- Run-of-river hydro generates at near-constant output (flow-following)
- Demand spikes in the evening when people return home
- The mismatch is most acute in **dry-season evenings**, when hydro output is at its annual floor and demand at its daily peak

This evening peak pattern is what makes storage + solar the physical complement to the hydro fleet. See [[data-nepal-peak-load-curve-fy2024-25]] for the annual load shape.

## The Electrification Gaps — Where Demand Is Not Yet Created

### Electric cooking: the largest untapped demand

- Electric cooking used by **0.5% of households** (2021 WB estimate, up from 0.1% in 2011)
- Biomass (firewood) and **imported LPG** dominate cooking energy
- NEA's FY 2024/25 report explicitly flags government policy to "encourage the use of electricity for cooking, transportation, and industry to reduce reliance on fossil fuels"

The strategic implication: if Nepal could shift even 20–30% of cooking to electricity, it would create ~50–75 PJ of new electric demand — roughly **doubling** current electricity consumption. That is bigger than any plausible export contract. See [[energy-substitution-pathway]] for the full substitution logic.

### Residential electricity still dominated by lighting

- Household electricity use has tripled since 2010, but remains only ~5% of residential energy (the rest is biomass + LPG)
- 92.2% of households used electricity for lighting in 2021 (up from 67.3% in 2011) — lighting drove the first wave of residential electrification
- The second wave — cooking, heating, appliances — has barely started

## Value-Capture Framing

Electricity "captures value" when:
1. It sells scarce kWh at high value (dry-season firm power, peak hours)
2. It buys deficit kWh at low cost (off-peak imports when available)
3. It uses the bulk of output domestically in **high-productivity activities**

Nepal currently does (1) partially (improving IEX spreads) and (2) adequately in monsoon (exports at NPR 7.11/unit average) but still pays a winter scarcity tax. On (3) — the domestic transformation pathway — the electric cooking statistic (0.5% of households) captures the scale of the gap.

The deepest value-capture move is not better export contracts. It is **electrifying cooking, industrial heat, and transport** at scale — turning Nepal from a seasonal kWh exporter into a country where electricity is the backbone of economic productivity. See [[consumption-trajectory-outlook]] for the forward view.
