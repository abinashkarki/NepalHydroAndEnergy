# Industrial Electricity Consumers in Nepal

Which industries consume Nepal's electricity — and which ones burn coal and biomass instead. The companion page to [[data-domestic-demand]] and the industrial-demand half of [[energy-substitution-pathway]].

## Headline Numbers

| Metric | Value | Source |
|--------|-------|--------|
| Industrial electricity sales (FY 2024/25) | **~4,136 GWh** | NEA Annual Report |
| Share of total electricity sales | **36.64%** | NEA DCSD |
| Industrial consumer count | ~70,000 (1.23% of 5.7M) | NEA DCSD |
| Average per-industrial-consumer | ~59,000 kWh/yr | Calculated |
| Industrial total energy (all fuels, FY 2079/80) | **111,306 TJ (~30,918 GWh)** | WECS 2024 |
| Electricity share of industrial total energy | **11.6%** (12,874 TJ) | WECS 2024 |
| Industrial revenue from electricity (FY 2024/25) | NPR 39.88 billion (36.52% of total) | NEA DCSD |
| Outstanding industrial dues (FY 2024/25) | **NPR 29.42 billion** | NEA finance section |

> [!finding] Industry uses 11× more energy from non-electric sources than from the grid
> The 4,136 GWh of industrial electricity (~12,874 TJ) sits inside a total industrial energy consumption of 111,306 TJ. Coal + biomass + petroleum supply ~88.4% of industry's energy. The electrification of Nepal's industry — brick kilns, cement clinker, process heat — is still largely ahead of the country. See [[energy-substitution-pathway]] for the displacement strategy.

### Sum check
The above tiers sum to roughly 3,100–4,800 GWh, compared to the NEA-reported 4,136 GWh. The long tail of small industrial consumers — thousands of workshops, mills, and processors — fills the remaining gap.

> [!gap] NEA sub-sector data
> NEA does not disaggregate electricity sales by industrial sub-sector in any public document examined. The table above is constructed from (a) the WECS total-energy cross-tab showing which fuels go to which industrial activities; (b) known process-energy ratios for each industry; (c) rough plant counts and output volumes. **This is the single biggest data gap in the consumption profile.** Obtaining actual sub-sector GWh data from NEA Load Dispatch Centre or Department of Industry would significantly sharpen the industrial-demand argument.

## Structural Features of Nepal's Industrial Electricity Demand

### 1. It's a few large consumers, not a broad base

From the NEA consumer data: industrial consumers are **1.23% of all customers** but consume **36.64% of all electricity**. This means approximately 70,000 industrial connections serve an average of ~59,000 kWh/yr each — but the distribution is heavily skewed. The top ~50-100 consumers (cement plants, steel mills, large cold stores) likely account for 30-50% of total industrial consumption.

This concentration makes industrial demand the highest-leverage policy target: converting a single major brick kiln cluster or cement plant to grid electricity can shift the demand curve more than adding thousands of new household connections.

NEA explicitly recognizes this by categorizing top consumers as "Most Important Consumers" (MIC), "Very Important Consumers" (VIC), and "Important Consumers" (IC) with dedicated customer care.

### 2. Industry is underelectrified compared to peers

From the WB CEM: Nepal's industry consumed **~12,800 TJ of electricity in 2022** (IEA data), which was only **9% of final industry energy consumption**. By comparison:

| Country | Industry Electricity Share | Electricity Consumed (TJ) |
|---------|------:|------:|
| Bangladesh | 35% | ~147,000 |
| Nepal | 9% | ~12,800 |

Bangladesh's industry consumes ~11× more electricity despite a somewhat larger economy. The gap is not primarily size — it is electrification depth. Nepal's industry burns coal and biomass because the grid was historically unreliable (load shedding 2007-2017, 14 hours/day) and industrial tariffs didn't incentivize fuel-switching.

### 3. Captive diesel generation is a hidden consumption layer

The WECS cross-tab shows **~11,646 TJ of diesel** consumed in the industrial sector — equivalent to ~3,235 GWh of potential electricity demand if the grid were reliable. At NPR 130/L diesel and NPR 9.44/kWh average grid tariff, the per-kWh cost of captive diesel generation (~NPR 25–35/kWh) is **3–4× the grid price**. Yet firms pay it because outages still affect 76% of firms (WB CEM 2022 data).

This captive diesel load is the low-hanging fruit of industrial electrification. Each GWh displaced from diesel to grid electricity saves the firm ~NPR 15–25 million in fuel costs and saves Nepal the foreign exchange cost of importing that diesel.

### 4. Manufacturing has been shrinking, not growing

Manufacturing fell from **7.3% of GDP** (conflict era) to **5.7%** (repeated-shocks era). Construction overtook manufacturing as the largest industry subsector. This means:

- **Industrial electricity growth (~10% CAGR) is outpacing manufacturing GDP growth** — suggesting the growth comes from electrification of existing processes (diesel → electric switching, electric grinding replacing coal-based processes) rather than from new factories.
- If manufacturing *recovers*, industrial electricity demand could accelerate sharply — a positive-risk scenario for the demand-creation strategy.
- If manufacturing continues to shrink, industrial electricity growth may slow — a negative-risk scenario.

> [!warning] Dues are a structural concern
> Outstanding industrial dues (NPR 29.42 billion) represent ~74% of annual industrial revenue (NPR 39.88 billion). This suggests either legacy arrears from the load-shedding era or ongoing payment difficulties in certain industrial segments. Either way, it complicates the "build demand to absorb surplus" thesis — the customers you need most may not pay reliably.

## Industrial Consumption and Hydro

Hydro is 94.4% of installed capacity but only ~89% of available energy (imports supply 11% in FY 2024/25, mostly in dry season). For industrial consumers this means:

- **Monsoon period:** abundant hydro, low marginal cost, reliable supply — the ideal time for industrial production
- **Dry season:** hydro output drops, imports fill the gap at higher cost, and evening peaks strain the system — the reliability gap that caused 76% of firms to report outages in 2022

Industrial consumers that can **shift production seasonally** (e.g. food processing timed to harvest, construction materials for building season) benefit from the monsoon abundance. Industries that need **year-round continuous power** (cold storage, continuous-process cement, 24/7 steel mills) face the dry-season reliability gap and are the economic case for [[storage-deficit]] and [[solar-hydro-complementarity]] build-out.
