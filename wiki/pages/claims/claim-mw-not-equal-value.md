## Claim

**Installed megawatts**, **annual generation**, **seasonal output shape**, and **firm (dependable) power** are routinely **conflated** in Nepal's hydro discourse—producing misleading narratives about both "potential" and "solved" energy security.

## Evidence

- **Hermes:** Describes **category confusion** as the source of "a shocking amount of bad analysis"—especially when headline **potential** figures are read as if they were **dispatchable capacity** or near-term **delivery**.
- **Codex:** Frames Nepal as **resource-rich but not yet dispatch-rich**—large theoretical endowment does not translate into firm, tradeable, time-targeted power without storage, grid, markets, and institutions.
- **The slogan stack:** Public politics still tolerates juxtaposition of very large **~83,000 MW** "potential" rhetoric against **~3,591 MW** installed (order-of-magnitude gaps depending on definition) without clarifying that these belong to different accounting categories than **dry-season firm power** or **monetizable export capacity**.
- **Claude / Gemini:** Reinforce that **nameplate** is not **energy**, and **energy** is not **firm power** at the times the grid needs it most.

## Confidence rationale

**High** because the error mode is observable in policy speech and investor-facing materials, and the corrective distinction is standard power-systems literacy; provider threads independently flag the same epistemic failure.
