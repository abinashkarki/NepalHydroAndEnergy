## Claim

**Sediment** is a **central** hydropower constraint in Nepal—shaping design, outage risk, maintenance cycles, reservoir economics, and rebuild timelines—not a secondary operations-and-maintenance footnote.

## Evidence

- **Kali Gandaki / high-sediment exemplars (synthesis):** Monsoon concentrations on the order of **~50,000 ppm** sediment and very large annual sediment load narratives (**~65M tonnes/yr** in cited campaign-style summaries) illustrate why abrasion, fouling, and intake stability dominate RoR economics in certain gorges.
- **Turbine wear signal:** Reported rapid erosion example where turbine **clearance gap** moved **0.6 → 1.35 mm in one year**—a concrete mechanism linking sediment to efficiency loss, failure risk, and forced outages.
- **September 2024 flood episode (synthesis):** Damages across **11 projects** and **600+ MW** (as cited in provider research logs) connect extreme hydrometeorology to **project-level** operational fragility; sediment transport is part of that damage stack even when immediate cause is flood hydraulics.
- **Hermes / Codex / Gemini:** Agree sediment interacts with **buildability**, **insurability**, **reservoir life**, and **PPA bankability**—i.e., project finance, not just mechanics.

## Confidence rationale

**High** for the **structural** claim (sediment is first-order in multiple major basins and must be in the design/finance model). Specific ppm/load numbers vary by gauge, event, and citation chain—those should be treated as **indicative** unless pinned to a primary measurement table in wiki sources.

## Unresolved issues

- Need consistent wiki sourcing for **event lists** (which plants, verified MW offline, duration) vs headline aggregates.
- **Reservoir trap efficiency** and climate-driven landslide supply are active research fronts—design factors should be basin-specific ([[gandaki-basin]], [[koshi-basin]]).
