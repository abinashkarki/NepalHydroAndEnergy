## Claim

Nepal's **operating** hydro fleet is overwhelmingly **run-of-river (RoR)** and **peaking run-of-river (PRoR)**, with only a **tiny** share of true **seasonal storage** capacity.

## Evidence

- **Research ledger:** Composition snapshot cited in synthesis: **85.7% RoR**, **9.9% PRoR**, **3.7% storage**—a fleet structurally aligned with monsoon energy and misaligned with multi-month dry-season firm delivery.
- **World Bank and source review:** Multiple threads describe **>90%** RoR+PRoR in operational or planning discourse, differing slightly by classification boundaries but agreeing on the dominant RoR/PRoR reality.
- **Fleet fact pattern:** [[kulekhani-cascade]] (**106 MW**, roughly **~85–150 MCM** depending on active vs gross storage framing) is repeatedly singled out as the main **true storage** anchor in public summaries, underscoring how small true pondage/storage is relative to installed nameplate across the system.

## Confidence rationale

**High** because the qualitative claim (RoR/PRoR dominance, minimal true storage) is stable across provider syntheses and matches standard Nepali power-sector descriptions; minor numeric differences (e.g., 85–90% bands) reflect classification and dataset vintage, not disagreement on the structural point.

## Unresolved issues

- **Definition sensitivity:** What counts as PRoR vs daily pondage vs "storage" varies by license, design, and reporting line in [[nea]] statistics—headline percentages should be tied to an explicit taxonomy when used in formal models.
- **Under-construction vs operating:** The claim is about the **operating** fleet; pipeline projects could change the mix slowly unless large storage comes online.
