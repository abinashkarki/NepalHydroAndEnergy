# NEA Structural Separation

## The Intervention

Separate the Nepal Electricity Authority's three conflicting roles into independent institutions through three specific legal/regulatory actions:

1. **Carve NLDC into an Independent System Operator (ISO).** Amend the Electricity Act to transfer the National Load Dispatch Centre out of NEA and into a legally independent, board-governed ISO. The ISO would control dispatch decisions free of NEA's commercial interest in its own generation fleet. Until legislative amendment passes, executive action could establish a ring-fenced dispatch unit with transparent, published merit-order criteria.

2. **Transfer AD penalty authority to the Electricity Regulatory Commission (ERC).** Amend the ERC Act or relevant bylaws to make the ERC — not NEA — the entity that calculates, levies, and adjudicates Availability Declaration penalties. NEA would retain the role of off-taker but would no longer be the simultaneous prosecutor and judge of its own counterparties.

3. **Create an open-access framework.** Promulgate the draft Open Access policy (anticipated since at least 2022) allowing IPPs to sell to third parties: industrial consumers, export markets, and trading companies. This would end the single-buyer monopoly that makes every IPP a captive seller to NEA.

## Theory of Change

The NEA Triple-Authority Conflict ([[nea-triple-authority]]) is not an edge-case governance flaw — it is the institutional mechanism that converts Nepal's hydrological advantage into stranded assets and retail investor losses. When NLDC curtails a project for "grid security," NEA simultaneously stops the project's revenue as buyer and can penalise it for not generating as penalty administrator. The Barahi Hydropower case ([[barahi-hydropower]]) documents this double-bind operating even against an ERC statutory exemption.

Structural separation unlocks value by:
- Removing the built-in incentive to favour NEA-subsidiary generation (34% of national output) over IPP generation (55%) in dispatch decisions
- Creating a neutral penalty adjudicator, ending the metrological impossibility of penalising RoR projects for failing to predict monsoon hydrology 30 days ahead
- Enabling corporate PPAs and third-party sales, which would allow industrial consumers to contract directly with IPPs — creating a price-discovery mechanism that the single-buyer model suppresses

Without separation, every additional MW of IPP capacity faces the same structural risk: NEA can order it off-line and fine it for being off-line in the same transaction. ## Current Status

**PARTIALLY VERIFIED:**
- The 2003 World Bank Nepal power sector review recommended eliminating conflicts of interest in NEA's structure. No legislation has been enacted in the 23 years since.
- A draft Open Access policy exists and has been discussed since at least 2022. No promulgation date has been announced.
- The ERC has demonstrated willingness to exercise regulatory authority — ERC Bylaws 2076 created an AD penalty exemption for sub-10 MW projects. But the Barahi case proves the ERC cannot enforce its own bylaws against NEA's control of cash disbursement.

**UNVERIFIED:**
- Whether any parliamentary committee has formally investigated dispatch discrimination
- Whether any PPA contains an explicit clause exempting IPPs from AD penalties when curtailment is ordered by NEA/NLDC
- The exact statutory mechanism by which AD penalties are retained by NEA (rather than remitted to an independent fund)

**No legislation implementing any of the three separation actions has been enacted.**

The Fenner School's independent academic audit (ANU) has verified the institutional bias: *"The major complaint of the rising IPPs is that the playing field is not level, that NEA as generator, transmitter and distributor gives preferential terms to its own projects compared to IPPs."*

## What This Unlocks

- **End of uncompensated curtailment.** An independent ISO would make dispatch decisions on technical merit, not commercial self-interest. NLDC curtailment decisions affecting billions of rupees in annual IPP revenue would be subject to published criteria and regulatory oversight.
- **Fair penalty enforcement.** Transferring AD authority to the ERC would create a neutral forum where projects can challenge penalties. The Barahi case — where a statutory exemption was overridden because NEA controls the cash — would become structurally impossible.
- **Price discovery through open access.** Industrial consumers paying NPR 10.00–10.20/kWh at peak could contract directly with IPPs whose blended PPA rate is NPR ~5.52–6.20/kWh, creating margin for both parties and reducing NEA's near-monopoly pricing power.
- **IPP bankability.** Removing the structural risk of uncompensated curtailment and unaccountable penalties would reduce the risk premium priced into hydro project finance. At the DSCR break-point of ~82–84% of contracted energy ([[q-design-discharge]]), every percentage point of revenue protected from penalty clawback directly improves bankability.

**Quantified impact: UNVERIFIED.** No independent economic modelling of structural separation's NPV for Nepal's power sector exists in the wiki. The 37.1% revenue advantage Chilime enjoys over comparable private IPPs through its subsidiary PPA architecture ([[chilime]]) provides a directional benchmark: a 20-25% reduction in the effective revenue gap between NEA-subsidiary and IPP generation would represent tens of billions of NPR in redistributed value.

## Research Gaps

- **PPA clause text.** The most important unresolved legal question: does any standard PPA contain an exemption for curtailment-ordered shortfalls? Primary PPA text has not been accessed. Resolving this would confirm or refute the double-bind's legal basis.
- **NLDC dispatch data.** No public, project-level dispatch logs exist. Without them, claims of dispatch discrimination against IPPs remain inferential (from the Fenner School audit and the Chilime PPA rate comparison), not directly data-proven.
- **ISO legislative pathway.** Which specific clauses of the Electricity Act 1992 require amendment? Has any draft bill been tabled in parliament? These questions are unanswered in the wiki.
- **AD penalty aggregate volume.** The 16% of revenue claim is UNVERIFIED. NEA's own records of penalties deducted, by project and fiscal year, would resolve this.

## Political Feasibility

**Who benefits:** Private IPPs, their lenders (NRB-regulated BFIs carrying ~4-5% hydro exposure), industrial consumers seeking lower-cost power, retail investors in hydro IPOs/rights shares who currently bear penalty risk without disclosure.

**Who resists:** NEA management and the NEA employee union — the triple-authority is NEA's institutional power. NEA staff are also significant shareholders in Chilime and its subsidiary cascade, benefiting directly from the PPA rate advantage documented in [[chilime]]. The Ministry of Energy may resist loss of direct control over a major state-owned enterprise. Political patrons of NEA procurement may resist transparency in dispatch decisions.

**Realistic path:** Nepal has had 18+ governments in 30 years. A comprehensive legislative overhaul (Electricity Act amendment + ERC Act amendment + open-access promulgation) is improbable in a single government's tenure. A more realistic sequence:
1. **Executive action first:** Ring-fence NLDC within NEA with published merit-order criteria — achievable without legislation
2. **ERC capacity-building:** Fund and staff ERC to prepare for penalty adjudication authority — requires budget commitment, not law reform
3. **Legislative sequencing:** Open-access regulation (simplest, most precedent internationally) → ISO legislation → penalty transfer

The window of a majority government (current post-2024) is the most favourable in a decade for structural reform. The risk is that the political cycle turns before any component is enacted.
