# Electric Cooking Transition

## The Intervention

Design and implement a coordinated cooking electrification programme with four integrated components:

1. **Appliance financing for induction cookers.** A public or blended-finance facility providing concessional loans or hire-purchase for induction cookers targeting peri-urban and grid-connected rural households. Scale: aiming to raise electric cooking penetration from the current **~0.5% of households** toward 20–30% over a decade. Target areas: municipalities and grid-connected corridors where distribution infrastructure is adequate.

2. **NEA time-of-day tariff redesign for cooking load.** Restructure residential tariffs to create an off-peak window (e.g., 11:00–16:00) with a discounted rate that makes induction cooking cost-competitive with subsidised LPG. The current industrial ToD tariff splits peak (NPR 10.00–10.20/kWh, 17:00–23:00) from off-peak (NPR 4.65–5.25/kWh). A similar residential cooking tariff tier could target the midday window, avoiding the 18:30–19:25 evening peak where the system is already capacity-constrained.

3. **LPG subsidy reform.** Nepal's LPG subsidy makes imported gas artificially cheap — undercutting electric cooking on marginal-cost terms despite hydropower being domestically produced. A phased rebalancing: gradually reduce the LPG subsidy while commensurately reducing the effective cost of induction cooking through the tariff and financing mechanisms above. The freed fiscal resources can partially fund the appliance financing facility.

4. **Grid reliability improvement in peri-urban areas.** Electric cooking is not adopted when the grid is unreliable. Feeder-level upgrades, transformer deployment, and reduced SAIDI/SAIFI in the target areas must precede or accompany cooking electrification — or the programme will fail on first implementation.

## Theory of Change

Nepal's energy problem is not that it lacks electricity — it's that it produces enormous monsoon surplus (700–800 MW of daily spill during wet season, [[stranded-generation]]) while importing expensive winter power and burning imported LPG for cooking. The [[energy-substitution-pathway]] identifies cooking electrification as the **single largest demand lever**: biomass (fuelwood) at 257 PJ and LPG at 23.7 PJ together dominate residential energy. Shifting 20–30% of cooking load to electricity would create ~50–75 PJ of new electric demand — roughly doubling current electricity consumption. This is bigger than any plausible export contract.

The intervention creates a virtuous cycle:
- **Absorbs monsoon surplus domestically.** Every induction cooker that operates during the day or early evening uses electricity that would otherwise be spilled, curtailed, or exported at NPR ~7/kWh. Domestic absorption builds productive infrastructure rather than exiting the economy as a commodity transaction.
- **Displaces imported LPG.** LPG (23.7 PJ, concentrated in urban/peri-urban kitchens) is almost entirely imported. Every kWh that displaces LPG saves foreign exchange — the import-substitution value far exceeds the per-kWh export realisation ([[seasonal-arbitrage-trap]]).
- **Creates baseload for the hydro fleet.** Cooking load is predictable, schedulable, and partially shiftable. Evening cooking (18:30–19:25) coincides with the daily peak, which is a problem. But midday cooking (a significant share in Nepali household patterns) aligns with solar generation and off-peak hydro availability. A time-of-day tariff can steer load toward system-surplus hours.
- **Enables the domestic-led strategy.** The [[domestic-led-hydro-strategy]] argues that Nepal should build generation for domestic dry-season reliability first. Cooking electrification is the largest single domestic demand pool that makes that strategy numerically credible — without it, "domestic demand" remains a fraction of the export-ambition scale.

## Current Status

**VERIFIED:**
- Electric cooking penetration: **~0.5% of households** (2021 WB estimate, up from 0.1% in 2011). 92.2% of households use electricity for lighting, but cooking remains dominated by biomass and imported LPG. See [[data-domestic-demand]].
- Residential electricity is 42% of NEA sales (~4,742 GWh in FY 2024/25), and the sector grew at 10.8% CAGR. But this is overwhelmingly lighting and appliances, not cooking.
- NEA's FY 2024/25 report explicitly flags government policy to "encourage the use of electricity for cooking, transportation, and industry to reduce reliance on fossil fuels."
- Per-capita consumption: **465 kWh/year** (FY 2024/25), 5–7× below middle-income norms of 2,000–3,000 kWh.
- Seasonal monsoon surplus: 700–800 MW spilled/curtailed daily during peak wet season. Nepal exported NPR 17.46 billion in FY 2024/25 against imports of NPR 12.92 billion (net surplus ~NPR 4.5 billion).
- LPG: ~23.7 PJ of imported cooking fuel, concentrated in urban/peri-urban households, undercutting electric cooking on marginal cost.

**PARTIALLY VERIFIED:**
- NEA has a ToD tariff structure for industrial consumers. Whether any residential ToD tariff for cooking has been formally proposed or piloted is not documented in the wiki.

**UNVERIFIED:**
- No Nepal-specific cooking load timing profile study exists in the wiki. Understanding the temporal distribution of Nepali cooking energy demand (breakfast, midday, evening shares; seasonal variation; urban vs. rural patterns) is critical to sizing the tariff, financing, and grid impact of this intervention.
- The fiscal cost of LPG subsidy and the precise per-unit LPG subsidy rate are not documented.
- The current market price range of induction cookers in Nepal, distribution channels, and consumer financing availability.
- Grid reliability metrics (SAIDI/SAIFI) for the peri-urban areas that would be targeted for cooking electrification.

## What This Unlocks

- **Domestic demand at export-relevant scale.** Shifting 20–30% of cooking to electricity creates ~50–75 PJ of new demand — roughly doubling current electricity consumption. For comparison, Nepal's total electricity export was 2,380 GWh (~8.6 PJ) in FY 2024/25. Cooking electrification at 20% penetration creates 6–9× more domestic demand than the entire current export volume.
- **Foreign exchange savings.** LPG is 100% imported. At the energy substitution rate implied by induction efficiency (>80% vs. LPG ~40–50%), each GWh of electric cooking displaces roughly 2–2.5 GWh of LPG primary energy, saving NPR tens of billions in annual petroleum imports.
- **Monsoon surplus absorption.** 50–75 PJ of annual cooking demand, even if used year-round, provides a permanent domestic offtake for energy that currently faces curtailment or low-priced export. The load is inherently distributed — every connected household is a potential demand node — reducing the transmission dependency that constrains industrial offtake.
- **Health and environment co-benefits.** Indoor air pollution from biomass cooking kills an estimated 20,000+ Nepalis annually (WHO estimate). Electrification eliminates this entirely for converted households. Forest degradation from fuelwood collection is reduced.
- **Women's time and labour.** Fuelwood collection and biomass cooking are overwhelmingly performed by women. Electrification directly reduces this unpaid labour burden.

## Research Gaps

- **Nepal-specific cooking load timing profile.** No study exists in the wiki documenting when Nepali households cook, for how long, and with what seasonal variation. Without this, the tariff design and grid impact assessment are speculative. **This is the most important research gap for this intervention.**
- **Induction cooker cost and distribution.** What is the current market price range? What financing products exist (if any)? What is the supply chain from import to rural retailer?
- **LPG subsidy quantum.** The exact per-cylinder subsidy rate and total fiscal cost are needed to model the fiscal space for reallocation to electric cooking incentives.
- **Peri-urban grid reliability.** SAIDI/SAIFI data for target municipalities would determine the minimum reliability investment envelope before cooking electrification can succeed.
- **Consumer behaviour research.** Why have Nepali households not adopted electric cooking when 92% already have electricity for lighting? Is it cost, reliability, appliance access, cooking culture (induction vs. gas flame for traditional foods), or information? Primary survey data would resolve this.
- **Peak load impact modelling.** What happens to the 18:30–19:25 evening peak if even 10% of households shift cooking to electricity? Does the grid have the headroom, or does cooking electrification require concurrent storage/BESS deployment?

## Political Feasibility

**Who benefits:** Urban and peri-urban households (reduced cooking fuel costs over time); the national treasury (reduced LPG import bill, improved balance of payments); NEA (a new, large, domestic customer base that absorbs surplus); the Ministry of Energy (visible domestic electrification achievement); women (reduced indoor air pollution and fuelwood labour).

**Who resists:** LPG importers and distributors (whose business model is threatened); the Ministry of Finance (who must find budget for appliance subsidies and may resist LPG subsidy reform that is politically sensitive); potentially NEA's distribution function (added load on already-stressed urban feeders).

**Realistic path:** This intervention has medium political feasibility — higher than structural separation or SEBON reform because it offers visible benefits to voters (cheaper cooking, cleaner kitchens) rather than abstract institutional restructuring. But obstacles are real:
1. **LPG subsidy is politically entrenched.** Any attempt to reduce it triggers consumer protests. The reform must be carefully sequenced: electric cooking must be demonstrably cheaper and more reliable *before* the LPG subsidy is cut, not after.
2. **Appliance cost is a real barrier.** An induction cooker costs NPR 3,000–8,000 [UNVERIFIED: market survey needed] — significant for low-income households. Financing mechanisms need subsidy support in early years.
3. **Grid reliability is the silent blocker.** If households buy induction cookers and the power cuts out at dinner time, the programme loses all credibility. Reliability improvement must come first, which requires NEA distribution investment.
4. **Time-of-day tariff complexity.** Residential ToD metering requires smart meters or at minimum time-segmented billing — infrastructure NEA does not currently have at residential scale.

The most realistic near-term path: a geographically-targeted pilot in 2–3 municipalities with adequate distribution infrastructure, combining appliance financing + promotional tariff + reliability investment, generating data that can inform national scale-up. The pilot approach can survive political churn by being municipally rather than centrally anchored. This page flags the specific evidence gap — no Nepal cooking load timing profile study exists — that should inform pilot design.
