# Transmission Completion

## The Intervention

Complete two transmission infrastructure priorities with specific mechanisms:

1. **Hetauda–Dhalkebar 400 kV (134 km, 795 towers).** As of late April 2026, the line is 98.24% complete — only **14 towers remain unbuilt**, all in Wards 15, 16, and 17 of Hetauda Sub-metropolitan City (the Hatiya area). The intervention is to execute the mediated compensation agreement (brokered by the Energy Minister, late April 2026), complete the remaining 14 towers under the security arrangement already deployed, and energise the line. Target completion: mid-July 2026.

2. **New Butwal–Gorakhpur 400 kV cross-border.** Complete the second 400 kV cross-border interconnection to India. This corridor creates the physical capacity for Nepal's export ambitions to be realised — the current ~1,141 MW of approved cross-border capacity (primarily through the single Dhalkebar–Muzaffarpur 400 kV link) lags far behind installed generation of 3,591 MW and growing.

Supporting mechanism: apply the December 2025 land-use regulation amendment (permitting subdivision of land under transmission RoW with limited compensation) systematically to all future transmission corridor conflicts, reducing the reoccurrence of Hetauda-style blockades.

## Theory of Change

Transmission is the #1 immediate monetisation bottleneck in Nepal's energy system ([[claim-transmission-immediate-blocker]]). The 400 kV east-west backbone — essential for moving power from western basins to eastern load centres and cross-border points — has been incomplete for over a decade. The consequence is not abstract: **700–800 MW of zero-carbon monsoon electricity is spilled or curtailed daily** because the grid cannot move it.

The Hetauda–Dhalkebar section is the most dramatic bottleneck in the entire system:
- **Only 14 towers remain** out of 795. This is not a construction or financing failure — it is a pure community-resistance failure. The project has been under construction since 2013 (original target: 2016).
- **The cost of this single bottleneck is staggering:** NEA estimates **NPR 5–6 billion per year** in direct losses from power that cannot be evacuated. Over the multi-year delay, the cumulative loss runs into tens of billions of NPR — more than the cost of the line itself.
- **Completion would be transformative.** The Hetauda–Dhalkebar segment is the keystone of the 400 kV east-west backbone. When operational (combined with the already-completed Dhalkebar–Inaruwa 154 km eastern section), the full 288 km corridor provides the domestic grid architecture to move power from generation-surplus to demand-surplus regions.

The Butwal–Gorakhpur cross-border line addresses the export capacity dimension. Nepal's single 400 kV cross-border link (Dhalkebar–Muzaffarpur) carries ~1,141 MW of approved export. With installed capacity at 3,591 MW and growing, export ambition is limited not by generation but by corridor hardware. The second 400 kV cross-border line roughly doubles export capacity.

Without these corridors, the logic of [[stranded-generation]] continues: more RoR MW commissioned → more monsoon surplus → more curtailment → more IPP financial distress → more retail investor losses. Transmission completion breaks this cycle at its physical root.

## Current Status

**VERIFIED:**
- **Hetauda–Dhalkebar:** 134 km section is 98.24% complete. Of 795 towers, 781 are built. Exactly 14 towers remain unbuilt: 2 in Ward 15, 10 in Ward 16, 2 in Ward 17 (Hatiya area, Hetauda Sub-metropolitan City, Makwanpur). **Construction resumed April 23, 2026** under heavy police security, following a high-level mediation agreement orchestrated by the Energy Minister. Government target: mid-July 2026 completion.
- **Community resistance nature:** Demands for rerouting away from dense settlements, fair land compensation for RoW, and unaddressed electromagnetic field (EMF) health risk concerns. No major political group identified — appears community-driven. A district-level compensation evaluation committee has been formed under the Chief District Officer (CDO). Initial compensation payments were made in 2018 but some residents refused them.
- **Delay cost:** NEA estimates NPR 5–6 billion per year in operational losses. 700–800 MW of monsoon surplus cannot be evacuated.
- **Institutional reform:** December 2025 land-use regulation amendment permits subdivision of land under transmission RoW with limited compensation — specifically designed to ease future such conflicts.

**VERIFIED (from [[claim-transmission-immediate-blocker]]):**
- **Dhalkebar–Inaruwa:** 400 kV, 154 km — Operational (June 2024). Eastern backbone complete.
- **Dhalkebar–Bara:** 400 kV, 100 km — Completed (March 2025). Operating at 132 kV via tapping; ~200 MW, upgradeable to 400 MW.
- **New Butwal–Bardaghat:** 220 kV, 21.5 km — Operational (December 2024). Creates 220 kV backbone from Butwal to Hetauda (~1,200 MW capacity).
- **Marsyangdi Corridor:** 220 kV, 68 km — 65% complete (target was May 2025; delayed).
- **Damauli–Bharatpur:** 220 kV, 33 km — 76% complete (March 2026).

**VERIFIED (comparable case):**
- The **Bharatpur–Bardaghat 220 kV line** (World Bank-funded) faced similar community resistance and was resolved in 2023 through the World Bank's Dispute Resolution Service — a mediated settlement between community and government enabling line completion. This is a proven resolution model for Nepal's transmission conflicts.

## What This Unlocks

- **Hetauda–Dhalkebar completion (~July 2026):**
  - Operational 400 kV east-west backbone, connecting western generation to eastern load centres and the Dhalkebar–Muzaffarpur cross-border point
  - End of the NPR 5–6 billion/year operational loss from transmission-constrained curtailment
  - 700–800 MW of currently stranded monsoon generation becomes evacuable — directly addressing [[stranded-generation]]
  - The line's completion enables additional generation in western basins (Karnali, Gandaki) to reach markets, removing a binding constraint on the Phase 2 pipeline in [[twenty-year-strategy]]

- **Butwal–Gorakhpur completion:**
  - Roughly doubles Nepal's 400 kV cross-border export capacity
  - Creates a second independent India interconnection, reducing single-point-of-failure risk on Dhalkebar–Muzaffarpur
  - Enables the export volumes contemplated in Phase 2 (2030–2037) of the [[twenty-year-strategy]]
  - Strengthens Nepal's negotiating position in bilateral electricity trade by providing physical alternatives

- **Systemic unlock:**
  - The December 2025 land-use regulation amendment, if systematically applied, provides a legal template for resolving future community-resistance conflicts before they become multi-year blockades
  - The Bharatpur–Bardaghat resolution (World Bank DRS, 2023) provides a dispute-resolution mechanism model that can be institutionalised

**Quantified:**
- NPR 5–6 billion/year in direct avoided losses from Hetauda–Dhalkebar completion alone
- 700–800 MW of additional monsoon generation evacuation capacity
- ~500–700 GWh of additional annual export potential (based on 700 MW × ~3 monsoon months × 24 hours × ~40% effective utilisation — rough directional estimate; seasonal and operational factors determine the precise figure)

## Political Feasibility

**Who benefits:** NEA (NPR 5–6 billion/year in avoided operational losses); IPPs (end of curtailment for projects whose output the completed backbone can now evacuate); industrial consumers (improved grid reliability for east-west power flows); export revenue (additional cross-border capacity for monsoon surplus); the national treasury (reduced petroleum imports if additional domestic electricity displaces diesel/LPG).

**Who resists:** The 14-tower communities in Hetauda Wards 15–17 have already been compensated (2018) and the April 2026 agreement appears to have resolved remaining objections — but community resistance could reignite if the compensation evaluation committee's work is perceived as inadequate. Future corridor projects will face similar dynamics in new geographies.

**Realistic path: HIGH feasibility for Hetauda–Dhalkebar, MEDIUM for Butwal–Gorakhpur.**

**Hetauda–Dhalkebar:** The intervention is already in execution. With only 14 towers remaining, construction resumed under police security, and a mediated compensation agreement in place, the mid-July 2026 target is physically achievable. The primary risk is re-escalation of community resistance if compensation payments stall or if the EMF health concerns are not addressed transparently. This is the closest thing to a "sure win" in Nepal's energy infrastructure landscape — the cost of failure is immense (NPR 5–6 billion/year), the remaining work is tiny (14 towers), and the political will is demonstrated (Energy Minister personal mediation, police deployment).

**Butwal–Gorakhpur:** Cross-border corridors face dual approval requirements (Nepali and Indian regulatory processes). The construction status is "under construction" but the timeline to completion is less certain than Hetauda–Dhalkebar. Indian regulatory coordination and any emergent community resistance on the Nepali side are the primary risks.

**Institutional lesson:** The Bharatpur–Bardaghat resolution (World Bank DRS, 2023) and the December 2025 land-use regulation amendment represent institutional learning. Nepal now has both a dispute resolution model (third-party mediation) and a legal tool (land subdivision under RoW) that should prevent future transmission conflicts from becoming decade-long blockades. The key question is whether these tools are systematically applied to the Marsyangdi corridor (65% complete) and Damauli–Bharatpur (76% complete) before they encounter Hetauda-style resistance.
