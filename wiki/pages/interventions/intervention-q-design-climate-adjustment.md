# Q-Design Climate Adjustment

## The Intervention

Issue a formal directive from the Department of Electricity Development (DoED) mandating that all new hydropower project feasibility studies submitted for licensing must incorporate climate-adjusted hydrological decrement factors based on the **ICIMOD "Guideline for Climate Resilient Hydropower in Nepal"** (published March 11, 2024).

Specifically:
1. **Revise Article 9 of the "Guidelines for Study of Hydropower Projects 2018"** to require — not merely permit — the use of bias-corrected General Circulation Model (GCM) projections, multi-pathway (SSP245, SSP585) climate scenarios, and SWAT/SWAT+ hydrological routing in all feasibility studies for projects above a specified threshold (e.g., 10 MW).
2. **Retire the WECS/DHM 1990 methodology** as the default for ungauged tributary hydrology estimation — ending the continued reliance on empirical regression equations derived from 1970s and 1980s data that assume hydrological stationarity.
3. **Require a climate-adjusted Q-design parameter** that applies a forward-looking decrement factor to the historical flow-duration curve, producing a design discharge that reflects projected dry-season flow declines — not the historical average.
4. **Align licensing rules with multilateral lender requirements** — all three major MDBs (World Bank, ADB, IFC) already mandate climate-adjusted hydrology as a non-negotiable condition for project financing. DoED's failure to mandate equivalent standards creates a two-tier system where mega-projects (Upper Arun, Dudh Koshi) use modern methods but domestically-financed mid-tier projects use 1990 methods.

## Theory of Change

The Q-design discharge parameter ([[q-design-discharge]]) mechanically determines both turbine sizing and the PPA contracted energy obligation. When Q-design is set using the WECS/DHM 1990 methodology — which assumes stationarity — the PPA promises the river will deliver what historical data projects, not what a warming climate will actually provide.

This is not a theoretical risk. The Urja Khabar audit of 100+ projects found **zero projects exceeded 80% of contracted energy** except Chilime. The DSCR break-point analysis shows projects fall below the 1.0x covenant threshold when actual generation drops below ~82–84% of contracted energy ([[q-design-discharge]]). The regulatory system is currently licensing projects into structural financial default — and retail investors buying IPO and rights shares are not told this.

Climate adjustment fixes the root cause, not the symptom:
- **Upstream fix:** Correct the Q-design parameter before the PPA is signed, rather than managing the consequences (AD penalties, DSCR breaches, loan restructuring) after the project inevitably underperforms.
- **Downstream unlock:** A climate-adjusted Q-design → realistic contracted energy → bankable project finance → fewer distressed IPOs and rights share bailouts → a healthier hydro sector that attracts rather than traps capital.
- **Systemic unlock:** If all new projects use climate-adjusted design, the aggregate contracted energy of the fleet becomes a realistic planning target rather than a fiction, enabling credible national energy planning.

The intervention also captures the full value of the ICIMOD guideline — which extends beyond hydrology to GLOF risk, sediment loading, and permafrost degradation — making project-level hazard design climate-rational rather than historically anchored.

## Current Status

**VERIFIED:**
- ICIMOD's "Guideline for Climate Resilient Hydropower in Nepal" was formally published on **March 11, 2024**, following basin-level modelling by NDRI in collaboration with ICIMOD and WECS (September 2023 – March 2024).
- The 2024 guideline explicitly assigns a policy target to DoED: *"Revise and implement the DoED Guidelines for Study of Hydropower Projects by incorporating additions proposed in this Guideline."* This revision remains unexecuted as of mid-2026.
- DoED's governing document remains the "Guidelines for Study of Hydropower Projects 2018" with Q40–Q45 design requirements under Article 9. The 4th amendment added active storage requirements (15 days of design discharge, 35% dry-season energy minimum) but did not mandate forward-looking climate modelling.
- The World Bank (Decision Tree Framework), ADB (Climate Risk and Vulnerability Assessment at concept stage), and IFC (life-cycle climate risk screening per revised Performance Standards) all mandate climate-adjusted hydrology for financed projects.
- Post-2022 mega-projects — Upper Arun 1,040 MW ESIA (January 2024), Dudh Koshi 635 MW assessments — demonstrate clear integration of climate-adjusted hydrology, abandoning static Q40 methods for dynamic risk models.
- NRB has amended unified directives requiring BFIs to report on climate exposures of borrowers (early-stage ESG integration).

**PARTIALLY VERIFIED:**
- The DoED 5th amendment (Ashadh 2082 / mid-2025) has been published but does not include climate-decrement factors.

**UNVERIFIED:**
- Whether any purely domestically-financed mid-tier project (10–50 MW, using local bank capital without MDB involvement) has voluntarily adopted the ICIMOD 2024 guideline. Current evidence strongly suggests they are adhering to minimum Q40/Q45 DoED standards to reduce upfront CAPEX and expedite licensing.
- The exact internal bureaucratic timeline for DoED revision of Article 9, including any draft circulars or working group minutes.
- The specific political-economy dynamics blocking adoption — domestic IPP lobby resistance to higher upfront feasibility costs is the most likely factor but has not been directly sourced.

## What This Unlocks

- **More accurate PPA contracting.** If Q-design is set at climate-realistic levels, contracted energy targets become achievable — reducing the systemic DSCR breach currently affecting the IPP sector.
- **More resilient system planning.** With realistic fleet-level generation projections, the twenty-year strategy's Phase 2-3 buildout assumptions ([[twenty-year-strategy]]) become grounded in achievable output rather than stationary-hydrology projections.
- **Reduced stranded asset creation.** Every project licensed today on 1990 hydrology assumptions that underperforms by 20–40% against contract creates a future distressed asset. Climate adjustment prevents this at the licensing stage.
- **Alignment with international finance.** Formal adoption would allow domestically-financed projects to meet MDB due diligence standards, reducing the current two-tier system where only mega-projects get climate-resilient design.
- **Dry-season output more predictable.** With GCM-driven estimates of declining dry-season baseflow (see [[peak-water]] and the Karnali decomposition showing snowmelt at 24% of annual flow), grid operators can plan winter import requirements more accurately.

## Political Feasibility

**Who benefits:** The national planning apparatus (WECS, NRB, Ministry of Finance — who underwrite the systemic risk of a distressed hydro portfolio); MDBs (whose standards would be aligned with domestic regulation, reducing transaction costs); future IPP investors whose capital would be deployed against realistic generation targets rather than regulatory fictions; retail investors who would not be sold shares in projects structurally incapable of meeting PPA targets.

**Who resists:** The domestic IPP lobby and their engineering consultancy ecosystem. Climate-adjusted feasibility studies require:
- Upfront capital expenditure for advanced climate modelling (~3–5× current feasibility study costs, based on MDB project experience)
- Potentially smaller authorised mechanical capacities (lower Q-design → smaller turbines → lower headline MW → less attractive to retail IPO marketing)
- Specialised geospatial modelling capability that concentrated domestic consultancies may not possess

The political economy is a classic short-term cost vs. long-term benefit trade-off, but the costs are concentrated (on IPPs and consultants developing projects now) while the benefits are diffuse (financial system stability, realistic national planning). The domestic banking sector, carrying 4–5% hydro exposure with rising NPLs, should in theory support the reform — but NRB forbearance ([[q-design-discharge]]) means banks have not yet been forced to recognise their hydro book's true distress.

**Realistic path:** The reform does not require legislation — a DoED directive amending Article 9 of the 2018 guidelines is administratively executable. The blocking force is political will, not legal pathway. Two near-term levers:
1. **NRB can force the issue from the financial side.** If NRB mandates climate-adjusted hydrology as a condition of hydro project lending (extending its existing climate-exposure directive), the banking system becomes the enforcement mechanism for what DoED will not mandate.
2. **MDB conditionalities already create a two-tier system.** As mega-projects backed by MDBs increasingly use ICIMOD methods, the regulatory disparity becomes politically visible — and domestically-financed projects' physical underperformance becomes harder to attribute to "bad luck" rather than obsolete methodology.

The 18+ governments in 30 years reality applies: administrative action under the current government is more probable than legislative embedding. But the DoED Directive pathway is administratively achievable within a single ministerial term if the political will exists.
