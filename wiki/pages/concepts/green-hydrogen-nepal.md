# Green Hydrogen Nepal

**Green hydrogen** in Nepal is best understood as a **bridge technology** between **surplus monsoon electricity** and **hard-to-electrify domestic industry**—not as a export commodity fantasy disconnected from grid physics. Electrolysis converts **low-utilization wet-season kWh** into **storable molecules** (hydrogen, then ammonia or other carriers), reshaping the economics of [[seasonal-arbitrage-trap]] by **absorbing energy when the system is long** and delivering **products** when the economy needs them.

## Why fertilizer is the natural first anchor

Nepal **imports essentially all** chemical fertilizer; food security and farm productivity are therefore **foreign-exchange and supply-chain dependent**. A **domestic ammonia** pathway—hydrogen → Haber-Bosch → urea/ammonium products—ties directly to **sovereignty** and **base-load demand**. Order-of-magnitude sector sketches in national roadmaps suggest a **modern fertilizer complex** can present **hundreds of megawatts** of **baseload-class** demand (commonly cited **~500 MW** in policy-grade scenarios—exact sizing is plant-design specific). That scale matters: it is large enough to **change dispatch** rather than decorate it.

## Cement and other industrial offtake

**Cement decarbonization** is another hydrogen sink: replacing fossil fuel combustion in clinker/pyroprocessing with **hydrogen or hybrid fuels** stacks up large **annual H₂ demand** in national modeling discourse (illustrative national figures on the order of **~228,000 tonnes H₂/year** implying **~2 GW** of electrolysis capacity are used in roadmap arithmetic—treat as **planning magnitude**, not a signed FEED package). The point for [[domestic-led-hydro-strategy]] is structural: these are **domestic value chains** that **pay for reliability** differently than **spot export**.

## Policy trajectory

The **Green Hydrogen Policy (2024)** signals official intent to coordinate **standards, pilots, incentives, and safety**. The typical staged narrative runs **pilots 2025–2027** toward **industrial scale by ~2035**, with **cost projections** in policy documents clustering around **~$2.15/kg** by mid-2030s under favorable renewable input cost paths—again, a **directional** planning anchor.

## Relationship to curtailment and surplus

If new RoR continues to outpace demand and storage, [[stranded-generation]] risk rises: energy with nowhere to go at **non-trivial variable cost**. Electrolysis is one of the few **scalable sinks** that can **ride** renewable variability if **system integration** (oxygen use, water treatment, grid connection, industrial symbiosis) is solved.
