# IPO Hydropower Bailout

How Initial Public Offerings (IPOs) and rights share issuances in Nepal's hydropower sector are used to transfer project debt risk onto retail investors — and why the "Chilime success model" is not replicable.

**This page is under research.** Verifiable data on IPO use-of-funds is blocked by SEBON's systemic use of non-machine-readable scanned PDFs for critical financial disclosures — frustrating automated accountability tracking.

## What is known

Six hydro companies have issued IPOs and subsequent rights shares, documented in SEBON and NEPSE registries:

NEPSE has suspended trading on multiple hydro companies: Upper Syange Hydropower (USHL) for insider trading and delayed disclosure of rights share bailout plans; Khani Khola Hydropower (KKHC) amid controversial Q4 reporting and promoter share dumping.

## The Chilime contrast

Chilime Hydropower's 2010 IPO occurred after **seven years of profitable operations** and distributed a 35% dividend within a year. Modern IPOs float pre-revenue, when the project is hemorrhaging cash from capitalized interest. See [[chilime]] for the pre-IPO profitability context and [[q-design-discharge]] for why Chilime's overperformance is not replicable.

## The triple-authority blindspot

Retail investors purchasing rights shares in distressed companies are not informed that the off-taker (NEA) can curtail the project and then penalize it for non-generation. IPO prospectuses project future revenue using the PPA tariff but do not model the scenario where a portion of that revenue is clawed back through AD penalties. See [[nea-triple-authority]] for the institutional framework and [[ad-penalties]] for the penalty mechanism.
