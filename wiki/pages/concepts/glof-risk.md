# GLOF Risk

**Glacial Lake Outburst Floods (GLOFs)** are rapid releases of water from moraine-dammed or ice-dammed lakes, often triggered by **avalanches**, **seismicity**, or **hydrostatic/overpressure failure**. In Nepal’s context, they are a **first-order constraint** on how confidently one can site **run-of-river** corridors, **headworks**, and **storage** in transboundary basins—because a GLOF is not a “flood” in the sense of a predictable monsoon stage; it can arrive as a **high-energy sediment slurry** with **invented hydrographs** that exceed historical design bases.

## Process: from lake instability to basin-scale impact

Glacier retreat creates and enlarges **supraglacial and proglacial lakes**, frequently impounded by **unconsolidated moraines** with weak spillway geometry. When the impoundment fails, the release can **cascade**: one lake drains into another valley reach, remobilizing sediment, eroding channels, and **amplifying peak discharge** far downstream. **Flow paths can extend hundreds of kilometers**, affecting tributaries and mainstems long after the originating valley.

## A concrete 2024 illustration: Thame / Dudh Koshi

The **2024 Thame Valley** sequence—where a **rock avalanche** impacted **Upper Ngole Cho**, then contributed to failure dynamics affecting **Lower Ngole Cho**, producing on the order of a **~22 m** head drop through a **~51 m** breach width (figures as reported in rapid assessments)—generated a **hyper-concentrated flow** tracked **>80 km** down the **Thame** and **Dudh Koshi** systems, damaging **settlements** and **hydropower works**. The event is a textbook case for why **stationary hydrology** assumptions are inadequate: the initiating process was **mass movement + lake chain**, not a monsoon exceedance.

## Implications for hydropower design and siting

Planned **RoR** and **storage** projects along GLOF-prone corridors face three intertwined issues:

1. **Hydraulic load cases** that historically used **gauge-based floods** may understate **tail scenarios** driven by **sudden moraine breaches** and **sediment bulking**.
2. **Sediment pulses** from GLOFs connect hazard to turbine wear, intake burial, and channel avulsion—overlapping with [[sediment-as-design-constraint]] in basins like [[koshi-basin]].
3. **Insurance, financing, and EPC risk** feed back into [[buildability]]: projects that look attractive on LCOE spreadsheets can fail **non-market** tests once tail risks are priced honestly.

Storage concepts in high-hazard reaches—[[dudhkoshi-storage]] is a prominent example—must therefore integrate **landscape stabilization**, **monitoring**, and **cascading-failure** scenarios, not only energy-yield optimization.

## Link to the glacio-hydrological arc

The same deglaciation dynamics that define the [[peak-water]] window also **produce** unstable lakes. Mitigation (lowering, controlled drainage, early warning) competes for **fiscal and institutional bandwidth** with generation targets. Treating GLOFs as a side environmental issue is a category error: they are **infrastructure solvency** issues.
