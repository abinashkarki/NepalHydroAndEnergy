# Seasonal Arbitrage Trap

The **seasonal arbitrage trap** describes how Nepal can appear to "win" on electricity trade while **losing on economic value**: the country systematically **sells cheap wet-season surplus** and **buys expensive dry-season power**, so headline trade balances mask a **destroyed margin on timing**.

## The numbers that look like success

Reported averages illustrate the wedge: **exports** have averaged roughly **NPR 7.11–8.72 per unit** (depending on year and channel), while **imports** cluster around **NPR 9.17–16 per unit**—a large spread that reflects when each transaction occurs, not inefficiency in a single market. In **FY 2023/24**, Nepal **exported about NPR 17.1 billion** and **imported about NPR 16.9 billion**, leaving a **net of only ~NPR 200 million** despite being described as a "net exporter." In **FY 2024/25**, the headline improved — exports NPR 17.46B vs imports NPR 12.92B, net surplus ~NPR 4.5B. But the monthly pattern exposes the trap's persistence: first-half FY 2025/26 data shows peak monthly revenue of **NPR 5.03B (Ashoj/Sep–Oct)** collapsing to **NPR 1.10B (Mangsir/Nov–Dec)** before exports stop entirely and imports begin — a **4.6x swing** within a single fiscal year (see [[data-trade-time-series]]).

## The monthly table: the trap made visible at full resolution

The NEA System Operation Department's FY 2079/80 (~2022/23) operational report provides month-by-month GWh data — the first full monthly resolution in the entire research corpus. Nepal's four biggest import months (Magh, Falgun, Chaitra, Baisakh) record **zero exports**. Baisakh alone logged **395.7 GWh imported, zero exported**. A single peak winter month absorbs more import cost than any single monsoon month earns in export revenue.

A concrete dry-season snapshot: **Falgun 2080** (mid-Feb to mid-Mar 2024) saw **406.3 GWh imported in one month**, peak import hitting **643 MW on a single evening** around 18:30–18:55 — the daily cooking-and-lighting peak — when run-of-river output was at its annual floor. Even with 3,591 MW installed, Nepal approved imports of up to **654 MW** for Jan–Mar 2026 to cover winter peak demand.

See [[data-trade-time-series]] for the full month-by-month breakdown.

## Why this is structural, not accidental

The pattern is the trade manifestation of [[seasonal-mismatch]]: a **run-of-river–heavy fleet** produces when rivers are high and must import when rivers are low. Without large-scale seasonal shifting — what [[storage-deficit]] summarizes as Nepal's physical gap — **arbitrage runs against the domestic system** rather than for it. India's grid and approval corridors matter because they define **when** cross-border capacity is usable and at what effective price; see [[india-energy-relationship]].

## Two price realities: IEX vs whole-system

The trap operates on two layers that must not be conflated:

**Exchange channel (IEX):** Nepal's weighted-average IEX sell price exceeded its buy price in 2023-24 — ₹5.61/kWh sell vs ₹4.43/kWh buy, a **+₹1.18/kWh positive spread**. In 2022-23 the spread was negative (buy ₹5.95, sell ₹5.14 — a −₹0.81 penalty). The improvement is real: Nepal is competing more effectively in India's real-time market during high-hydro months.

**Whole-system average:** In the same FY 2023/24, Nepal's overall sale price averaged **Rs 8.72/unit** while purchase averaged **Rs 9.17/unit** — a **negative spread of Rs 0.45/unit** across the full portfolio. The gap between the positive IEX spread and the negative system average is explained by structural winter imports at constrained, high-cost rates (peak hours, non-exchange procurement) that wash out the monsoon exchange gains. Even in its best recent trade year, Nepal was a net payer per kWh when all transactions are averaged.

## Policy signals: pricing dry-season scarcity

Power purchase and tariff design are beginning to **encode scarcity by season**. PPA restructuring for storage projects splits **winter (~NPR 14.80/unit)** from **monsoon (~NPR 8.45/unit)** — an admission that dry-season energy is a different commodity. Nepal's industrial time-of-day tariffs confirm the same logic: peak hours (17:00–23:00) at ~NPR 10.00–10.20/kWh vs off-peak at ~NPR 4.65–5.25/kWh. The system prices scarcity; it just cannot yet produce the peak supply from its own fleet.

## Strategic implication

Nepal is currently **resource-rich but value-poor** because timing destroys the margin. The cleanest formulation (DeepResearch Part 7): *"The system is getting better at monetizing surplus, but it still pays a winter scarcity tax, and it has not yet built enough domestic electrification depth to turn hydropower into broad-based wealth."*

A [[domestic-led-hydro-strategy]] reframes the objective: build and price for **domestic dry-season reliability first**, then use exports to monetize **true surplus** — rather than exporting because the alternative is curtailment at bad prices.
