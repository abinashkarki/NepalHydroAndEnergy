# Peak Water

**Peak water** (in the glacio-hydrological sense used across Hindu Kush-Himalaya work) is the period when a glacierized catchment reaches **maximum annual meltwater yield** before **long-term decline** sets in. It is not "peak" in the oil-depletion sense of a single day; it is a **multi-decade phase** where retreat temporarily **increases** exposed ice area and melt efficiency, then yields to **shrinking ice volume** and **lower dry-season baseflows**.

## Mechanism: more melt before less water

As glaciers retreat, many basins first see **higher annual melt** — more energy at the surface, longer melt seasons, reconfiguration of flow paths — before entering a regime where **ice storage is depleted** and **late-century flows fall**, especially in the **low-flow months** that hydropower and irrigation care about most. For most Nepal river basins, literature and regional assessments commonly place **peak water between ~2040 and 2070**, with uncertainty driven by emissions pathway, debris cover, and sub-grid hypsometry.

## Why it matters for hydropower planning

Nepal's dominant fleet is sensitive to **intra-annual** and **inter-annual** hydrographs; see [[seasonal-mismatch]]. Peak water defines a **planning window**: roughly **20-40 years** during which glacial contribution is near its maximum for many catchments — not a stable eternity, but a phase where high-head, glacier-fed rivers can look deceptively "strong" in mean annual flow statistics.

The climate-rational engineering strategy is dual:
1. **Build and finance during peak water** where projects clear benefit, consent, and hazard screens — because energy and water yields for glacier-dependent schemes are comparatively favorable.
2. **Design for post-peak decline** — reservoir operating rules, sediment management, environmental flows, and downstream expectations must assume **a worsening dry-season regime**, not a stationary 20th-century hydrograph.

Basin-specific discharge framing lives in [[koshi-basin]] and [[karnali-basin]] pages; cryosphere evidence synthesis is anchored on [[icimod-hkh-glacier-change]].

## Hazards ride the same trend

Retreat does not only alter means; it reconfigures **risk**. Lake formation, moraine instability, and cascading processes connect peak-water-era construction to [[glof-risk]]: the same decades that maximize certain yields also maximize some tail-risk exposures unless design assumptions are updated. Glacial lake area has been expanding at **~0.83%/year** since 1987 — a slow accumulation of potential energy with episodic catastrophic release.

## Orders of magnitude for urgency

Quantitative markers from recent Himalayan climate reviews and ICIMOD:

| Indicator | Value | Confidence |
|-----------|-------|------------|
| Nepal temperature rise rate | **0.02–0.16 °C/year** (recent decades) | Moderate — regional variation wide |
| HKH glacier mass balance | **−0.3 to −0.8 m water-equivalent/year** | Moderate — peer-reviewed |
| Glacial lake area expansion | **~0.83%/year** since 1987 | Moderate — satellite-derived |
| Nepal glacier area (1980s -> 2010) | **5,168.3 -> 3,902.4 km²** (**−24.5%**) | Moderate — inventory comparison |
| Snow/ice cover decline (Nepal) | **~40%** documented | Medium — boundary definition varies |
| Long-run volume loss (high emissions) | **~65% by 2100** across HKH | Low precision — model range is wide |
| HKH glacier area loss 1990-2020 | **~12%** | Moderate — ICIMOD aggregates |

The policy point is not precision to the third decimal: it is that **Nepal's hydropower golden window is time-bounded** and should be used to build **durable domestic value**, not only nameplate MW.

## The Karnali decomposition: what "glacier-fed" actually means

A peer-reviewed high-resolution hydrological decomposition of the Karnali basin breaks long-term discharge into contributions:
- Rainfall runoff: ~**40%**
- Baseflow (groundwater): ~**35%**
- Snowmelt: ~**24%** (peaks pre-monsoon, buffers late dry season)
- Glacier ice melt: ~**0.8%** of annual volume

This is the corrective to the "glacier-fed = reliable" assumption. Glacier *ice melt* is a tiny share of annual Karnali flow — **snowmelt and baseflow are the real dry-season buffers**. As warming shifts more precipitation from snow to rain at high elevations and depletes snowpack, the dry-season buffer degrades even before glacier volume is substantially reduced. The near-term risk is **loss of snowmelt buffer**, not yet major glacier volume loss — the timing of this risk is earlier than most public narratives acknowledge.

## Climate change and seasonal amplification

Recent climate synthesis points to this directional consensus:
- **Monsoon precipitation:** likely to intensify (heavier events, more flood risk), while total annual rainfall shows mixed regional trends
- **Snowpack:** declining as warming shifts precipitation from snow to rain at progressively higher elevations
- **Net seasonal effect:** wetter wet seasons, drier dry seasons — amplifying [[seasonal-mismatch]] over time
- **Hydropower implication:** more extreme flood risk in monsoon (structural and safety design), more acute lean-season deficit (reliability design) — both costs rise simultaneously

The [[seasonal-mismatch]] that already defines Nepal's energy problem today will structurally worsen over the planning horizon. Storage becomes more valuable, not less, as climate instability increases.
