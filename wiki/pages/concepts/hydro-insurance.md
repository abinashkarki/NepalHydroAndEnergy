# Hydro Insurance

The insurance market for Nepal's hydropower sector — mandatory coverage requirements, premium escalation, claim settlement failures, and the systemic interaction with AD penalties and debt service obligations. The central connection is to [[nea-triple-authority]]: when physical damage prevents generation, NEA may still apply AD penalties while the insurer delays settlement, draining the developer's working capital from both sides.

## Premium Burden — Verified Project-Level Data

Two verified data points establish the insurance premium burden on operational hydro projects:

| project | gross_revenue_npr | annual_premium_npr | premium_pct_revenue | fiscal_year | source |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Sanima Mai HPP | 764,000,000 | 13,770,000 | 1.80% | FY2023 | CARE Ratings Nepal + Sanima Reliance Life disclosures |
| Upper Tamakoshi | 8,678,000,000 | 99,000,000 | 1.14% | FY2080 | UTKHPL Annual Report |

For infrastructure assets with 85%+ PBILDT margins, an insurance line consuming 1.8% of top-line revenue represents 10–12% of the entire operating cost envelope. Hydro project finance models assume minimal variable OPEX once construction is complete — insurance premium escalation directly compresses the DSCR headroom these models depend on.

## Market Structure and the Premium Surge

The premium surge originated from two simultaneous events:

1. **Mandatory minimum premium policy** introduced FY2078 BS by Beema Samiti (now Nepal Insurance Authority). This set statutory minimum premium floors across the hydro sector regardless of individual project risk profiles.

2. **International reinsurer withdrawal** from Nepal hydro risk portfolios post-2021, driven by accumulated flood, landslide, and GLOF loss experience. This removed the competitive pricing pressure that had previously kept domestic premiums manageable.

**Consequence:** Nepal Re and Himalayan Re were ordered to raise paid-up capital from NPR 13 billion to NPR 20 billion to absorb the reinsurance gap. Source: Insurance Khabar (English edition).

**On the "800% surge" claim:** This figure has no locatable primary source. IPPAN's documented public statements (Ekantipur/Kantipur, April 2025) cite "up to 500%" as the maximum increase. This figure should be used in preference to the 800% figure. [PARTIALLY VERIFIED: sourced to IPPAN lobbying statement, not independent actuarial calculation.]

## Market Concentration

Shikhar Insurance Company Limited is the dominant non-life insurer in Nepal, collecting NPR 4.80 billion in premium in an 8-month period (FY2080/81 annual: NPR 5.25 billion). Source: Investopaper / Shikhar Insurance Annual Reports.

The 14 non-life insurance companies in Nepal collectively collected over NPR 31.70 billion in premiums by the close of Falgun FY2082/83, reflecting a 13.11% year-over-year expansion. Source: Nepal Insurance Authority (NIA) via Insurance Khabar.

The banking sector's requirement for comprehensive project insurance as a loan disbursement condition creates a captive market for domestic insurers. IPPs cannot self-insure or go uninsured without triggering lender covenant breaches — giving domestic insurers effective pricing power without competitive constraint.

## Claim Settlement Delays

The "90% claim settlement delays" claim circulating in industry discussions has not been numerically verified. No named project claim case — with documented damage, filing date, settlement date, and financial impact during the gap — has been found in research passes to date. The Nepal Insurance Authority (NIA) has recognized the systemic threat and proposed developmental roadmaps, but no market-making intervention has been implemented. ## Interaction with AD Penalties and Force Majeure

Whether the PPA contains force majeure or physical damage exemption from AD penalties during the insurance claim settlement period is unresolved.
