# Hydropower Potential Categories

“Nepal has 83,000 MW” is a political slogan and a methodological landmine. **Hydropower potential categories** exist precisely because **theoretical river power** is not the same as **financeable capacity**, **buildable projects**, or **firm winter electricity** (see [[firm-power]]).

## The reduction pyramid (conceptual chain)

Think of a pyramid—quantified in [[data-potential-pyramid]]—where each step applies filters the previous step ignores:

1. **Theoretical / gross potential** — legacy national figures near **~83,000 MW** appear in older discourse; WECS (2019) reframed theoretical potential around **~72,500 MW** depending on methodological choices.
2. **Technical potential** — what is physically plausible with conventional technology; commonly discussed in the **~42,000–46,000 MW** range in national studies (exact bounds depend on site database and assumptions).
3. **Techno-economic potential** — WECS (2019) reports **32,680 MW** at this layer: sites that meet engineering and economic screening rules *as modeled*.
4. **Economically viable (market window)** — often discussed as **~25,000–35,000 MW** under price and financing assumptions that move with interest rates, carbon policy, and India import terms.
5. **Realistically developable by ~2040** — planning horizons frequently land around **~10,000–15,000 MW** once execution risk, transmission, and social license are acknowledged.
6. **Installed** — roughly **~3,591 MW** hydro installed (order-of-magnitude anchor for “today”).

These categories are **not interchangeable**. Moving down the chain is not “being conservative”; it is **changing the question**.

## Basin concentration

A large share of gross potential concentrates in three major basins—**[[koshi-basin]]**, **[[karnali-basin]]**, and **[[gandaki-basin]]**—often cited on the order of **~94%** of national potential. That concentration matters for cascade externalities, sediment, and geopolitical export corridors: “national potential” is geographically lumpy.

## Why this matters for analysis

Potential-category discipline prevents two common failures: inflating creditworthiness with theoretical MW, and confusing **annual energy** with **dry-season dependability**. Primary methodology and citations live under [[wecs-hydropower-potential-2019]].
