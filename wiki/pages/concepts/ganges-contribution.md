# Nepal's Contribution to the Ganges

Nepal occupies only about **13%** of the **Ganges basin area**, yet contributes roughly **40–45%** of **mean annual flow** and on the order of **70–80%** of **dry-season flow** measured at **Farakka**-style accounting points in strategic basin literature. This **hydrological asymmetry**—small footprint, large leverage on timing—is the physical basis for Nepal’s **upstream role** in South Asian water security.

## Underused abundance

Nepal's problem is not that the basin is empty. In National Water Plan framing, the country has roughly **225 BCM** of annual water availability but uses only about **15 BCM** for economic and social purposes. The strategic issue is therefore conversion: storage, delivery, irrigation timing, and coordinated releases — not raw annual scarcity.

## What the asymmetry implies downstream

**Bihar**, **Uttar Pradesh**, and—through the concatenated regime—**Bangladesh** are sensitive to **Nepal-origin** contributions for:

- **Kharif and rabi irrigation** scheduling (canal drafts depend on low-flow stability).
- **Flood peaks** shaped by transboundary storms and sediment pulses (see [[koshi-basin]] for the extreme case).
- **Drought resilience** when monsoon spatial correlation fails.

Aggregated planning numbers in basin assessments commonly cite **~5–7 million hectares** of **irrigated agriculture** and **~200–250 million people** as **significantly affected** by Nepal-influenced flow regimes (definitions vary; treat as **order-of-magnitude** strategic scale, not a census).

## Climate change raises the stakes

If **variability intensifies** and **low flows become less reliable** while **extreme events** increase, the **marginal value** of upstream **storage, forecasting, and coordinated releases** rises. Nepal’s geography could translate into **bargaining power**—but only if institutional capacity converts hydrology into **credible, enforceable cooperation**. Today, the more common pattern is **leverage without durable agreement**: episodic crisis diplomacy rather than **rules-based benefit sharing**.

## Geopolitics and national planning

The strategic read is developed in parallel on [[hydro-geopolitics]] and [[india-energy-relationship]]: electrons and cubic meters are **joint products** in peoples’ minds even when markets separate them. Nepal’s **National Water Plan** discourse (see [[national-water-plan-nepal]]) is the domestic mirror—attempting to align **IWRM**, **energy**, and **disaster risk** under one national narrative.

## Western basins as part of the same puzzle

[[karnali-basin]] feeds the **Ghaghara** complex and therefore **UP’s** vast irrigated plains. Storage and diversion debates there are not “local” Nepali stories; they are **subcontinental** stories with **Nepali headwaters** at the center.

## Analytical bottom line

Nepal’s contribution to the Ganges is **not proportional to its land share**. That fact creates **real upstream leverage**—and also **moral hazard** if Nepal builds **unilateral infrastructure** that ignores downstream sensitivities. The research task is to map **where cooperation creates surplus** for both sides versus where **zero-sum framing** is unavoidable.
