# Consumption Trajectory Outlook

What Nepal's electricity consumption growth trajectory implies for the country's energy strategy — the import-displacement opportunity, the growth risks, and the metric that determines whether the hydro build-out succeeds or fails on its own terms.

This page synthesizes [[data-domestic-demand]], [[data-industrial-electricity-consumers]], [[data-agricultural-energy-consumption]], and [[data-consumption-geographic-distribution]] into a forward view. The companion supply-side analysis is [[twenty-year-strategy]].

## The Consumption Story in One Sentence

Nepal's electricity consumption has grown ~55% in four years (from ~7,277 GWh to 11,288 GWh), but electricity is still only **7.23% of total final energy** and **0.5% of households cook with it** — meaning the growth runway is measured in decades, not years.

## Current State — Electricity Consumption (FY 2024/25)

| Segment | GWh | Growth (4yr CAGR) | Electricity Penetration in Segment |
|---------|------:|------:|------:|
| Residential | ~4,742 | ~10.8% | 5.0% of residential energy |
| Industrial | ~4,136 | ~10.1% | 11.6% of industrial energy (FY 2079/80) |
| Commercial | ~879 | ~14.5% | 9.9% of commercial energy (FY 2079/80) |
| Construction/Mining | ~591* | — | **55.5%** of sector energy — already majority-electric |
| Agriculture | ~315 | — | 22.5% of agricultural energy |
| Transport | ~25 | — | 0.05% — essentially zero |
| Other (streetlight, irrigation, temp) | ~620 | ~17.8% | — |
| **Total electricity** | **~11,288** | **~11.6%** | **7.23% of total final energy** |

\* Estimated from WECS sector share applied to FY 2024/25 total

## The Consumption Growth Model

Per-capita electricity consumption is 465 kWh/yr and growing ~10% annually. At this rate:

| Year | Per-capita (kWh) | Implied total (GWh) | Context |
|------|------:|------:|---------|
| FY 2024/25 | 465 | 11,288 | Current |
| ~2029 | ~750 | ~18,000 | Nepal reaches current India per-capita |
| ~2034 | ~1,200 | ~28,800 | Reaches lower-middle-income norm |
| ~2040 | ~2,000 | ~48,000 | Reaches middle-income norm |

This trajectory is not a forecast — it's a sense-check. At 10% annual demand growth, domestic consumption alone would absorb roughly the output of the planned hydro pipeline (see [[data-potential-pyramid]]) without needing massive export growth. But sustaining 10% demand growth requires the substitution pathway in [[energy-substitution-pathway]] to actually happen — cooking electrification, industrial fuel-switching, and EV uptake.

## The Import Displacement Ledger

The single most powerful number in the consumption profile is the gap between what Nepal *currently* imports (fossil fuels) and what it *could* displace with domestic electricity:

| Fuel | Total Consumption (TJ) | Import Cost (NPR Bill./yr, est.) | Displaceable by Electricity? | Electricity Equivalent (GWh) |
|------|------:|------:|------:|------:|
| Diesel (all sectors) | 49,728 | ~120 | Yes — transport, ag pumps, gensets | ~13,800 |
| LPG (all sectors) | 23,677 | ~55 | Yes — induction cooking | ~6,600 |
| Petrol (all sectors) | 21,932 | ~75 | Partial — EV passenger vehicles | ~6,100 |
| Coal (industrial) | 34,016 | ~40 | Partial — electric kilns, hydrogen | ~9,400 |
| ATF / jet fuel | 6,191 | ~20 | No — aviation | — |
| **Total displaceable** | **~129,353** | **~290** | | **~35,900 GWh** |

> [!finding] Total displacement opportunity is ~3× current electricity consumption
> If Nepal electrified all technically-displaceable fossil fuel consumption, it would need ~35,900 GWh of additional electricity — roughly triple the current 11,288 GWh. This is the domestic demand ceiling that the [[domestic-led-hydro-strategy]] is designed to capture.

### Displacement already underway

- **EV passenger vehicles:** >70% of new passenger vehicle imports in FY 2024/25 were EVs, driven by tax differentials
- **Electric cooking:** NEA FY 2024/25 report explicitly flags government policy to encourage electric cooking, transportation, and industry
- **Industrial switching:** diesel genset → grid connection is happening as reliability improves

### Displacement not yet started

- **Brick kilns:** ~1,500+ coal-burning kilns, essentially zero electric kilns deployed
- **Freight transport:** diesel trucks and buses, no material electrification
- **Cement clinker:** coal-fired, hydrogen co-firing still in concept stage (see [[green-hydrogen-nepal]])
- **LPG-to-induction cooking at scale:** 0.5% household penetration, needs appliance cost reduction and reliability improvements

## Risk Factors — What Could Slow or Accelerate Growth

### Upside risks (faster consumption growth)

| Factor | Mechanism | Magnitude |
|--------|-----------|-----------|
| LPG subsidy removal / rebalancing | Shift households from LPG to induction cooking | +50–75 PJ (~14,000–21,000 GWh) potential |
| Industrial policy for ferroalloys | Attract electro-intensive industry with cheap reliable power | +500–2,000 GWh per major plant |
| EV adoption beyond passenger vehicles | Electric buses, two-wheelers, three-wheelers | +500–1,500 GWh by 2030 |
| Data centres / ICT expansion | Nepal's competitive advantage — cheap green power + English-speaking workforce | +200–500 GWh |
| Cold chain build-out | Refrigerated transport and storage for agriculture | +500–1,000 GWh |

### Downside risks (slower consumption growth)

| Factor | Mechanism | Magnitude |
|--------|-----------|-----------|
| Manufacturing decline continues | Manufacturing already fell from 7.3% to 5.7% of GDP | −200–500 GWh vs trend |
| Grid reliability stalls | If outages persist, firms stay on diesel, households don't buy induction cookers | −300–800 GWh vs potential |
| Fuelwood inertia | Cultural preference + zero cash cost keeps biomass dominant in cooking | −100–200 PJ cooking load |
| Tariff distortion | If industrial tariffs don't reflect the true cost advantage over diesel, fuel-switching economics erode | −100–300 GWh |
| Migration / depopulation | Rural areas lose electricity consumers faster than urban gains | −100–200 GWh |

## The Metric That Determines Success

From [[energy-substitution-pathway]]:

> **Biomass + fossil share of Nepal's final energy consumption.** Today ~92.8%. A serious transition by 2040 should bring it below 70%, implying ~25–30 PJ of net displacement per year.

Applied to 2025-2030 specifically, the intermediate targets are:

| Metric | Current (FY 2024/25) | 2030 Target | Status |
|--------|------:|------:|--------|
| Electricity share of final energy | 7.23% (FY 2079/80) | 12–15% | On track if 10% demand CAGR holds |
| Electric cooking penetration | 0.5% | 5–10% | Requires active policy push |
| EV share of new vehicle sales | >70% (passenger only) | >90% (all light vehicles) | Ahead on passenger, behind on freight |
| Industrial electricity share | 11.6% (FY 2079/80) | 18–22% | Requires grid reliability + industrial policy |
| Per-capita electricity | 465 kWh | 800–1,000 kWh | On track |
| Diesel displaced in agriculture | ~0 (currently rising) | 20–30% of ag diesel | Requires tubewell electrification programme |

## The Export Surplus Question

If domestic consumption grows at 10% CAGR and generation grows at roughly the same pace (the IPSDP pipeline implies ~12,000-15,000 MW installed by 2035), the net export profile depends on which grows faster:

| Scenario | Demand CAGR | Supply CAGR | 2030 Net Export (GWh) | Character |
|----------|------:|------:|------:|-----------|
| Demand-led | 12% | 10% | ~500–1,500 | Imports still needed in winter |
| Balanced | 10% | 10% | ~2,000–3,500 | Monsoon exports, winter imports |
| Supply-led | 8% | 12% | ~5,000–7,000 | Large monsoon surplus, seasonal trade deepens |

The current trajectory (~11.6% demand growth, ~10% supply growth) most closely resembles the "Demand-led" scenario — **if it sustains**. The key variable is whether the demand-side policies (cooking electrification, EV uptake, industrial switching) materialize at the same pace as the supply-side pipeline (22 projects commissioned in FY 2024/25 alone).

The nightmare scenario is the "Supply-led" one: large monsoon surplus with weak domestic demand → exports at low spot prices while winter imports at high peak rates persist → the [[seasonal-arbitrage-trap]] institutionalizes.

## What the Data Cannot Yet Answer

1. **Hourly consumption by consumer category** — we know the evening peak shape but not which sectors drive it (residential cooking + lighting probably dominates). Without this, the storage + demand-side management sizing is approximate.
2. **Actual industrial sub-sector GWh** — the estimates in [[data-industrial-electricity-consumers]] are indicative. NEA has this data but does not publish it.
3. **Captive generation inventory** — how much diesel generation capacity exists behind industrial meters, and how much is actually dispatched, is unmeasured. This is the hidden consumption layer.
4. **Province-level sectoral splits** — we have provincial totals but not the domestic/industrial/commercial breakdown within each province.
5. **Price elasticity of demand** — how sensitive is each consumer category to tariff changes? Essential for demand-side policy design but unresearched for Nepal.
