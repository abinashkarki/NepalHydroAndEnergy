# Agrivoltaics and Land

Module costs have collapsed, grid costs are middling, LCOE is already below small hydro ([[solar-lcoe-crossover]]). The binding constraint on the next 5–10 GW of Nepal solar is neither technology nor money. **It is land.** Specifically, it is the politics of converting Terai agricultural land into ground-mounted solar at the scale the grid can absorb.

This page argues that **agrivoltaics is not a "nice to have" framing for Nepal; it is the only politically survivable at-scale configuration for Terai utility solar**, and explains why.

## The land arithmetic

Utility-scale ground-mount solar needs ~2.5 ha per MWp at typical Nepal spacing (row-to-row, tilt, O&M lanes). Building 5 GW on fee-simple purchased land requires **~12,500 ha** — roughly **0.6% of Nepal's total cultivated area**, but concentrated in a narrow belt:

- Terai agricultural land: ~1.4 million ha
- Within 10 km of a 132+ kV substation in Terai: **~220,000 ha**
- Non-forest, non-protected, slope < 3%, within that belt: **~160,000 ha**
- Of which, publicly-owned (*sarkari*) or non-permanently-cultivated: **~5,000–15,000 ha** (estimates vary; no single cadastral register)

So **the fee-simple, non-agricultural, grid-adjacent land available for 5 GW of Terai solar is roughly the land needed for 2–6 GW.** Nothing past that is buildable without either (a) buying agricultural land from smallholders, or (b) finding a way to share the land with agriculture.

Option (a) is a political non-starter at that scale. Nepal's hydropower-era displacement politics (Upper Tamakoshi, Budhigandaki, Pancheshwar) has produced a sensitised Terai farming public, distrustful of large land conversions, with active civil society and elected representation. Fee-simple conversion of rice paddy to PV modules — at the ten-district Terai scale — produces a food-security / landlessness fight that a government elected with an agricultural base cannot win and should not try.

Option (b) is agrivoltaics.

## What agrivoltaics is, in Nepal-specific terms

The generic agrivoltaic definition — **dual-use of land for crop production and PV generation, through elevated or spaced modules** — covers a range of configurations:

| Configuration | Module height | Crop compatibility | Yield interaction | Capex multiplier |
|---|---|---|---|---:|
| **Low-mount vegetable rotation** | 0.8–1.2 m | Leafy greens, onion, garlic, turmeric, shade-tolerant veg | +5 to −10% crop yield under partial shade | 1.0–1.1× |
| **High-canopy rice / wheat** | 3.0–4.5 m | Rice, wheat, maize (partial-shade-tolerant varieties) | −10 to −25% crop yield; tractor access preserved | 1.35–1.55× |
| **Animal-grazing (agri-PV)** | 1.2–1.8 m with fencing | Goat / sheep grazing under; moderates heat | Forage gains; livestock welfare up | 1.05–1.15× |
| **Tree / plantation (orchard-PV)** | 2.5–4.0 m | Mango, litchi, guava, banana | Microclimate benefit; water saving | 1.2–1.4× |
| **Pond-edge / irrigation canal** | 1.5–3.0 m over canal | Fish / aquaculture below | Reduces evaporation from canals | 1.1–1.3× |

Two of these configurations fit Nepal's Terai farming calendar especially well:

- **Leafy greens + low-mount PV on the 6-month paddy-to-fallow rotation.** Paddy in monsoon (when PV yield is lowest anyway), leafy greens Oct–May under the modules (when PV yield is highest and the crop *benefits* from shade in the pre-monsoon heat). Ideal match.
- **High-canopy PV over rice + wheat double-crop.** Higher capex but preserves the dominant Terai land-use pattern; yield penalty of 10–20% on rice is offset by a PV revenue-share of NPR 40,000–80,000 per ropani per year, a 4–10× uplift on smallholder cash income. The economics of this are not marginal.

## Why it is the politically survivable path

A solar project structured as:

- **25-year lease** (not purchase) of 1–5 ha from each participating smallholder, with a right of return at lease-end.
- **Revenue share** of a defined percentage of PPA receipts, indexed to inflation.
- **Preserved agricultural use** under / between modules, with the farmer as the agricultural tenant.
- **Shared community-scale services** — rural electrification credit, pump irrigation at cost, cold-storage co-development.

…has a fundamentally different political valence than "buy the land for a solar farm." It resembles a successful cooperative more than a land grab. It mirrors the structure that made India's Gujarat and Maharashtra agrivoltaic deployments land-lightly, while the pure-purchase models in Rajasthan and Karnataka have had recurring land-conflict problems.

It is also the structure most compatible with Nepal's cooperative-heavy rural economy ([[aepc-renewable-framework]]) and with the farmers' cooperative (*krishak sahakari*) networks that already operate at the 10–500-household scale across Terai.

## What this concept is *not* saying

- Not that every Terai solar site must be agrivoltaic. Government-owned *sarkari* parcels, degraded / alkali land, oxbow scrub, industrial-zone-adjacent land, and hydro-co-location sites ([[hybrid-siting-logic]]) remain the first choice where available — perhaps the first 1.5–2 GW.
- Not that agrivoltaics automatically solves all politics. It changes the coalition, but requires specific contract drafting (lease-not-sale, revenue-share, return rights) that Nepal does not yet have template law for.
- Not that it is capex-free. The ~1.3–1.5× capex multiplier on high-canopy configurations is real and must be priced into tariffs. At current module prices, the agrivoltaic LCOE is still well below hydro PPA ([[solar-lcoe-crossover]]).

## The policy moves this concept implies

If the argument holds, Nepal needs four things it does not yet have:

1. **A lease-template law for 20–25 year solar leases on agricultural land**, separating usufruct transfer from fee-simple conversion. Without this, every agrivoltaic site is negotiated one-off, which kills scaling.
2. **An agrivoltaic tariff adder** — NPR 0.30–0.50/kWh uplift for dual-use configurations, conditional on post-build crop verification. Creates the capex-offset incentive.
3. **A recognised agricultural-technical standard** for minimum module height, row spacing, and crop-light requirements — Nepal currently has none.
4. **A VGF / green-finance channel for the incremental capex** — the [[eib-rural-solar-phase2]] and [[wb-grid-solar-ee-project]] are the most natural hosts, but neither currently prices an agrivoltaic multiplier.

Without these four, Terai utility solar hits the land wall at roughly 2 GW and stalls. With them, the same physical resource becomes a 10–15 GW build-out path that takes nothing out of food production, and actively raises smallholder cash income.
