# Firm Power

**Firm power** is dependable, **year-round**, **dispatchable** electrical capability under credible drought and system stress assumptions—not a branding label for “big projects,” and not interchangeable with **installed megawatts**, **annual energy (GWh)**, or even **average seasonal output**.

Nepal’s public discourse routinely collapses these quantities. That conflation is not harmless: it steers policy toward **headline capacity** and away from **scarcity months**, which is exactly where [[seasonal-mismatch]] bites a RoR-heavy fleet (see [[run-of-river-hydropower]]).

## Installed MW ≠ what you can count on in February

A plant can be “online” while being strategically irrelevant for reliability if its energy is concentrated in monsoon weeks when the grid is already long—or if its output is volatile within-day in ways that the transmission system cannot absorb. Conversely, a smaller reservoir or import contract may contribute more **firm** value than a large RoR string, depending on commit rights, hydrology, and dispatch rules.

The right planning question is therefore not “how many MW?” but **how much electricity is deliverable when the alternative is rationing, diesel, or blackout**—and at what cost.

## Potential pyramids are not firm-power statements

National “potential” figures sit at the top of a reduction chain from theoretical resource to what is financeable and buildable; see [[hydropower-potential-categories]] and the tabulated reductions in [[data-potential-pyramid]]. Those layers measure **energy opportunity**, not **contract-grade dependability**. Firm power is the bottom of the stack in strategic terms: it is what you underwrite, what you trade, and what industrialization plans should assume in dry weeks.

## Analytical payoff

Keeping **firm** distinct from **big** clarifies why winter deficits can persist even as installed capacity rises, why export discussions hinge on **commitment windows** not annual averages, and why system adequacy is a **seasonal adequacy** problem first.
