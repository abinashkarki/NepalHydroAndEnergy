# How To Read The Geopolitics Map

The geopolitics preset is not a Nepal-only map. It follows Nepal-linked rivers into the northern Indian plains, Bangladesh, and the Bay of Bengal because the political question is about downstream exposure, not just upstream origin.

## Visual Grammar

Read the layers in this order:

| Map mark | Meaning |
|----------|---------|
| Nepal-linked basin polygons | Where Nepal sits inside the upstream basin geography. |
| Nepal rivers and downstream rivers | The main water routes from Nepal into the plains. |
| Ocean route | A strategic continuation through the Ganges/Padma/Meghna system to the Bay of Bengal. |
| Route notes | Merge, handoff, naming, and delta markers that explain the route. |
| Dependency zones | Broad downstream exposure areas for flood, dry-season, agricultural, population, sediment, and delta sensitivity. |
| Population anchors | City and delta points that help orient the human geography around the routes. |
| Flow regulation | Storage projects that could change timing, dry-season support, flood attenuation, sediment behavior, and cooperation risk. |

## The Core Rule

Do not read the downstream layers as water-control claims. They are exposure claims.

The map does not say Nepal controls Bihar, Uttar Pradesh, or Bangladesh water security. It says Nepal-linked rivers pass through sensitive downstream geographies where timing, flood pulses, sediment, dry-season flow, and future storage decisions can matter politically.

## Why India And Bangladesh Marks Are Here

The downstream marks exist because Nepal's hydropower and storage choices are also timing choices. A run-of-river project mostly follows the hydrograph. A storage project can shift water across time, trap sediment, attenuate floods, or change the reliability of dry-season releases.

That is why the map keeps [[data-layer-future-regulation-scenario]] visible with the downstream layers. The political value of storage is not just megawatts. It is the ability to regulate time.
