# Rooftop, Mini-Grid, and Off-Grid Solar

The utility-scale solar story ([[hybrid-siting-logic]], [[agrivoltaics-and-land]]) is one of three tracks. The other two — **behind-the-meter rooftop** and **off-grid / mini-grid distributed PV** — operate under different institutions, different financing, and a different political coalition. They together carry roughly **700,000 MWh/year** of current generation, comparable to a 200 MW mid-sized hydro plant running flat, and they reach populations NEA's grid does not.

Treating them as a single track is the most common analytical error in Nepal-solar discourse. They are not.

## The three-track structure

| Track | Institution | Typical scale | Typical customer | Connection |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Utility-scale** | NEA + IPPs | 5–50 MWp | Grid at 33/132 kV | Wholesale PPA |
| **Rooftop / distributed** | AEPC + private installers + NEA net-metering | 3 kWp–500 kWp | Household, commercial, institutional | Behind-the-meter, net-metered |
| **Off-grid / mini-grid** | AEPC + community cooperatives + NGOs | 50 Wp–100 kWp | Rural household, village cluster | Standalone DC/AC, mini-grid LV |

AEPC — the Alternative Energy Promotion Centre — is the government institution that runs the second and third tracks. It has distributed **over 1 million solar home systems (SHS)** since 1996 and anchors ~30 community mini-grids in Karnali and Sudurpaschim; this is a substantial, existing solar deployment that rarely appears in installed-capacity tables because its MWp adds up household-by-household rather than plant-by-plant.

## The rooftop track

**~70–90 MWp installed in aggregate** across Kathmandu Valley, Pokhara, Birgunj, Biratnagar, Butwal, and secondary cities (rough estimate; no comprehensive registry). Growth has accelerated since NEA's 2018 net-metering framework cleared administrative friction for small commercial installs.

**Where it works:**

- Commercial rooftops (factories, warehouses, supermarket chains, telecom towers). Self-consumption + net-metered export during daytime fits daytime commercial load perfectly.
- Institutional rooftops (schools, hospitals, government buildings). Particularly valuable in load-shedding-legacy regions where institutional backup was diesel.
- Urban residential at the upper-middle and high-income segment (3–10 kWp systems with optional 5 kWh battery backup).

**Where it struggles:**

- Low-income residential. Capex of NPR 80–120k for a 3 kWp system is beyond the marginal middle-class household; subsidy / financing gap.
- Informal rooftops (tin-sheet, un-engineered, seismic retrofit). Mount feasibility is case-by-case.
- Kathmandu Valley pollution haze and the monsoon cloud penalty ([[solar-resource-geography-nepal]]) drop yield 15–25% below Terai benchmarks.

**Why it matters to the national strategy:** rooftop solar is the only utility-interactive solar resource whose **siting happens without a tender, without NEA involvement, and without land politics**. The bottleneck is not resource or economics but **net-metering procedural speed** and **consumer financing access**. A 2 GW rooftop build-out is technically feasible on urban commercial+institutional floorspace alone; actual deployment is ~5% of that.

## The off-grid / mini-grid track

Nepal has ~200,000 households served by AEPC-supported solar home systems and ~30,000–50,000 served by ~30 village-scale mini-grids (Humla, Jumla, Mugu, Dolpa, Bajura, Kalikot, remote Sankhuwasabha and Taplejung). These are small, often 5–50 kWp aggregate per village, coupled to battery banks (increasingly lithium replacing lead-acid), and typically run by a community committee with AEPC technical backstop.

**Why off-grid matters more than its MW suggests:**

- It reaches the ~**8.6% of households** not yet on the NEA grid — concentrated in [[karnali-basin]] and [[mahakali-basin]] districts where grid extension is either impractical (>3 km terrain per household, uneconomic) or a 10+ year proposition.
- It is the only rural-electrification pathway that works on the NEA grid's timeline, not 10 years later.
- Diesel-displacement: off-grid solar + BESS directly replaces the NPR 60–90/kWh effective cost of diesel generators that remote clinics, schools, and tourism lodges have been running for decades. LCOE is not "competitive with grid" — it is competitive with NPR 60/kWh incumbent.

**The emerging hybrid model:** the NEA annual report FY 2024/25 notes "**off-grid solar plants with Battery Energy Storage Systems (BESS) in Karnali**" as a programme line. This is the right model: AEPC handles community engagement and technical backstop, NEA handles the grid-edge integration logic so these systems can sell into the main grid when the radial feeder eventually reaches them. Without that integration design, each off-grid system becomes a stranded asset once the grid arrives — which is the historical pattern.

## The three tracks, economically contrasted

| Variable | Utility | Rooftop | Off-grid |
|---|---|---|---|
| Capex per MWp | NPR 55–75m | NPR 75–95m | NPR 120–180m (+BESS) |
| Effective tariff earned | NPR ~5.43/kWh (960 MW LoI weighted avg.) | NPR 7–10/kWh (retail displaced) | NPR 60–90/kWh (diesel displaced) |
| Who pays | NEA tariff base | Rooftop owner | Grant / concessional + user |
| Bottleneck | Land (Terai) | Net-metering speed | Capex finance + community O&M |
| Political constituency | NEA + big IPPs | Urban middle class + commerce | Rural poor + remote districts |

The off-grid tariff displacement number is the one most commonly overlooked. Off-grid solar, *evaluated against the resource it actually displaces* (diesel and kerosene), is the highest-value solar kWh in Nepal — 10–15× the utility PPA price. This is why donor finance flows here disproportionately and why the simplistic "MW efficiency" framing under-reads the track's importance.

## The political coalition argument

Utility solar needs NEA tender capacity, large IPP balance sheets, Terai land lease law, and agrivoltaic contract templates. None of these exist in finished form.

Rooftop + off-grid solar needs AEPC capacity (exists), a consumer-finance channel (partial), and net-metering procedural acceleration (in progress). The political constituency for it runs from Kathmandu's commercial chamber through every rural MP whose district still has off-grid villages. **That coalition is broader and easier to assemble than the utility-scale coalition**, which is why in any 10-year window the rooftop + off-grid build-out is likely to move faster per-rupee than the utility build-out — even though utility carries larger MW.

The strategic consequence is that the **"solar in Nepal" number** that matters to the energy balance is not the utility line item in [[data-fleet-composition]]. It is **utility + rooftop + off-grid**, and the latter two are currently under-counted by a factor of 2–3× in most published tables.

This is also the link to [[claim-solar-political-coalition-is-rural]]: if solar policy is designed only for utility, the coalition that makes it survive is narrower than the coalition that actually uses it.
