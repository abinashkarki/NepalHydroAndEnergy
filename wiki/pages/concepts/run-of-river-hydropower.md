# Run-of-River Hydropower

Nepal’s power system is dominated by **run-of-river (RoR)** hydropower: plants that divert part of a river through headworks and a tunnel, then return it downstream without holding a seasonal inventory of water. RoR is not “small hydro” and not automatically low-impact—it is an **architecture** defined by what you do *not* store.

## RoR vs PRoR vs storage

| Architecture | Water use | Seasonal carryover | Typical role |
|----------------|-----------|--------------------|----------------|
| **RoR** | Uses contemporaneous river flow | None | Energy follows the hydrograph |
| **Peaking RoR (PRoR)** | Short **pondage** (hours) for shifting output within a day | **Not** seasonal storage; usually 2–6 hours of useful peaking | Better intraday shape, still hostage to the monsoon |
| **Reservoir / storage** | Large impoundment | Yes—wet-season inflow can be banked for dry-season release | Seasonal firming and strategic reserves |

Fleet composition matters analytically: Nepal’s installed hydro is roughly **~85.7% RoR**, **~9.9% PRoR**, and **~3.7% storage** (see [[data-fleet-composition]]). That single sentence is already a forecast: most megawatts are structurally aligned with **wet-season inflow**, not with **steady demand**.

## Why Nepal favored RoR

The political economy pushed toward RoR for coherent reasons: **lower unit cost** than large storage, **faster financial close** for private IPPs, **less resettlement** than big reservoirs, and a regulatory/licensing path that matched export-oriented and domestic “capacity addition” narratives. RoR also fragments risk across many sponsors instead of concentrating it in one megaproject state balance sheet.

The trade-off is strategic: RoR solved **how to add MW quickly** without solving **how to add seasonal value**. More RoR in a monsoon climate does not average out into “more reliable power”—it tends to deepen the same dual outcome: **surplus when the river is high** and **deficit when it is low** (see [[seasonal-mismatch]]).

## Fleet type as destiny

In the medium term, **fleet type is destiny**: you cannot wish PRoR pondage into seasonal storage, and you cannot dispatch what physics never impounded. The rare counterexamples—[[upper-tamakoshi]] as a comparatively “wet-season heavy” large RoR story, and [[kulekhani-cascade]] as almost the entire operational storage base—illustrate how narrow the exceptions are.

For planning, the operative question is not headline megawatts but **what kind of electricity** those megawatts become across months: that is the bridge from RoR economics to [[firm-power]] and the physical gap summarized as [[storage-deficit]].
