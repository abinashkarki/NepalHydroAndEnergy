# Buildability

**Buildability** is the first-order filter on Nepal’s hydropower future: a project is not “good” because a desk study assigns a large MW number—it is good if the **entire delivery package** remains credible after geology, hydraulics, hazards, and grid realities are stressed.

WECS and licensing discussions note a persistent pattern: **many licensed schemes remain stalled for years**, not because Nepal lacks river energy on paper, but because **paper feasibility ≠ executable project** under Nepal’s combination of **mountain geology**, **extreme sediment**, **slope access constraints**, and **thin EPC markets**.

## What buildability actually tests

A serious buildability review chains interdependent risks:

- **Geology and underground works** — tunneling and cavern stability under high cover, fault zones, and variable rock mass ratings.
- **Sediment and abrasion** — turbine technology, wear life, and maintenance cycles are not O&M trivia when concentrations swing orders of magnitude seasonally (see [[sediment-as-design-constraint]]).
- **Hydraulic transients and flood/GLOF exposure** — hazard tolerance and intake resilience.
- **Evacuation** — generation without [[stranded-generation]]-grade transmission is incomplete infrastructure. See the [[likhu-2|Likhu cascade]]: four private IPPs totaling 213 MW commissioned into a New Khimti substation sized for half their output, stranding ~40% of contracted generation in FY 2023/24 until a 200 MVA transformer upgrade restored the corridor.
- **Execution realism** — contractor capacity, local logistics winter, and financing covenants that survive delay. See [[chameliya-hydropower|Chameliya]] (11-year build, $7.5M/MW, headrace tunnel collapse) and [[upper-trishuli-3a|Upper Trishuli 3A]] (2024 flood destroyed gates and control room, 88-day outage at [[upper-tamakoshi|Upper Tamakoshi]]).

## Strategic implication: rank system value, not headline MW

Nepal should rank options by **buildable system value**: incremental dependable energy, loss factors, curtailment risk, and delivered cost—not by the largest number in a license. That reframing connects naturally to [[storage-deficit]] (seasonal re-timing is worthless if the dam never reaches COD) and to basin-scale complexity in [[karnali-basin]] megaproject corridors.

Megaprojects such as [[budhigandaki]] are instructive case files: the controversy is not only "how big," but whether the **integrated package** of reservoir, tunnels, communities, and finance can survive the decades-long path to operation. At the opposite scale, [[marsyangdi|Marsyangdi]] (69 MW, 36 years old) demonstrates that aging units approaching overhaul age carry a different buildability risk. [[khimti-i|Khimti-I]] (60 MW, 1996) proves what an early-generation negotiated PPA can deliver, while [[likhu-4|Likhu-4]] (120→52 MW redesign after India wheeling dispute) shows buildability includes the regulatory environment the project commissions into.: the controversy is not only “how big,” but whether the **integrated package**—reservoir, tunnels, communities, finance—can survive the decades-long path to operation.
