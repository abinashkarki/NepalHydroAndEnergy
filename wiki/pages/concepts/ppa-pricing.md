# PPA Pricing

How Power Purchase Agreement rates are set for hydropower projects in Nepal — and whether rate-setting follows an economic formula or a discretionary process. The central institutional framework is [[nea-triple-authority]]: the same entity that sets PPA rates (NEA as single buyer) is also the penalty enforcer and dispatcher.

## PPA Rate Comparison Table

Nepal's Power Purchase Agreement rates are set project-by-project through bilateral negotiation between the developer and NEA, without a publicly disclosed cost-reflective formula prior to 2026. Trishuli 3A is frequently confused with Trishuli Jal Vidhyut Company Limited (37 MW, the 3B cascade), which IS a subsidiary entity with a standard commercial PPA at NPR 4.80/8.40. **NOTE 2 — Sanima Mai promotional tariff expiry:** The Mai HPP (22 MW) initially operated under a government promotional tariff of NPR 4.80 wet / 8.40 dry. This expired mid-April 2022 after 7 years from COD. The current active rate of NPR 5.08/8.89 reflects accumulated escalations post-expiry. The promotional tariff mechanism — and its expiry — is itself a structural feature of Nepal's PPA framework, not an anomaly.

**NOTE 3 — Sahas Urja reframing:** Sahas Urja operates on the identical base rate as Nyadi and Rasuwagadhi (NPR 4.80/8.40). Its superior financial performance (NPR 902M net profit on NPR 2.648B revenue, FY2023/24) is entirely attributable to cost efficiency (NPR 172–175M/MW build cost vs industry average NPR 200–300M/MW) and scale (86 MW generating large absolute dry-season cash flows despite a ~19% dry energy mix). Sahas Urja does not demonstrate that the standard IPP PPA rate is viable — it demonstrates that the rate is only viable at exceptional scale and cost discipline unavailable to most developers. See [[sahas-urja]].

**NOTE 4 — Upper Chameliya seasonal modality:** Unlike the standard 8-month wet / 4-month dry split, Api Power's Upper Chameliya PPA uses a 6-month wet / 6-month dry modality, with the dry-season rate mathematically double the wet-season rate. This significantly bolsters the blended tariff average and enhances cash flow for commercial debt service. Source: CARE Ratings Nepal. ## NEA Internal Generation Cost vs Commercial PPA Rates

Directly NEA-owned generation assets do not operate under commercial PPAs. They use internal transfer pricing based on operational cost, royalty, and allocated depreciation — accounting figures that bear no relation to the full capital cost of the asset. These internal generation costs are structurally incomparable to commercial PPA rates and should not appear in rate comparison tables alongside IPP or subsidiary PPAs.

The table below shows FY2080/81 internal generation costs for five major NEA-owned assets:

| NEA Direct Asset | Capacity (MW) | FY2080/81 Generation (MWh) | Total Cost (Million NPR) | Generation Cost (NPR/Unit) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Kaligandaki 'A' | 144 | 841,596 | 1,805.87 | 2.15 |
| Middle Marsyangdi | 70 | 441,127 | 1,684.58 | 3.82 |
| Upper Trishuli 3A | 60 | 427,055 | 538.62 | 1.26 |
| Chameliya | 30 | 133,968 | 406.66 | 3.04 |
| Trishuli | 24 | 117,042 | 316.41 | 2.70 |

Source: NEA Generation Directorate Report 2080/2081 (16th Issue, 2024).

**Why these figures cannot be compared to commercial PPA rates:** NEA internal generation costs reflect post-subsidy accounting where sovereign capital failures are absorbed off-balance sheet. The state utility tolerates catastrophic inefficiency in its own construction — Chameliya cost $7.5M/MW vs the global benchmark of $1.5–2.5M/MW — without letting those financial failures reflect in the energy pricing metric. Simultaneously, the NEA enforces rigid PPA caps on private developers who must fully amortize commercial debt at double-digit interest rates. A private IPP that suffered Chameliya's $226M cost overrun on a 30 MW asset would be insolvent at any standard PPA rate. The NEA's own asset registers that same energy at NPR 3.04/kWh. This asymmetry is the quantitative core of the [[nea-triple-authority]] governance problem. See [[chameliya-hydropower]].

## Rate-Setting Mechanism and Regulatory History

The Electricity Regulatory Commission (ERC) now vests with rate-setting authority. The 2026 Directive on reservoir-based PPAs transitions large projects (>100 MW) to a transparent cost-plus determination model — factoring in loan interest, depreciation, foreign exchange risks, and hedging costs. Sub-100 MW reservoir projects are capped at NPR 14.80/kWh (winter) and NPR 8.45/kWh (monsoon). Legacy contracts like Chilime's 1997 PPA remain grandfathered into the system, perpetually skewing the competitive balance. ## Royalty and Taxation Schedule

Years 1–15: NPR 100/kW capacity royalty + 2% of average tariff as energy royalty. Years 16+: NPR 1,000/kW capacity royalty + 10% of tariff. Corporate income tax is 20% with a 100% rebate for the first 10 years of operation and 50% rebate in years 11–15. Free-power provisions (typically 10–22% of generation to the government) are negotiated project-by-project in concession PPAs rather than codified in statute. At end of license (30–35 years), the plant transfers to the state. [Under research — needs Electricity Act section citation and free-power provision statutory basis.]

## NEA Subsidiary vs Private IPP Differential Analysis

The PPA rate comparison table reveals a structural divergence in how NEA treats its own subsidiaries versus private IPPs. This differential is not executed through a published discriminatory rate card — the Electricity Act theoretically mandates uniform treatment — but through bespoke contract structuring that allows subsidiaries to bypass seasonal pricing constraints.

### The mathematical proof

Take the two clearest comparable cases:

**NEA subsidiary — Chilime Hydropower (22.1 MW):** Chilime's 1997 PPA discards the wet/dry seasonal constraint entirely. Instead, it uses a "Regular" and "Excess" quota system. In FY2078/79, Chilime generated 156.06 GWh — and **85.4%** of it (133.24 GWh) was billed at the premium NPR 8.17/kWh Regular rate year-round, regardless of season. Only 22.82 GWh was classified as Excess at NPR 4.08/kWh. Total revenue: NPR 1.18 billion. **Blended rate: NPR 7.57/kWh.**

**Private IPP baseline (Nyadi/Sahas Urja model):** A standard private RoR IPP is locked into NPR 4.80/kWh (wet) and NPR 8.40/kWh (dry). Assuming an optimized 20% dry energy yield — generous for most Nepalese rivers — the blended rate is: (0.20 × 8.40) + (0.80 × 4.80) = **NPR 5.52/kWh.**

**The differential: NPR 2.05/kWh = 37.1% revenue advantage per unit for the NEA subsidiary.**

For a 50 MW comparable private IPP generating 200 GWh/year, this represents approximately **NPR 410 million in foregone annual revenue** relative to an identically-sized project that received the Chilime billing architecture.

### The billing architecture is the mechanism, not the rate

Chilime's per-unit rates (8.17/4.08) are not dramatically higher than the private IPP cap (8.40/4.80). The advantage comes from **classification escape**: Chilime's PPA categorizes output by "Regular" vs "Excess" rather than by season, allowing the vast majority of its generation to bill at the premium rate continuously. A private IPP cannot reclassify its monsoon output this way — the season determines the rate.

### Independent academic verification

The Fenner School of Environment and Society (Australian National University), *Nepal State of Knowledge Report — Renewables and PSH*, independently documents this dynamic:

> "The major complaint of the rising IPPs is that the playing field is not level, that NEA as generator, transmitter and distributor gives preferential terms to its own projects compared to IPPs; and indeed, this is seen in the case of the Chilime hydropower company that is owned by the NEA and its staff."

Citation: Fenner School of Environment and Society, ANU, *Nepal State of Knowledge Report — Renewables and PSH*, p. 20. URL: https://fennerschool.anu.edu.au/files/Nepal%20State%20of%20Knowledge%20Report%20-%20Renewables%20%26%20PSH.pdf.

### Legacy contract grandfathering

Chilime's 1997 PPA remains fully active and has not been downgraded to match contemporary IPP standards. The ERC now vests with rate-setting authority and the 2026 Directive on reservoir-based PPAs transitions large projects to cost-plus determination, but legacy contracts like Chilime's are grandfathered — perpetually skewing the market's competitive balance.
