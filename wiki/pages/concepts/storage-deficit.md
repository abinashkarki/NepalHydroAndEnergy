# Storage Deficit

Nepal’s energy crisis is often narrated as a shortage of projects. At the water–energy interface, the sharper truth is often a shortage of **seasonal inventory**: **storage deficit**—the gap between the impoundment capacity needed to re-time monsoon water to dry-season scarcity and what actually exists on the ground.

## Orders of magnitude

International comparisons are sobering: Nepal is frequently placed around **~5 m³ per person** of storage, versus rough reference magnitudes such as **India ~180**, **China ~664**, **Norway ~15,100** (see [[data-storage-comparison]] for the project table and caveats). Nepal's operational hydropower storage is essentially **[[kulekhani-cascade]]** — on the order of **106 MW** with original storage volume of **85.3 MCM** (JICA Storage Master Plan, 2014). However, cumulative sediment deposition reached **25.3 MCM by 2010** — a **~30% capacity loss** over 28 years. At a linearised ~1.1% annual loss rate, Kulekhani's effective storage would be under **50 MCM** by the mid-2020s — a quiet erosion of Nepal's only reservoir inventory that planning documents do not reflect. The "106 MW of storage" claim therefore overstates the system's actual seasonal-shifting capability.

World Bank water diagnostics have framed multi-decadal storage needs at extraordinary volumes—on the order of **29.86 km³** in one national diagnostic framing—while noting that **effective seasonal storage for that purpose is effectively absent**. Whether one takes that headline literally or as an order-of-magnitude provocation, the ratio implies something like a **~200×** mismatch between aspirational storage need and operational reality: the exact factor matters less than the **sign**—Nepal is storage-poor in both water security and power-firming terms.

## Solar changes the storage job

[[solar-role-in-winter-deficit]] adds an important correction: not every dry-season kWh must come from reservoir water. Utility solar can supply a meaningful share of winter **daytime** energy faster and cheaper than new storage hydro. The storage deficit therefore splits into two problems:

- **Seasonal inventory** — water storage that shifts monsoon energy into dry months.
- **Daily shifting** — reservoir peaking, BESS, and demand response that move mid-day solar into the winter evening peak.

This makes storage no less important, but it makes a hydro-only storage story too narrow. Nepal still needs reservoirs where they are buildable; it also needs BESS and dispatch design so winter solar becomes firm enough to matter after sunset.

## Topography as paradox

Nepal’s steep gradients are good for **head** and therefore for RoR hydropower, but narrow **V-shaped valleys** work against **large gentle reservoirs** at acceptable inundation. The contrast with classic big-basin storage (e.g., Aswan-scale volumes from modest dam heights) is instructive: Nepal can get “big numbers” on paper from river energy, yet struggle to find **low-loss, low-conflict** reservoir geometries—see discussions around [[budhigandaki]] and [[dudhkoshi-storage]].

## Why storage is politically hard even when physically “obvious”

Seasonal mismatch ([[seasonal-mismatch]]) makes storage the clearest physical answer for evening and multi-week scarcity, but projects collide with **sedimentation**, **seismic hazard**, **resettlement**, **financing**, and **treaty-dependent** downstream politics. That is why [[buildability]] matters as much as MW: a reservoir is not a line on a map until the whole engineering–social–finance package survives scrutiny.
