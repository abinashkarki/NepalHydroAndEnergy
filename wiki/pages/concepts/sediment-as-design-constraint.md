# Sediment as Design Constraint

In Nepal, sediment is often discussed as an operations headache — rakers, flushes, lost efficiency. That understates the issue. **Sediment is a core design constraint**: it determines what kind of plant you can build, what turbine you can credibly run, how fast maintenance capital is consumed, and whether a reservoir's economic life matches its financial model.

## Orders-of-magnitude swings are normal, not exceptional

Case evidence from Himalayan plants shows concentration swings that are almost incomprehensible outside high mountain rivers. At Kali Gandaki A style plants: **dry-season** suspended sediment on the order of **~20 ppm** climbs to **monsoon** peaks on the order of **~50,000 ppm** — not a gentle seasonal tilt but a **2,500x regime change** in fluid abrasiveness. At that scale, **tens of millions of tonnes per year** pass the dam axis while **millions of tonnes per year** move through turbines — turning "a little silt" into a **metal removal process** measured in millimeters per season.

## Kali Gandaki A: the field test that tells the real story

A thermodynamic field test of three Francis units at Kali Gandaki A (Nepal's largest NEA-owned RoR plant) conducted in accordance with IEC 60041 provides the only direct Nepal plant-efficiency measurement in the entire research corpus:

- **Best-point efficiency: ~90–92%** depending on unit condition and time since overhaul
- **The oldest unit** (two years since last overhaul) had **materially lower best-point efficiency** than the recently refurbished unit
- The study explicitly traces the degradation to **erosion of guide vane and facing plate geometry** and **leakage growth through labyrinth seals** — i.e., abrasion physically converts a precision machine into an increasingly leaky one between overhauls
- Hard coatings including **HVOF tungsten carbide** are being applied, but wear on runners, guide vanes, facing plates, and labyrinth rings reoccurs after each monsoon

This is "efficiency left on the table" in the most literal sense: the gap between a freshly refurbished unit and a two-year-worn unit, multiplied across a fleet of dozens of plants, represents recoverable generation that does not require new MW — just sediment discipline.

## Francis clearance gaps as a parable of wrong-kind-of-precision

A commonly documented maintenance case: a **Francis** runner designed with **~0.6 mm** clearance erodes to **~1.35 mm in one monsoon year** — more than doubling, with cascading effects on efficiency, cavitation margins, and vibration signatures. Mitigations — **HVOF coatings**, **NDT campaigns**, shortened overhaul intervals — are not "nice to have"; they are **capitalized lifecycle costs** that belong in LCOE and investor due diligence from day one.

For high-head impulse machines: a Pelton installation (22 MW) case study documents sand erosion of nozzles and buckets after a single monsoon season — consistent with what is seen globally in sediment-laden high-head systems.

## The Khimti benchmark: what best practice looks like

The 60 MW Khimti RoR plant in Dolakha is documented (by a sediment engineering practitioner) as having **exceptionally comprehensive sediment removal** for a run-of-river plant:

- **Stone trap** → **Gravel trap** → **Large desanders** (multi-chamber) → **Additional tunnel sediment traps**
- Sized beyond standard 0.2 mm criteria to remove nearly all particles **above ~0.13 mm**
- The multi-stage cascade approach — coarse removal first, then progressively finer — is the engineering standard Nepal should be applying consistently but is not

Khimti demonstrates Nepal is **capable** of best practice. The problem is that best practice is **not consistently embedded** across the buildout, especially when intake land constraints and cost pressure force compromises.

## Forced outages: sediment's operational footprint

A Nepal engineering study analyzing five years of operational data for four utility-owned PRoR plants identifies the distribution of forced outages by cause. **80% of forced outages** were driven by turbine, generator, and excitation system failures — exactly the components most exposed to sediment abrasion. Availability figures look high on paper (~99% range), but the recurring failure modes point to a fleet operating under chronic stress from its operating environment.

## Design and technology selection implications

Sediment pushes engineering toward:

- **Robust turbine families** — Pelton preference where head allows (no runner blades in the suspended sediment path during normal operation, though nozzle erosion remains)
- **Multi-stage sediment removal** at intake (stone trap → gravel trap → desander → tunnel traps) with **flushing discipline** rather than just settling
- **Sediment-aware operations** — threshold-based shutdown during extreme sediment events (Bhutan's approach: switch-off intake above a turbidity threshold to protect runners)
- **Real-time sediment monitoring** instruments as operational infrastructure, not just research tools — enables decisions rather than only post-damage audits
- **Shorter payback horizons** and conservative debt sizing — because the river, not the spreadsheet, sets wear rates
- **Reservoir sediment bypass tunnels** (Japan and Switzerland model) for future storage projects — routing sediment-laden flood flows around the reservoir and turbines during peak monsoon

Basin context matters: sediment regimes differ systematically across [[gandaki-basin]] and [[koshi-basin]] corridors; climate-driven landslide connectivity can tighten constraints over time. Buildability ([[buildability]]) is partly "can you keep the runner in spec?" Sediment-linked hazards also overlap with [[glof-risk]] as part of the same mountain hazard stack.

## The technology roadmap implication

Nepal should treat **abrasion control as an efficiency program, not merely a maintenance program**. The field data show measurable efficiency degradation over time and link it to leakage and erosion — meaning abrasion control is "recoverable generation" expressed in NPR/year per plant, not just O&M cost lines. At scale across 189 plants, systematic sediment-fit upgrading could recover generation that exceeds what several new RoR plants would add.
